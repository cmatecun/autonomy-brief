On last Thursday’s episode of Sharp Tech ( https://sharptech.fm/member/episode/five-questions-on-wwdc-2026-fable-5-and-its-guardrails-what-anthropic-has-in-common-with-apple ) , Andrew and I discussed WWDC and Fable 5.

On to the Update:

Fox Buys Roku
-------------

From the Wall Street Journal ( https://www.wsj.com/business/deals/fox-roku-deal-f6e564f9 ) :

Fox Corp. said it is acquiring Roku in a deal valued at around $25 billion, making a major bet on the future of ad-supported streaming. The deal — Fox’s largest to date—brings together a media company known for its live news and sports programming with the biggest provider of streaming platforms for connected TVs. It will add scale to Fox’s streaming business, currently home to free, ad-supported streaming service Tubi, which the company bought for $400 million in 2020, and subscription-based Fox One and Fox Nation. In addition to distributing other streaming services through connected TVs and devices, Roku has its own ad-supported Roku Channel. The combined company will better compete with the likes of Amazon.com and Netflix for ad dollars…

Fox will pay around $160 per Roku share, with $96 in cash and 0.9693 Fox Class A shares. The deal is valued at around $22 billion on an enterprise basis. Fox expects to fund the cash portion of the transaction with $12 billion in new debt, as well as cash on hand. The combined company expects to cut about $400 million in annual costs. Fox plans to keep Tubi and the Roku Channel as separate offerings, Murdoch said on the call, noting the two services complement each other.

The market was not very happy about this deal; from Bloomberg ( https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-15/fox-to-buy-roku-at-22-billion-value-in-streaming-video-push ) :

Roku shares jumped 20% on Friday after Bloomberg reported the company was in talks about a sale. On Monday, Roku shares slipped 1.9% to close in New York at $140.90. Fox shares fell 17%, a record, to $54.76. The sharp decline in Fox shares means the stock component of the agreement would be worth less if it holds through the closing of the deal. Based on where Fox traded Monday, the ratio of stock and cash the company would pay Roku shareholders was roughly $149 per share, about 5.8% above where Roku stock ended trading.

I have to admit I was pretty surprised by the market’s reaction; my immediate reaction to the deal was very positive. However, after looking more into the deal…I’m still very positive, but I get the skepticism.

The Problem With Fox’s Smart Strategy
-------------------------------------

The first reason I was optimistic about this deal was because of recent history. Specifically, I hold Fox’s business acumen in high regard, as I feel the company — along with Sony — has handled the shift to streaming better than anyone. The company set its course back in 2017 when it sold 21st Century Fox, its studio business, to Disney; I wrote in an Update at the time ( https://stratechery.com/2017/disney-to-buy-21st-century-fox-two-strategies-for-content-companies-netflix-and-espn/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDE3L2Rpc25leS10by1idXktMjFzdC1jZW50dXJ5LWZveC10d28tc3RyYXRlZ2llcy1mb3ItY29udGVudC1jb21wYW5pZXMtbmV0ZmxpeC1hbmQtZXNwbi8iXX0sImV4cCI6MTc4NDE5NjE1NSwiaWF0IjoxNzgxNjA0MTU1LCJpc3MiOiJodHRwczovL2FwcC5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUvb2F1dGgiLCJzY29wZSI6ImZlZWQ6cmVhZCBhcnRpY2xlOnJlYWQgYXNzZXQ6cmVhZCBjYXRlZ29yeTpyZWFkIGVudGl0bGVtZW50cyIsInN1YiI6IjAxOTUxNmYxLWEzNDYtNzYxNC05ZTk2LWI2M2JkMTFmZTg1NiIsInVzZSI6ImFjY2VzcyJ9.gNpp-FEm8OY7PreEb5e9oGE-nymSvyvuYVP5npOEPhxDHgR9AJvuR-oZTCJYUlSFdSJUkWVfurfstDE38dZ2rx5ET_92Kfw1m1Sbv91TpFyy8_nXWgeKhbGeXIk2-Kez7fmWG_UBunBNLIVdciUy3pmsA2THrTO2mAPB2k1rglw3OrjRrdiah5xkfjpq6N5txGHTR5FCqSoZNAMWe8Gl-6WNxhTbQYOgQG5H8DTVAMh582WxFRJWt7MEwv2T6B0opvI-SSSDoOkxC94YPDLimxJqTEiltWWvug1tUId8jBoY6EH7YKyMZfFBBG4nchB-MTygnbxiA8TTynOzvh6pRQ ) :

In that last sentence I actually put forth two distinct strategies: selling direct to consumers, and charging distributors significantly more. Both do depend on having differentiated content, which was the point of that Daily Update, but the similarities end there. To explain what I mean, this deal actually offers two great examples: if it goes through, that means Fox is pursuing the second strategy, and Disney the first.

Start with Fox: its news and sports divisions — particularly the former — are highly differentiated. That gives Fox significant pricing power with shrinking-but-still-very-large TV distributors. Moreover, given that both news and sports are heavily biased towards live viewing, they are also a good fit for advertising, which again, matches up with traditional TV distribution. What Fox would accomplish with this deal, then, is shedding a huge amount of that detritus I mentioned earlier: sure, more was better when there was only one distributor, but now that there is competition for viewer’s attention, filler is a drag on the content that actually gives negotiating leverage. Fox could come out of this deal with the same pricing power it has today but a vastly streamlined corporate structure and cost basis.

Disney, in contrast, decided to double down on content in order to build its own streaming service; and it hasn’t gone so well. On March 20, 2019, when the deal closed, Disney’s stock opened at $106.47; Fox’s newly listed stock opened at $35.89. Last Friday, meanwhile, Disney’s stock closed at $100.04; Fox’s closed at $58.92.

Even with Monday’s market reaction, Fox’s Class B stock is still at $49.95, a 32.86% increase. No, that’s not the most impressive growth in the world, but it is growth, and it’s a lot better than Disney.

News remains a great business for Fox, but where the company has really doubled down has been on sports rights. Back in 2022 ( https://stratechery.com/2022/big-ten-blame/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDIyL2JpZy10ZW4tYmxhbWUvIl19LCJleHAiOjE3ODQxOTYxNTUsImlhdCI6MTc4MTYwNDE1NSwiaXNzIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9hcHAucGFzc3BvcnQub25saW5lL29hdXRoIiwic2NvcGUiOiJmZWVkOnJlYWQgYXJ0aWNsZTpyZWFkIGFzc2V0OnJlYWQgY2F0ZWdvcnk6cmVhZCBlbnRpdGxlbWVudHMiLCJzdWIiOiIwMTk1MTZmMS1hMzQ2LTc2MTQtOWU5Ni1iNjNiZDExZmU4NTYiLCJ1c2UiOiJhY2Nlc3MifQ.NleQWY5-MXaVarYKU2q5IPqOCPlISvVZarD2qx8BISLaKOQszNPhMYhXivd-ZErM3oO2kAgpVzTnclUtQ9xEELlIpzmcG8eF4ZozY8c8_7D_XGRJmwZt9xZIyOFJHWCITUAHyfjn-M31o9NUBF6oVo9O_1jDCnvesMkgQb9Tilo7ouQtl-9jfhLrrNP9g4QN2AP2TWGYt8wC3kUcQVEPwSdDoEBN3NyDawUwK2PjSNIzakKg4xMxmE7IOx9Chf_iaqVdlfTVuY1yF8Nz-uozuaFrRv9XO70AcmWxyy59Wj0JTd9JbbKkYAjfGdiR7FStpaTiW0NhRcMzPZnvr-_2yQ ) , when the Big Ten expanded to the West Coast, I praised Fox’s decision to not only not launch a streaming business, but also exit original content completely; I also noted that its focus on the bundle left it at the mercy of rights owners determined to extract ever more value from their fans:

The logic of the Big Ten’s expansion into California is obvious: the more of an audience that the Big Ten can command, the more of the money flowing through that value chain it can extract. Sure, it’s not quite to the level of the NFL, but it’s the next closest thing. This is also the downside to Fox’s bet on live: while the company owns the content it produces on channels like Fox News, it has to buy sports rights, and it is the Big Ten that is determined to take its share, even if that means an expansion that otherwise makes no sense at all.

In other words, I think the tweet ( https://x.com/wilnerhotline/status/1543218179601625088 ) above has it backwards: Fox and ESPN are not “grandmasters calling the shots behind the scenes”; they are essential but ultimately replaceable parts in the movement of money from consumers to the entities that provide the content those consumers want.

Still, the tweet is instructive: perhaps the most essential role Fox and ESPN play for universities is taking the blame as the latter make more money than ever.

This is the biggest problem with the demise of the bundle. Once Fox was reduced to news and live sports, it was increasingly just a conduit for transferring money from fans to sports leagues, because the latter had wholesale transfer pricing power ( https://25iq.com/2013/06/12/wholesale-transfer-pricing-and-the-free-parking-business-model/ ) , given Fox wasn’t really selling distributors anything else other than Fox News (which, to be fair, achieves very high affiliate fees in its own right). This did not, to be clear, mean that spinning off 21st Century Fox was wrong; it did, however, mean that Fox was both dependent on an ecosystem in secular decline and had decreased bargaining power within that ecosystem (especially as cable news viewers, one of the oldest demographics there is, started to die off).

Streaming That Works
--------------------

There is one other important part of recent Fox history that made me enthusiastic about this deal. Fox may have decided to not even try and compete in the paid streaming game, but that doesn’t mean it wasn’t a streamer. In March 2020, Fox acquired Tubi ( https://www.foxcorporation.com/news/corp-press-releases/2020/fox-corporation-to-acquire-tubi/ ) , a Free Ad-Supported Streaming Television (FAST) service.

FAST services are free and full of ads, and they look a lot like the cable television channels of my youth: lots of syndicated TV shows (including ones that first appeared on streamers), movies, and the occasional low-budget original series. They are also one of the fastest-growing TV categories, collecting a combined $3.5 billion in revenue in 2025; Tubi grew viewership by 16% and revenue by 27% in 2025, to $1.1 billion.

Roku, meanwhile, has a FAST service of its own called the Roku Channel, as well as advertising throughout its TV OS. In April, Roku finally broke out its advertising revenue for the first time ( https://www.sec.gov/Archives/edgar/data/1428439/000162828026024842/wk-20260413.htm ) — almost certainly because they were looking for a buyer! — and it’s a healthy business: $2.3 billion in 2025, a 13% increase.

Now, not everything is perfect in the FAST world: Paramount’s Pluto TV declined 14% in revenue, and the segment as a whole saw its growth slow from 17% in 2024 to 10% in 2025 as Amazon and Netflix in particular grew their advertising businesses. That, though, makes this acquisition all the more compelling. First, Fox is taking a dominant position in a space that is still growing, and second, Fox is increasing its bargaining power for those old shows and movies that struggling studios do still want to make money from, right as Paramount is taking on a massive amount of debt to acquire Warner Bros., and will likely be incentivized to use Pluto as an outlet for its own content. Fox is well-positioned to earn more money from advertising thanks to its increased scale, even as it reduces its costs to operate the business, and acquires content for better prices than it might have otherwise.

This also has the potential to benefit Fox’s core business as well. First, increasing profits from FAST will give Fox more capacity to absorb sports rights increases. Second, Fox can explore putting games on its FAST channels, increasing customer awareness and downloads. Third, Fox can go to advertisers with a scaled offering that extends across all of its properties, including Fox One, the streaming service for its linear TV offerings.

Roku also makes money in other ways, most notably through being a storefront that sells subscriptions to other streaming services. This also means that Roku has a better handle on who its viewers are, which could help with targeting. At the same time, Fox will need to take care to not alienate those partners, or get distracted by driving people to Roku; the value here is in the scale that comes from combining multiple advertising-supported streaming services, not in owning a vertical device business (and, it’s worth noting, the device business isn’t exactly high margin).

So why did the market hate this deal? There is the debt, which is always risky for a company predicated on a sector in secular decline; we already saw how that drowned Warner Bros. Discovery. There’s also the general mistrust of media companies, particularly when it comes to acquisitions. And most pertinently, while the premium Fox paid for Roku was relatively small compared to Roku’s current stock price, it’s much larger compared to Roku’s price a year ago, and it’s massive compared to Roku’s actual profits — about 52x Roku EBITDA. Fox’s investors thought they owned a focused and eminently understandable company with knowable risk and strong cash flows; now, thanks to dilution from new stock issuance, they own much less of a much more complicated business with real execution risk and a big debt load.

Ultimately, however, I like the deal. Not only is Fox a company that deserves the benefit of the doubt, but also advertising is a business that needs scale: Fox is getting exactly that, pointing towards a future where the current rights-renting business that bleeds leverage to rights holders funds the build-out of a new rights-renting business — FAST — that builds leverage for the renter in a way that only advertising can. Advertisers should be buyers; it’s the subscription models that struggle with scale.
