# WeRide Hits Madrid, Tesla's Map Grows but Its Fleet Doesn't, NVIDIA Wants to Be the Robotaxi Standard

👋 Welcome back everyone,
Two companies dominated the newsflow this week, and not separately. NVIDIA and Uber moved together. NVIDIA used GTC Taipei to position DRIVE Hyperion as the common platform for the whole robotaxi industry, and Uber showed up in almost every announcement as the demand layer on top. Add a quantified Nuro investment, a new European city, and a fresh set of Tesla contradictions, and there is a lot to work through.
Let’s dive in!

The Rocsys M1 – Hands-free Charging Solutions for Autonomous Fleets
For autonomous vehicles, manual charging is more than an inconvenience – it’s a bottleneck that keeps vehicle stuck at a depot instead of on the road.
The Rocsys M1 is the first multibay solution designed specifically for robotaxi fleets. Its rail-mounted design allows the system to serve up to ten vehicle bays, picking up the connector, plugging it into an EV, and moving on to the next vehicle. Once charged, the M1 unplugs the connecter from the vehicle – all without manual intervention.
This allows depot staff to focus on higher priority tasks, allowing EVs to charge and return to service sooner, enabling fleets to realize their full operational potential.
NVIDIA WANTS TO OWN THE ROBOTAXI PLATFORM LAYER
At GTC Taipei, NVIDIA released Alpamayo 2 Super, a 32-billion-parameter reasoning vision-language-action (VLA) model for Level 4 development. The headline number is the parameter count, up 3x from the 10-billion-parameter Alpamayo 1 generation. 
Alongside the model, NVIDIA announced new tools, models and agent skills that complete the pipeline from real-world data capture to closed-loop training and in-vehicle deployment, including NVIDIA AlpaGym, NVIDIA OmniDreams and new NVIDIA Omniverse NuRec models.
Alpamayo 2 Super helps accelerate AV development by eliminating the need to build key autonomy infrastructure from scratch. It enables humanlike perception, reasoning and action, and provides the interpretability needed for safety validation and regulatory collaboration.
DRIVE Hyperion becomes the platform pitch. NVIDIA’s framing is that DRIVE Hyperion is now the “global platform for a robotaxi-ready world.” It is a Level 4-ready reference design: DRIVE AGX compute, the Halos safety system, a certified DriveOS base, a defined sensor suite, and the DRIVE AV software. The strategy is to make Hyperion the default foundation so that any automaker or AV developer can build a robotaxi without assembling the base layer themselves. To make that real, NVIDIA rolled out four deployment partners in one day.
Foxconn, Taiwan. Foxconn is expanding its NVIDIA collaboration to build and deploy Level 4 robotaxi fleets in Taiwan, starting in Kaohsiung and scaling across Asia. The plan targets a 2028 service launch, beginning with airport-to-city routes and later extending along Taiwan’s high-speed rail corridor. Kaohsiung’s mayor appeared at GTC to back it, and the city already has an NVIDIA-built digital twin that can pre-train the driving systems before deployment. Foxconn brings the contract manufacturing. NVIDIA brings the platform. This is the same build-it-for-everyone logic that made Foxconn the assembler of the smartphone era, now pointed at robotaxis.
HUMAIN, Saudi Arabia. HUMAIN is working to bring Hyperion-powered robotaxis to Saudi Arabia. Thin on specifics so far, but it extends the platform into the Middle East and fits the pattern we have tracked of Gulf states buying their way to the front of the AV adoption line.
VinFast, Southeast Asia. VinFast is building a Level 4 program for Southeast Asia on DRIVE Hyperion 10, using Autobrains’ agentic AI software. The pitch is explicitly about cost. VinFast’s deputy ADAS CEO framed it as autonomy “without the premium cost structure that has limited wider deployment,” aimed at the dense, chaotic traffic of the region. This is the first of two Autobrains deals this week, and it matters for reading the second one.
Uber and Autobrains, Munich. Uber and Autobrains announced a robotaxi program in Munich built on DRIVE Hyperion, pending regulatory approval. Autobrains is a Tel Aviv company with more than 300 patents and a different technical approach than most of the field. Instead of one monolithic end-to-end model trying to solve every situation, its agentic AI decomposes driving into specialized agents that activate only when a given context demands them. The claim is that this runs on standard automotive sensors and lower compute, which keeps cost down and makes the stack portable across vehicle platforms.
That portability is the strategic core. Uber, Autobrains and NVIDIA are pitching an OEM-agnostic model: the driving software is designed to run across many carmakers, not one bespoke fleet. Read it from each party’s seat and the alignment is clear. For Uber, OEM-agnostic means more potential vehicle supply. For NVIDIA, it means broader adoption of Hyperion as the common compute base. For automakers, it offers a path into autonomous ride-hailing without having to build the full stack, the marketplace and the fleet operations themselves.
This fits Uber’s whole autonomy strategy, and the logic is worth stating plainly. Uber does not need one dominant AV winner. It benefits if there are many. The more the AV supply side fragments across providers, OEMs and vehicle platforms, the more valuable Uber becomes as the aggregation layer, and the stronger its negotiating position. More AV players also means more compute demand for NVIDIA. Both companies are economically aligned in enabling more participants, not fewer. 
The announcement named no vehicle and no automaker, only that the OEM would be revealed later this year, so anyone naming one is speculating. Two readings are plausible. The first points to Stellantis, which committed back in October to an L4 robotaxi collaboration with the exact same partners NVIDIA and Uber. That existing public commitment is the hardest evidence on the table. The analysis from the Autonomy Signals team leans this way, and adds that the German OEMs are high-friction software partners with slow proprietary cycles, while Mobileye is already embedded across the VW Group, leaving little room for a rival stack from the same corner of Israel.
The second reading points to BMW, and it is stronger than it first looks. Munich is BMW’s home market. BMW i Ventures has been an investor in Autobrains. And BMW shut down its own L3 program, so it has no in-house L4 path. Here the cost angle actually cuts in BMW’s favor, not against it. Autobrains’ whole pitch is that its agentic stack runs on standard sensors and cheaper compute, and a carmaker that just decided building autonomy in-house was not worth the cost is exactly the kind of buyer that would adopt a cheaper, OEM-agnostic stack rather than rebuild its own. Buy, not build.
The skepticism that remains is narrower. The “cheaper” claim is still Autobrains’ own marketing, unproven at commercial robotaxi scale. 
On the choice of city, Munich is close to the least forgiving place in Europe to prove a new driving stack. Dense streets, fast road networks, and a serious German regulatory framework. That is likely the point. If you want to sell an OEM-agnostic system to German automakers, you prove it on their home turf. There is also a clustering story here. Uber already runs Momenta L4 tests in Munich. Nuro opened a Munich-area European hub, which we covered in CW20 alongside the open question of whether its German partner would be Lucid or BMW. Now Autobrains and NVIDIA enter the same ecosystem. That does not make Munich the next San Francisco overnight. It does suggest Germany is becoming one of Europe’s more serious AV testbeds.
UBER WASN’T DONE: MADRID, A DATA FLEET, AND DETAILS ON THE NURO BET
WeRide and Uber announced Spain’s first commercial robotaxi pilot in Madrid.   Madrid becomes the 12th city globally for WeRide’s robotaxi business and the first joint Uber-WeRide entry into Europe.
Source: WeRide
The service is backed by the regional government of Madrid and slated to launch within the year. Riders hail a WeRide robotaxi through the Uber app. Vehicles start with trained safety operators, and as milestones are met, WeRide, AVOMO and Uber plan to scale to hundreds of robotaxis and introduce fully driverless service in the core city. WeRide supplies the autonomy. AVOMO, a Moove Cars Group company that already runs Uber fleet operations in Atlanta and Austin, manages daily operations. This is the Abu Dhabi and Dubai playbook exported to Europe, where Uber and WeRide already run fully driverless commercial service. Recall the May 2025 commitment to 15 new international cities over five years. With Madrid, they are at four, with 11 to go by 2030.
The bigger read this week is about WeRide itself. WeRide is one of the few robotaxi operators making fast, tangible global progress right now, and it is taking the international expansion seriously, adding cities at a steady clip while most rivals are still proving out a single market. WeRide’s structural advantage over Waymo is cost. Waymo is ahead on vehicle count, but WeRide has the edge on sourcing cheaper vehicle platforms and on a manufacturing process that lowers the all-in price per car. Waymo’s own fix for its cost problem, the Chinese-built Zeekr, makes the same point from the other direction. As deployment shifts from who has the most cars toward who can put cars on the road cheaply and in volume, that cost-and-manufacturing edge is the threat quietly building under WeRide’s name. Europe also has no homegrown pure-play robotaxi operator at scale, which is exactly the white space they are moving into.
Uber also unveiled a prototype data-collection vehicle this week, a Hyundai Ioniq 5 loaded with 14 cameras, eight solid-state lidars and nine radars, retrofitted by Roush Performance, with everything routed through NVIDIA’s Dual Drive Thor computer. Uber plans to put 500 of these on roads globally this year, 50 by summer, collecting two million miles per month of high-fidelity data for its AV partners including Avride, Waymo and WeRide. This is the first vehicle Uber has assembled itself since it sold its ATG self-driving unit to Aurora in 2020. The company already has a head start from outward-facing cameras on thousands of partner vehicles and data from hundreds of Lucid Air cars used by its fleet partners over the past two years.
The same fragmentation logic from the Munich deal runs underneath this. Uber’s stated aim is to democratize AV training data. Building the world’s most geographically diverse dataset is expensive, and collecting it at scale is something smaller AV developers may never be able to afford on their own. By gathering it once and sharing it across its partners, Uber helps more of them stay in the race. The more AV providers that survive, the more fragmented the supply side stays, and the more valuable Uber’s aggregation layer becomes. It is the same playbook as the OEM-agnostic robotaxi program, applied one layer down at the data level.
And the last Uber news this week: Reuters reported that Uber’s total commitment to Nuro is close to $500 million, the first time the full scale has been reported. This closes a thread from CW19, where Uber’s Nuro stake was described only as a “multi-hundred-million-dollar” investment. The detail: Uber participated in a $203 million round that valued Nuro at $6 billion, then made an unreported follow-on significantly larger than the first, plus milestone-triggered funding. The first milestones have already been hit, releasing some funds. The rest is tied to testing without a driver later this year, carrying passengers in driverless cars by year-end, and ramping the service next year. 
The same week showed the other side of Uber’s portfolio management. On June 2, Uber sold 67.5 million shares of Aurora Innovation at $7.72 each, cutting its Aurora stake by about 14.46% to roughly 258.5 million shares. Aurora is the company Uber sold its in-house self-driving unit to in 2020, and it sits on the autonomous-trucking side of the field.
TESLA: A BIGGER MAP, A SELF-CERTIFIED LICENSE, AND DISAPPEARING CONTRACTS
Three Tesla stories this week, and read together they describe a company managing perception and liability faster than it is scaling vehicles.
The geofence grew, the fleet did not. Tesla announced its unsupervised Robotaxi service now covers the entire Austin metro area. 
On paper that is a major expansion. In practice, the active unsupervised fleet is roughly 20 vehicles, and it has been shrinking, not growing. The fleet peaked at 25 cumulative vehicles in late April and dropped to 20 active. Dallas and Houston, launched in April with tiny geofences, have seen zero growth at 3 and 6 vehicles.  Expanding a geofence costs nothing and requires no cars. It is the easy metric to move.
Tesla self-certified Level 4 in Texas. A new Texas law lets companies with SAE Level 4 or higher vehicles offer commercial driverless service, and on the day it took effect, Tesla self-certified its robotaxi FSD software as Level 4 compliant. This is a real shift in posture. By certifying L4 for commercial operations, Tesla is legally stating its vehicles can handle the entire driving task and the fallback within a defined operational design domain, with no human required, which means it is absorbing a meaningful share of operational liability for the first time. Two caveats keep it grounded. The certification is self-issued, not granted by an independent regulator. And it covers only the robotaxi vehicles. Consumer cars running similar FSD software remain Level 2, with the driver fully responsible. The robotaxi vehicles also get geofencing, extra localized FSD training and remote assistance that consumer cars do not.
The most concerning story was reported by Electrek. According to the report, Tesla has retroactively modified “Full Self-Driving” purchase agreements signed between 2016 and early 2024 to add “supervised” language that did not exist when owners bought the product. In some cases the original documents are now inaccessible, linking to invalid pages. Electrek confirmed it with multiple owners, and the pattern is specific: on affected accounts, the only documents that will not open are the ones referencing FSD purchase details. Everything else opens fine. The timing is what makes this serious. Tesla faces up to $14.5 billion in lawsuits spanning FSD false advertising, Autopilot liability and securities fraud, and a certified class action covers FSD statements from exactly October 2016 to August 2024. If the reporting holds, this is a legal problem, not a branding one.
THE BOTTOM LINE
This was NVIDIA’s and Uber’s week, and the two strategies rhyme. NVIDIA is trying to become the layer every robotaxi is built on. Uber is trying to become the layer every robotaxi rides on. Both win by enabling more players rather than backing one, which is why they keep showing up in the same announcements. 
The data fleet is the clearest tell of that strategy: Uber would rather arm many small AV developers with data they could never gather alone than let the market consolidate into two or three winners it would have to negotiate with. The OEM-agnostic model is the same instinct at the vehicle level, and it is genuinely good news for any automaker that missed the autonomy stack, because it offers a way back into the value chain without building everything. 
As a German, I will say plainly that I like seeing the movement in Munich. Germany has the OEMs, the suppliers, the engineering depth and the regulatory foundation to matter in autonomous mobility, and a Munich cluster forming around Autobrains, Momenta and Nuro is the kind of thing Europe needs more of. I am holding caution. The Munich program is an announcement, not a deployment yet, with no named vehicle, no fleet size, and a German approval process that will run longer than this year.
For Tesla, it’s still narrative versus reality. The map is bigger while the fleet shrinks.

CHINA CORNER: STOCK CONNECT, XPENG’S AI BILL, AND MOMENTA’S NEXT CITY
WeRide’s biggest news this week was the Madrid launch with Uber, which I covered above and will not repeat here since it fit the Uber storyline. Three other Chinese developments are worth your time.
WeRide and Pony.ai enter Stock Connect. Both companies were added to the Stock Connect eligible list, effective June 4, about seven months after their Hong Kong debuts on November 6, 2025. This matters more than it sounds. Stock Connect lets mainland Chinese investors buy the companies’ Hong Kong-listed shares directly through the Shanghai and Shenzhen channels. The program has strict entry thresholds on market cap, liquidity and compliance, so inclusion is itself a signal that both names clear those bars. For the companies, the payoff is a wider investor base and deeper liquidity from southbound mainland capital, which can support the share price and lower the cost of future raises. Given how capital-hungry fleet scaling is, access to a new pool of buyers is a structural advantage, not just a sentiment boost.
XPeng is spending like an AI lab, not a carmaker. XPeng’s head of autonomous driving, Dr. Xianming Liu, told Electrek the company spends roughly 300 million RMB (about $41 million) a month on AI training alone. That is close to $500 million a year, from a company that delivered around 200,000 vehicles last year and held RMB 47.66 billion (about $6.5 billion) in cash at the end of 2025. Liu was blunt about how unusual this is: “As a car company, you can never imagine such a big R&D investment because you can never make the money back. But our company is determined to be a Physical AI company.”
The framing is the part to keep. Liu compared XPeng to Google building Pixel phones, where the hardware exists mainly to demonstrate the software and collect data for it. In that view, building cars is the means, not the end, and the real product is the driving intelligence. That reframes the Volkswagen deal. VW became the first external customer to license XPeng’s VLA 2.0 driving system earlier this year, with deployment planned for 2027. Liu played down the engineering lift, noting XPeng already pushes the system across more than 20 of its own models, so adding VW’s is routine. The goal he stated openly is to get the whole industry onto the approach, because no single player gets there alone.
Momenta adds Wuxi. Momenta secured a road-testing and demonstration permit in Wuxi, Jiangsu, where it had already been testing since January. It is a routine permit, but it fits a broader map worth tracking.
The thread connecting these is capital, and how the Chinese players are arming themselves with it. Stock Connect widens the funding base. XPeng’s roughly $500-million-a-year training spend shows the scale of money the frontier now demands, and its licensing model, VW today and more later, is how it tries to earn that back across other companies’ fleets. The 2026 AV race is increasingly a capital race, and the Chinese leaders are systematically widening both the money coming in and the number of vehicles their software runs on.

VOLVO AND BOLIDEN COMPLETE AUTONOMOUS MINING HAUL AT SCALE
Volvo Autonomous Solutions and Boliden finished an autonomous transport project at the Garpenberg mine in Sweden, moving close to 700,000 tonnes of rock fill across more than 11,000 cycles and 56,000 kilometers to reinforce a dam. Delivered as Transport-as-a-Service.
🔗 Boliden [ https://investors.boliden.com/en/press/volvo-autonomous-solutions-and-boliden-complete-dam-construction-milestone-autonomous ]
WAYMO WILL TURN SPENT ROBOTAXI BATTERIES INTO GRID STORAGE
Waymo signed a deal with energy-storage firm B2U to repurpose retired robotaxi batteries, mostly from its Jaguar I-Pace fleet, into grid storage in California and Texas, involving “hundreds of megawatts” of capacity. 
🔗 TechCrunch [ https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/04/waymos-spent-robotaxi-batteries-will-be-used-as-grid-storage/ ]
HARBINGER AND RHEINMETALL TO BUILD AN UNCREWED MILITARY GROUND VEHICLE
Commercial EV truck startup Harbinger is partnering with American Rheinmetall to develop an uncrewed military ground vehicle on Harbinger’s medium-duty hybrid-electric, fully drive-by-wire chassis. Harbinger supplies the platform, Rheinmetall the integration, mission systems and modular autonomous architecture. The pitch is “attritable mass,” autonomy-ready vehicles cheap enough to field in numbers while keeping soldiers out of harm’s way through autonomy and teleoperation. Joint demonstrations start this summer.
