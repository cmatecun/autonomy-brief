5 Great Longreads You Might Have Missed This Weekend
by Brandon Gorrell
Not really a tech / business piece per se, but The New Yorker: The Car Crash Conspiracy [ https://www.newyorker.com/magazine/2026/04/20/the-car-crash-conspiracy ] is a fascinating look into a scam that’s been going on down in Louisiana. There seemed to be a particularly dangerous stretch of Interstate 10 in the New Orleans area, where there were hundreds of collisions with 18-wheelers over the past decade. But when authorities started digging into it, they determined the wrecks were part of an industrial-scale fraud, where drivers would slam into tractor-trailers with their cars filled with people, all of whom would sue the 18-wheeler companies, some going so far as to get unnecessary surgeries to increase the value of their claims. 
The New York Times put some of the data center/ energy prices FUD to bed this morning in a piece called Why Is Your Electric Bill Going Up? Blame the Broken Grid. [ https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2026/04/27/opinion/electricity-power-grid-infrastructure.html?smid=nytcore-ios-share ] The article describes in detail the difficulties of moving power across the American electricity grid, and the fact that there’s no single authority in the US that can push through national-scale upgrades efficiently. Basically, while we definitely need more energy, we also need a much bigger, faster, and more coordinated grid. 
Yesterday, Jacobin, a far-left publication, posted some surprisingly sensible observations and recommendations around the AI capex buildout in an article called Democratic Governance of AI Is the Real Solution [ https://jacobin.com/2026/04/ai-data-center-moratorium-democracy ]. Most notable (again because this is Jacobin) is that the author Holly Buck calls a moratorium on AI data centers a “terrible idea,” and essentially blames NIMBYism for such proposals:
 What if the picture that emerges of “data center resistance” is one of educated middle-class people — including exurban and rural residents but also professionals who work in knowledge jobs — mobilizing, consciously or not, to protect their class position from the threats AI poses? How many of these people will block data centers but end up paying for a subscription to a frontier model once it is clear how useful it is to navigate daily work and life? It’s not fair for affluent environmentalists and property owners to try to stop development of this infrastructure before most people in the world have even had a chance to work with and learn from these models.
Kind of a spicy article. But it’s always interesting when a piece like this breaks through to the other side of the political spectrum and results in bipartisan agreement [ https://x.com/andymasley/status/2048585835751096726?s=12 ].
