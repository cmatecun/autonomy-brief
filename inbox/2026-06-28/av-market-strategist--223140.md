# Momenta's $9B IPO Decoded, UNECE Writes the First Global Driverless Rulebook & Waymo Lands in Munich

👋 Welcome back everyone,
This week handed us a genuinely direction-setting piece of news. In Geneva, the UN body that harmonizes the world’s vehicle rules adopted the first global framework for fully driverless cars. 
But as in any week, the big news sits on top of the usual churn: new partnerships, fresh city expansions, regulatory tweaks across three continents, and one closely watched IPO filing out of China. 
That is a lot of ground to cover. So let’s not waste time.
Let’s dive in!

The week autonomy got a global rulebook
UNECE writes the first global rules for a car with no driver
At its 199th session, the UNECE World Forum for Harmonization of Vehicle Regulations (WP.29) adopted a new UN Global Technical Regulation on Automated Driving Systems (document ECE/TRANS/WP.29/2026/139). Major markets are behind it: the US, China, the EU, Japan, Canada and the UK. It enters into force in about a month. The headlines called it the first rules that “allow” fully autonomous cars. That is roughly right, but the interesting part is what was missing before, and what kind of instrument this actually is.
Until this week, the single ADS application with a dedicated UN regulation was ALKS (R157), the automated lane-keeping system built for low-speed highway traffic jams, with a human ready to take back control. For every other use case, urban driving, parking, higher-speed highway, anything performing the full driving task with nobody to fall back on, there was, in the regulation’s own words, “no clear regulation.” This GTR fills that void. It covers passenger and commercial vehicles (Category 1 and Category 2), across diverse operating environments, where the system performs the entire Dynamic Driving Task with no human in the loop. That is the line that was not there before.
This is a Global Technical Regulation, not a UN Regulation. The distinction matters. UN Regulations under the 1958 Agreement come with mutual recognition, approve a vehicle in one member state and the others accept it. GTRs under the 1998 Agreement do not. They are a common technical template that each country transposes into its own law on its own timeline. This is precisely why the US and China can sign on: both sit inside the 1998 GTR system but not the 1958 mutual-recognition one. So what was adopted in Geneva is a shared language and a shared safety bar, agreed by the markets that account for most of the world’s cars. It is not a passport. The honest framing is that the floor is now global, but the door is still opened country by country.
What does the floor actually require? Four things worth knowing.
First, a safety benchmark. The ADS must not present an “unreasonable risk to safety,” and the reference point is the performance of a “competent and careful human driver.” That is the bar manufacturers have to clear, and prove.
Second, a multi-pillar validation regime. The regulation is explicit that no single test method can validate a driverless system, so it mandates a combination: audit, simulation and virtual testing, test-track testing, and real-world testing, used together. This is the part with teeth. It is one thing to claim your system is safe. It is another to demonstrate it across four independent methodologies to an approval authority.
Third, a safety-management spine. Manufacturers must run a documented Safety Management System covering the full vehicle lifecycle, a safety culture, and a structured “safety case,” a body of evidence demonstrating the absence of unreasonable risk. The text leans on the existing alphabet of automotive standards (ISO 26262 for functional safety, ISO 21434 for cybersecurity, IATF 16949 for quality). In plain terms, you do not get to deploy and then learn. You have to govern safety as a process before and after the car is on the road.
Fourth, continuous accountability. The regulation requires in-service monitoring with short-term and periodic reporting of defined “reportable occurrences,” and it mandates a Data Storage System for Automated Driving that logs safety-relevant data. The drafters call responsibility attribution “one of the core challenges” of ADS, and they answer it with mandatory, auditable system-behaviour logging so that, in an accident, there is “an auditable basis for accountability determination.” 
Why does it matter? Because it changes the cost of going global. A shared safety case, a shared validation method, and a shared data standard mean a developer can build once against a common bar and adapt, rather than start from zero in each country.
There is one more effect, and it is the most concrete of all, especially for Europe. The EU could already type-approve a fully driverless vehicle, but only at small scale. Its interim ADS rule, Implementing Regulation (EU) 2022/1426, caps approvals at 1,500 vehicles per type per year. That is a pilot allowance, not a production line. The new UN regulation supplies the missing technical building block to lift it. Once the EU formally integrates the GTR into its type-approval framework, ADS vehicles can move from the small-series exception into genuine mass production. Scale stops being the thing that is not allowed. And the UN text itself sets no volume limit at all: the 1,500 ceiling is purely an EU small-series artifact, and the GTR is what lets Europe replace it with a full approval path.
But here is the sober counterweight, and it is one we keep returning to. Mass production was never blocked only by the lack of an approval route, because a small-series route already existed and was barely used. It was blocked by the vehicles. The honest reason you cannot buy a driverless car today is not missing paperwork. It is that the cars are not yet able to meet the requirements, including the ones this regulation just wrote down, and no L4 vehicle has been type-approved anywhere. So the GTR removes a real ceiling for a market that, for now, still has almost no products able to clear the bar beneath it. The approval problem is being solved ahead of the engineering one. That is genuine progress. It is not the same as cars on the road.
Europe writes its own rules, with a sovereignty string attached
Geneva set the global floor. Brussels spent the same week building something more pointed. The European Commission launched ADACities (the Autonomous Drive Ambition Cities initiative), announced by Executive Vice-President Henna Virkkunen at the first forum of the European Connected and Autonomous Vehicle Alliance (ECAVA) in Brussels. Billed as the mobility flagship of the EU’s Apply AI Strategy, it will let selected European cities become real-world deployment leaders across robotaxis, car-sharing, autonomous shuttles and self-driving cars, through a call for expression of interest now open to cities.
Notice the shape of the European answer, because it is consistent. The Commission states it plainly: supported partnerships “must be EU-centric, with European OEMs and tech at the core,” without excluding international collaboration, and the whole thing is wired to the EU Technological Sovereignty Package (European semiconductors, sovereign cloud, AI Factories). It builds directly on the Joint Declaration of Intent that 18 member states signed in Luxembourg three weeks ago, which carried the same sovereignty conditions: a preference for EU-made vehicles, non-EU players admitted mainly through an EU partner with a “meaningful footprint,” and data kept inside the EU. ADACities is the funding and deployment layer beneath that intent. Put them together and the European strategy is clear: harmonize the rules, pool the money, and aim the whole apparatus at producing a European champion rather than a European market for everyone else.
Here is the honest measure of the ambition, though. ADACities targets participating cities reaching fleets of 100 or more autonomous vehicles by 2030. Hold that number next to reality: Waymo already runs several thousand vehicles across 11 US cities today, in 2026. A flagship European programme whose 2030 goal is 100 cars per city is the clearest possible statement of where the continent actually stands. 
A coordinated, EU-centric, sovereignty-flavoured programme is the right instrument. But writing rules to favour a home champion only matters if the home champion shows up before the visitors do.
Which brings us to the opposite of EU-centric.
The opposite of EU-centric: a US stack and a Chinese platform, choosing Europe first
The same week Brussels was wiring its programme to protect the European value chain, a US autonomy company and a Chinese mobility platform agreed to bring robotaxis to Europe. May Mobility, the Ann Arbor-based autonomous-vehicle company, and Cao Cao, the Geely-affiliated Chinese ride-hailing platform, announced a strategic partnership to commercialize robotaxi services in international markets, “beginning with Europe.”
Source: May Mobility
This is, for now, an agreement to run joint feasibility studies and pilot programmes, with the stated objective of progressing from pilot validation toward scalable deployment. It is intent plus a workplan, not a launched service. But the division of labour is the textbook disaggregated model we keep documenting. Cao Cao serves as the fleet owner and operator, bringing its ride-hailing operations, fleet management, vehicle support and large-scale commercial muscle. May Mobility powers the fleet with its Autonomy-as-a-Service offering, built on an architecture it describes as “in-situ” reasoning that adapts to unfamiliar roads without per-city mapping. May currently operates autonomous services in the US and Japan. As its CEO and founder Edwin Olson put it, “Cao Cao has set an ambitious goal of deploying 100,000 robotaxis by 2030, and we are excited to work together to help realize this vision.” The deal is explicitly the overseas arm of Cao Cao’s RoboX strategy, the AI pivot we covered lately.
Step back and ask why May Mobility is doing this, and why now. There are two ways to read it. The charitable read is opportunity: Europe is opening, and a partner that brings vehicles and demand lets May expand without building an operator from scratch. The harder read is pressure. The US robotaxi market is becoming a brutal place for a mid-size developer. Waymo is years and thousands of vehicles ahead, Zoox is moving into the direction of commercial service, and Tesla is also on the table. Seen that way, May is not so much conquering international markets as stepping away from a home market it may not believe it can win, and looking abroad, with Chinese help, for room to grow. I lean toward a blend, with the pressure the heavier half.
And the Chinese help is the part to underline, because the Cao Cao deal is not isolated. Five weeks earlier, on 19 May, May Mobility signed a separate strategic agreement with ECARX, the Geely-affiliated Chinese automotive-tech company, to supply the full hardware stack for its next-generation robotaxi: a custom Level 4 central computing platform plus the complete sensor suite (lidar, radar, cameras), targeting at least a 50% cut in total vehicle-system cost by 2028 across tens of thousands of vehicles, a project ECARX values at roughly $750 million over its life. Now connect the dots. ECARX is Geely. Cao Cao is Geely. So a US “autonomy company” is leaning on the Geely ecosystem for its compute, its sensors, and now its international operations and demand, keeping essentially one thing American: the software brain, its Predictive World Model. That reframes the partnership we opened with. It is less “US software meets Chinese platform” and more a US software layer riding an increasingly Chinese body into Europe.
Now sit with the geometry. A US autonomy company and a Chinese mobility platform look at the world map and pick Europe as the place to start a global robotaxi push together. Not because Europe leads on deployment, but because Europe is the open, high-value market the rules are now unlocking. This is the harder version of our Europe reframe, the one that already came true in Zagreb and Madrid: the risk is not that Europe is hopelessly behind, it is that Europe becomes the distribution market for everyone else’s autonomy while it debates how to build its own. Brussels writes sovereignty rules with one hand. With the other, the door it opened is being eyed by a US-Chinese partnership.
A further US step into Europe: Waymo lands in Munich, and Germany is on the clock
And then there is Waymo, taking a step toward the continent that is more concrete than a press release. According to the commercial register, Waymo incorporated a German subsidiary, Waymo Germany GmbH: the articles of association are dated 13 May 2026, and the entity was entered at the Amtsgericht München on 15 June. The stated business purpose is not vague boilerplate. It is “offering ride services with autonomous vehicles,” plus supporting third parties who commercially offer such services. 
And it is not sitting on a shelf. Transdev, Germany’s largest private mobility provider (part of the French Transdev group, around 200 million passengers a year by bus and regional rail), is advertising for a “Betriebsleiter Autonomes Fahren,” an operations lead for autonomous driving, in Munich. Regular readers will recognize that name. Transdev is the same operator we profiled last week as Waymo’s longest-running US fleet partner, quietly running its depots and remote supervision since 2019. Separately, a staffing firm is recruiting test drivers and driver trainers for autonomous vehicles in Berlin and Munich, for work on, in the ad’s words, “real German roads, no simulator, no lab.” The Waymo link on that one is not confirmed, but the shape is unmistakable: a test phase is being stood up, and a serious operating partner is already lining up the ground game.
And Waymo is not the only one building here. At the other end of the chain, Uber is assembling the demand layer: its exclusive German contractor, the Safe Driver Group, has taken control of Taxi Frankfurt’s dispatch (more than 1,400 vehicles), and Uber is standing up its own robotaxi effort with Munich as the first site, using as its technology partner none other than Momenta, the Chinese supplier whose IPO we dissect in this week’s China Corner. The pieces of a German robotaxi market are being assembled right now, mostly by non-German hands.
As a German who writes about this every week from just outside Stuttgart, I find Waymo coming to Germany genuinely exciting.
In a separate move at home, Waymo also opened its Nashville service to everyone and will become bookable inside the Lyft app there later this year.
Tesla in Europe: one door opens in Finland, one sticks in Sweden
Tesla’s European story this week is the patchwork in miniature, the two-sided reality of a system that travels country by country. The opening door is Finland. The Finnish transport authority, Traficom, signalled it may approve Tesla’s supervised Full Self-Driving system ahead of the EU-wide decision expected in October, saying it is “prepared to proceed on a faster schedule after the summer” if it gets the information it needs on its key assessment areas. Traficom called its overall view of the system “positive,” while still working through how quickly drivers can retake control, overtaking in low-visibility conditions, and the speed-offset feature that neighbouring Sweden and Norway have flagged. Around 6,500 cars in Finland already have the system, about 0.24% of the national fleet. Tellingly, Traficom added that genuinely self-driving cars, not the supervised kind, could appear on Finnish roads as early as 2028.
This is the national-recognition fast path we have tracked the last weeks: the Netherlands approved first, then Estonia and Belgium accepted it, and Finland looks set to follow. It compresses approval from years to weeks, one country at a time.
Source: Reuters
But hold it against the door that is sticking. Last week we covered Sweden recommending the EU’s Technical Committee on Motor Vehicles vote against EU-wide FSD approval unless Tesla removes the system’s ability to exceed posted speed limits. That committee has its next member-state discussion on 30 June. So the same mechanism is running in both directions at once: Finland leaning toward yes, Sweden pushing toward no, and the EU-wide vote still ahead. The structural fragility we flagged remains. If the EU ultimately rejects the system, the Dutch provisional approval lapses after six months, and the national clearances built on it fall with it. The fast path is fast. It is also conditional.
Tesla in the US: a fatal crash, Tesla’s data-backed pushback, and a federal probe
While Europe debates approvals, the US debate is about a death. Last week we reported the fatal crash in Katy, Texas, where a Tesla Model 3 driven by Michael Butler left the road and slammed into the home of 76-year-old Martha Avila, who was later pronounced dead. Butler told Harris County deputies the car was on Autopilot. This week the story developed in two directions worth separating carefully.
First, Tesla pushed back. Ashok Elluswamy, Tesla’s VP of AI software and the first engineer on the Autopilot team, posted that the vehicle’s data showed “the driver manually overrode self-driving by pressing the accelerator all the way to 100% of the accel pedal in this residential area,” reaching 73 mph during the crash and keeping the accelerator pressed even after impact. Elon Musk amplified the point, arguing a high-speed crash is inconsistent with FSD, which drives slowly on neighbourhood streets. 
Second, the regulator is not taking anyone’s word for it. NHTSA confirmed to TechCrunch it has opened a special investigation, reportedly its fortieth-plus probe into Tesla crashes thought to involve driver-assistance systems, and the Harris County Sheriff will present findings to the district attorney on possible charges. Whether the system was active, overridden or malfunctioning will not be settled until investigators finish the data logs.
And one door the US may open wide: no brake pedal required
There is, however, good news for the purpose-built side of the American industry. The Trump administration’s Department of Transportation proposed changing federal rules to let companies skip the brake pedal in “vehicles designed to be driven exclusively by automated driving systems.” A 30-day public comment period now runs before any decision. Today, US safety standards (the FMVSS) are written around a human driver, so a vehicle missing those controls needs a special exemption, and regulations cap how many such vehicles can be on the road. Remove the requirement for genuine driverless designs and you remove that bottleneck. NHTSA Administrator Jonathan Morrison framed it in epochal terms, calling this “the greatest technological revolution in vehicle technology since the innovation of the Model T” and pledging to tear down “pointless barriers” under Secretary Duffy’s AV Framework.
It is not a Tesla-only gift. It helps anyone building from scratch with no driver in mind. Tesla’s Cybercab, a two-seater designed without wheel or pedals, benefits, though notably Tesla has never actually filed for the existing exemption, preferring to wait for the rules to change. Zoox, which holds a demonstration exemption and is waiting on a commercial one, benefits directly.
What this all means and what to watch
First, the regulatory bottleneck just got smaller, globally. The UNECE GTR gives every market a common safety bar to copy. But it is a template, not a passport. Watch how fast contracting parties transpose it into national law.
Second, Europe’s strategy is coherent and its timing is the risk. ADACities plus the Luxembourg declaration is the right answer to fragmentation and capital, but a 2030 target of 100 vehicles per city sits against visitors who run thousands now. The May Mobility and Cao Cao announcement, plus Waymo’s Munich entity, are reminders that the visitors are moving while the home team is still being assembled. The question is no longer whether Europe can build, it is whether it can build before its market is furnished by others.
Third, the Tesla patchwork. Finland leaning yes, Sweden pushing no, the TCMV discussion on 30 June, and a US special investigation all land inside the same window. Two tells to watch: whether the EU-wide vote validates or voids the national fast path, and whether the data logs confirm Tesla’s driver-override account.

The export machine grows up, on right-hand roads and public markets
Pony.ai opens to the public in Singapore, with a local operator carrying the demand
Pony.ai moved from invitation-only testing to a public service in Singapore. From 22 June, riders in the Punggol district can book its robotaxis through the Zig app, run by local partner ComfortDelGro, along a route connecting Punggol’s residential areas with Punggol Coast Mall, the Punggol Coast MRT station, One Punggol, Oasis Terrace and Punggol Plaza. Pony.ai is targeting more than 3,500 robotaxis across over 20 cities by the end of 2026, and Singapore is a deliberate piece of that international map. Founder and CEO James Peng framed the goal as service that can “complement existing public transport,” while ComfortDelGro’s group CEO Cheng Siak Kian traced the line from the partners’ robotaxi pilot in Guangzhou to this public launch in Punggol.
Look at the structure: a Chinese autonomy developer supplies the driver, a local operator (ComfortDelGro) supplies the demand, the app and the ground operations. The same disaggregated split we see in Europe, now in Southeast Asia. And it is contested turf: WeRide also runs a Singapore service with Grab, making the city-state another overseas battlefield where the Chinese players line up against each other rather than against Western incumbents.
And Singapore is a right-hand-drive market. That is the bridge to this week’s bigger China story.
WeRide, Farizon and Kwoon Chung build a robotaxi natively for right-hand-drive roads
On 22 June in Hong Kong, WeRide, Geely’s commercial-vehicle arm Farizon, and Hong Kong transit operator Kwoon Chung Motors signed a partnership to jointly develop a native, mass-produced right-hand-drive robotaxi based on WeRide’s existing GXR platform, with commercial service planned in the city. The framing from the partners is that this is a shift from “regional capability” to “global adaptation.”
The logic for Hong Kong is sound. It is one of the world’s densest and most demanding right-hand-drive markets, with complex mixed traffic and mature transit, which makes it both a stress test and a springboard. And the Chinese field is already converging there: Baidu’s Apollo Go has secured Hong Kong’s first batch of right-hand-drive test licences, and Pony.ai has run semi-closed testing near the airport. Kwoon Chung had previously brought a left-hand-drive GXR to Hong Kong under a pilot licence, so this is described as a native upgrade rather than an import. The stated prize is the wider right-hand-drive world: Singapore, Japan, the UK, Australia.
Now the part worth thinking critically about, because the “native right-hand-drive vehicle” framing deserves a harder look. For a fully driverless car, the side the steering wheel sits on is close to irrelevant. There is no human reaching for it. So what does “native development” actually buy you when the driver is software?
It’s probably the following: The driving policy itself, retrained for left-side traffic where merges, roundabouts and turn priorities are mirror images of what the system learned at home; sensor placement and calibration tuned to the opposite traffic geometry; homologation and type approval, which each right-hand-drive market demands on its own terms; and passenger access, because curbside pickup and the doors move to the other side of the vehicle. Those are genuine engineering and regulatory tasks. Moving a steering wheel is not the point.
Momenta files for its Hong Kong IPO, and the filing tells a different story than the headlines
Staying in Hong Kong, Momenta, the Chinese driving-automation supplier, filed for a Hong Kong listing and, per Reuters, plans to launch the IPO as early as the start of next week, pricing around 3 July and debuting around 8 July, raising about $900 million at a valuation near $9 billion. Its cornerstone list could include existing investor Mercedes-Benz, alongside Boyu Capital and Oaktree.
Asked about the plans, the company said only that it “is not in a position to comment on market speculation or reports concerning any potential capital markets activities.”
The news coverage led with the loss: Reuters noted losses widened to about 3.5 billion yuan (roughly $516 million) in 2025. That number is real, and it is also misleading on its own. I read the filing, and the actual picture is more interesting than the headline.
First, what the company actually is, because the filing is explicit about it. Momenta runs two business lines. The first, “Solutions for Mass-produced Vehicles,” is its L2 assisted-driving business: the software (L2-ADAS, highway NOA and urban NOA) it sells into cars that automakers ship to consumers today. The second, “Solutions for Robotaxi Services,” is the L4 ambition: a driverless mobility and logistics business, monetized as a share of ride fares. 
Start with the top line. Revenue grew from RMB 742.7 million in 2023 to RMB 1.32 billion in 2024 to RMB 2.41 billion in 2025 (roughly $340 million), compounding at more than 78% a year. Gross margin moved from 17.5% to 49.0% to 71.6%. That is not a hardware supplier’s margin curve. That is a software company’s.
The reason is the mix shift, and it is the bull case in one line. In 2023, technical-development service (bespoke engineering contracts) was 96.8% of revenue and licensing was 3.1%. By 2025, licensing had grown to 40.1% of revenue (RMB 967.9 million) while technical development fell to 59.9%. Momenta is moving from selling custom projects to licensing software that scales.
Now the loss, because this is where the headline misleads. The reported net loss for 2025 was RMB 3.46 billion, the figure the press picked up. But the single biggest component is a non-cash item: RMB 2.84 billion of “fair-value changes on preferred shares.”
Here is the mechanism, and I want to be transparent that this is my reading of the accounting, though I am fairly confident it is right. The preferred shares Momenta issued to its investors are not counted as equity under the accounting rules. Because of their investor protections, they sit on the balance sheet as a liability, re-measured to fair value every year. As Momenta’s valuation climbed, that liability climbed with it, and a rising liability books as a loss.
So the paradox is that the better the company did, the larger this “loss” grew, which is exactly why 2025’s was the biggest of the three years. No cash left the business. And it stops after the IPO: on listing, those preferred shares convert into ordinary shares, which are equity, and equity is not re-marked through the income statement, so the charge simply does not recur in future years. 
Remove that preferred-share swing alone and the loss drops to about RMB 615 million. Momenta’s own adjusted (non-IFRS) loss goes a step further, also stripping out share-based compensation (RMB 292 million) and listing costs, which brings it to RMB 302.8 million, or 12.6% of revenue, down from a 147% loss ratio in 2023. Be precise about that last step, though, because it is the kind of thing a careful reader should push on. Share-based compensation is non-cash, but unlike the preferred-share charge it does not vanish at listing, and it does dilute shareholders, so the honest underlying loss sits somewhere between the RMB 615 million and the RMB 303 million, not at the flattering end.
Operating cash burn narrowed to RMB 281.4 million from over RMB 1 billion two years earlier. Cash on hand at year-end was RMB 1.31 billion (about $185 million), against R&D spending of RMB 1.87 billion a year, which is precisely why it needs the public market now.
On position, the filing claims Momenta ranked first globally among independent providers of urban-NOA solutions, with a 64.5% share over the trailing twelve months to February 2026. Its customer and shareholder roster is a who’s-who: SAIC (its largest institutional shareholder), Mercedes-Benz, BMW, Audi, Volkswagen, Toyota and GM, plus a cross-shareholding with SAIC’s IM Motors.
Source: Gasgoo
Two cautions: First, customer concentration. The top five customers fell from 86.7% of revenue in 2023 to 62.6% in 2025, which is healthy diversification, but the single largest customer ticked back up to 21.6% in 2025. Second, the L4 half, Solutions for Robotaxi Services, is by Momenta’s own filing immaterial to revenue today. So strip away the robotaxi storyline and this is, for now, an L2 and urban-NOA company with an L4 ambition, not a robotaxi company. The fares are a future story, not a current one.
There is a wider lens worth holding here, because Momenta is not the only supplier straddling L2 and L4, and the comparison is instructive. Three companies sell driving software across both levels, but from opposite directions. Momenta and Wayve are L2-dominant: their revenue and their data engine today are assisted-driving and urban-NOA programs for mass-produced cars, with L4 robotaxi service as the reach. WeRide runs the other way, L4-dominant, built on robotaxis and pushing that road data down into a growing L2+ ADAS business, the “double flywheel” we have logged.
Same two levels, opposite centres of gravity. And the direction matters more than it sounds. WeRide’s CEO Tony Han argues, as Pony.ai’s Lou Tiancheng does, that L2 and L4 are not two points on one path but fundamentally different problems, with Han’s bar for “real” L4 being at least fifty driverless cars running in a million-person city. Read that asymmetrically and it is a pointed claim: climbing from L2 up to L4, the path Momenta and Wayve are reaching for, is the hard direction, while coming down from L4 to L2 is the “easier” one. And here WeRide does not just talk. Its WRD 3.0 ADAS, trained on L4 road data, just won a sixth straight Chinese urban-driving competition, the clearest evidence so far that the L4-to-L2 direction actually delivers. My honest caveat: every CEO talks their book, and L2-to-L4 is probably not impossible. But the cleaner proof on the table today is the L4-leader-also-winning-at-L2 case, not the reverse.
What this means and what to watch
The export machine is entering a new phase. The Chinese players continue trying to become the world’s default supplier of autonomy, and this week showed the next step in that ambition: native deployment. The right-hand-drive GXR is the tell. It is one thing to ship a retrofitted car into a foreign city for a pilot. It is another to design the vehicle and the driving policy from the ground up for a whole class of markets, Singapore, Japan, the UK, Australia, and to route the effort through Hong Kong as the staging ground. 
Second, Momenta is a company I will be watching more closely. Its position in China is genuinely impressive: number one among independent urban-NOA providers, a near-duopoly with Huawei, and a margin curve that looks like software. That is a strong L2 business, and on the L2 evidence I am a believer. The open question is the other half. What does Momenta actually deliver on Level 4, and can it afford to find out? Robotaxi is immaterial in the numbers today, and L4 at scale is a money furnace. For the L2 business, survival shouldn’t be the issue. Funding a real L4 push is. Against that bar, roughly $185 million of cash at year-end looks modest, which is exactly why the IPO matters, and why even a successful raise of around $900 million buys a credible attempt rather than a guarantee. So the thing I will be watching is easy to state and hard to answer: whether Momenta can become more than an excellent L2 company before the cash question forces the choice.
