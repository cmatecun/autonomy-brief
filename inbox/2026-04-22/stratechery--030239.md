John Ternus and Apple’s Hardware-Defined Future
-----------------------------------------------

Yesterday’s Article was about Tim Cook’s tenure as Apple CEO; what about John Ternus, his replacement? From Mark Gurman at Bloomberg ( https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-21/apple-bets-new-ceo-john-ternus-will-bring-back-jobs-era-decisiveness ) :

Ternus, 50, joined Apple in 2001, four years after graduating from the University of Pennsylvania with a mechanical engineering degree. To succeed in this next chapter, he will need to keep what works — operational discipline and calm leadership — while breaking from the consensus-driven decision-making that has defined Cook’s tenure. He will also need to move faster, sharpen Apple’s competitiveness in AI and deliver new hardware hits…

The Vision Pro — long envisioned by Cook as a capstone product — has flopped, despite a decade of development and billions in investment. Apple also spent roughly $10 billion on an autonomous-car project that was ultimately scrapped. In both cases, Ternus’ instincts appear to have been more cautious; he opposed the initiatives to varying degrees.

Where Ternus has excelled is execution. He has ensured that Apple consistently delivers updated versions of the iPhone, iPad, Mac and Apple Watch each year, while improving hardware quality, durability and performance — hallmarks of his stint as hardware chief.

Ternus also was a champion of the MacBook Neo, a product that broke with Apple’s typical premium-minded approach…This was the beginning of Ternus putting his stamp on the company, but he’ll now have to lead Apple into additional new categories. The tech giant is currently focused on two main areas of expansion: AI-powered smart home products and wearable devices.

I don’t know anything about Ternus; I’ll evaluate him based on the decisions he makes going forward. What we do know is his background at Apple, and in that sense it’s worth remembering Cook’s background when he took over. I wrote yesterday that Cook’s timing was impeccable ( https://stratechery.com/2026/tim-cooks-impeccable-timing/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDI2L3RpbS1jb29rcy1pbXBlY2NhYmxlLXRpbWluZy8iXX0sImV4cCI6MTc3OTQ0NDE1OSwiaWF0IjoxNzc2ODUyMTU5LCJpc3MiOiJodHRwczovL2FwcC5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUvb2F1dGgiLCJzY29wZSI6ImZlZWQ6cmVhZCBhcnRpY2xlOnJlYWQgYXNzZXQ6cmVhZCBjYXRlZ29yeTpyZWFkIGVudGl0bGVtZW50cyIsInN1YiI6IjAxOTUxNmYxLWEzNDYtNzYxNC05ZTk2LWI2M2JkMTFmZTg1NiIsInVzZSI6ImFjY2VzcyJ9.ctwwW4R8y5z9G6uL-Jhqwub8_BYlFvWmOkhc3gyMGOgZLUaX5bQw6z4SSzM6ZCJLUTWgtTyRhXjwwG8NAr5NK2A_KZ0Cvp8--tlbVVlLkzlxhjpR5IJwBIUfS__D9Dt4Cv_0XcjHsLrGY_OU_xYwkioAl059z4bEh9hDJGs3JfuJzVhumkVVeyhVx-Sca2UKjA9It_rGDC2u2sP416q1oCnqFxendKYrHDJRDiDIoSIxC1IiQ_QlwmVzJC_87-pKYNJ6QypONMfoyTCJaahYGDpL_Zkq0rxI_-hZf5adRgb31taEDepu-Xfrv0YX2aQto1qO3w9tntDVZwP7HmyIzg ) in terms of being able to capitalize on products created under Steve Jobs, but he was also the right man for the job at that specific moment in time: what Apple needed to do in 2011 was scale, and Cook was the master of scale.

To that end, what does Apple need today? One obvious answer is that they need an AI visionary, but that runs head-on into the problem of there not being any AI visionaries in leadership positions at Apple. You could also make the case that Apple needs software leadership, particularly given the ways that its software platforms have slipped over the last few years (particularly macOS). On the flipside, if the case is that Apple’s software has slipped, then surely current software leadership doesn’t deserve to be promoted, and more pertinently, is software going to be the most important differentiator in the AI age?

That leaves hardware, and the biggest factor in Ternus’ favor is that Apple’s hardware is in excellent shape, from the iPhone to the Mac and the Apple Silicon that powers both. To the extent that Apple was going to hire internally — and there is no way they were not going to — Ternus is the obvious choice for that reason alone.

Even beyond that, however, I go back to an Article I wrote in 2020 when the M1 launched called Apple’s Shifting Differentiation ( https://stratechery.com/2020/apples-shifting-differentiation/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDIwL2FwcGxlcy1zaGlmdGluZy1kaWZmZXJlbnRpYXRpb24vIl19LCJleHAiOjE3Nzk0NDQxNTksImlhdCI6MTc3Njg1MjE1OSwiaXNzIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9hcHAucGFzc3BvcnQub25saW5lL29hdXRoIiwic2NvcGUiOiJmZWVkOnJlYWQgYXJ0aWNsZTpyZWFkIGFzc2V0OnJlYWQgY2F0ZWdvcnk6cmVhZCBlbnRpdGxlbWVudHMiLCJzdWIiOiIwMTk1MTZmMS1hMzQ2LTc2MTQtOWU5Ni1iNjNiZDExZmU4NTYiLCJ1c2UiOiJhY2Nlc3MifQ.J9CGKYfv2fiUxZBPX0L7dLyMd5oH7eWxfDBgf0wK_XzlQn-WQpNrjvGDZyWGdGBXhs5AEYgsoDg6NApWusxR-bXGOd8nUnaqsx_aemUL1ndl-xOsMf40gfmcojpR1MYf5m22PBvE14A3fgXq33s0pTk11Wrkk1vo2Rqz4vGoUEI_fWOkb8b-TX5w-114PWSqDgyo_nNShido3M-ng6mUMO1WmG8wqTpxwwd-o9uKHc7vuO99-UBTEyjDyWbrpajZT_nBDjIus4OFlYJUiJyTSFX7N1CQC_jyiFjjyhcHdiOYaYxYcngQPglwADcBCf8NPNIeKpKxOpmFuhN7Z4GA6A ). From the introduction:

What is fascinating is that while modern Apple is indeed characterized by the integration of hardware and software, the balance of which differentiates the other has shifted over time, culminating in yesterday’s announcement of new Macs powered by Apple Silicon.

I traced three distinct periods in modern Apple’s history (i.e. starting from Jobs’ return in 1996):

* Apple 1.0 was differentiated by software, which sold the company’s products despite inferior hardware, particularly in terms of processor performance; this is when the shift to Intel really paid off.
* Apple 2.0 was the apex of integration, where Apple created entirely new products (the iPod and iPhone) that were only possible by controlling both hardware and software.
* Apple 3.0 was what was emerging when I wrote that Article: Apple’s software differentiation was diminishing thanks to rough app parity with Android, the shift to web apps on the desktop, and Microsoft’s embrace of Linux. That was why Apple Silicon was so important: the best reason to buy an Apple device was because they had superior performance.

I wrote in the conclusion:

It is possible that Apple’s chip team is so far ahead of the competition, not just in 2020, but particularly as it develops even more powerful versions of Apple Silicon, that the commoditization of software inherent in web apps will work to Apple’s favor, just as its move to Intel commoditized hardware, highlighting Apple’s then-software advantage in the 00s…the company believes it can take both share and margin, and it’s a reasonable bet from my perspective. The company has the best chips in the world, and you have to buy the entire integrated widget to get them.

The MacBook Neo is the apotheosis of this vision in terms of the current software paradigm; more generally, Ternus’ elevation to CEO is the ultimate affirmation of this shift.

Still, this argument is, to a certain extent, looking backwards. To that end, the other takeaway I have from this appointment is that Apple has in all likelihood decided that focusing on devices and edge compute is the best way for it to compete in AI. In other words, not only do I think that Ternus was involved in the decision to adopt Gemini as the basis of Apple’s AI efforts, I think that decision is in fact inseparable from the decision to make Ternus CEO — in many respects it’s the same decision. Apple is betting that making the best hardware will carry the day, that AI will become commoditized — and, to the extent it does not, that differentiated AI will at least run (and run best) on Apple’s devices.

I think it’s a reasonable bet. It is, however, a bet!

SpaceXAI and Cursor
-------------------

From Bloomberg ( https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-21/spacex-says-has-agreement-to-acquire-cursor-for-60-billion ) :

SpaceX said it has an agreement giving it the right to acquire artificial intelligence startup Cursor for $60 billion later this year or to pay $10 billion for the companies’ work together, part of the Elon Musk-run firm’s efforts to catch up with rivals in AI coding tools. Musk’s rocket, satellite and artificial intelligence giant announced the deal in a post on X ( https://x.com/spacex/status/2046713419978453374?s=46 ) , saying the two companies are “now working closely together to create the world’s best coding and knowledge work AI.”

SpaceX recently merged with xAI, Musk’s artificial intelligence company, which competes with Anthropic and OpenAI in creating generative AI tools for consumers and businesses. The deal comes shortly after Musk said that xAI is behind on software coding tools compared with peers and vowed to rebuild the company from the ground up. In March, he ordered a round of layoffs. He’s also been seeking engineering talent, and has previously hired from Cursor.

It’s hard to know what to make of the situation at what is apparently now known as SpaceXAI; from an in-depth article on Tech Insider ( https://tech-insider.org/xai-co-founders-exodus-spacex-acquisition-2026/ ) :

In a development that has sent shockwaves through the artificial intelligence industry, all 11 original co-founders of Elon Musk’s xAI have now departed the company. The final two—Manuel Kroiss and Ross Nordeen—left in late March 2026, completing an unprecedented leadership exodus that began accelerating after SpaceX’s landmark acquisition of xAI in February 2026. The departures raise fundamental questions about the future of Grok, the $18 billion Colossus supercomputer in Memphis, and whether Musk’s vision of rebuilding xAI under SpaceX’s engineering culture can succeed without the elite AI researchers who helped build it.

I think the technical term for the current situation is “a mess”, which is quite a step down from a year ago, when Grok 4 was, for a brief time, arguably the best model in the world. Meanwhile, the compute buildout continues; from the same article:

At the center of xAI’s value proposition—and the tensions driving the co-founder exodus—sits Colossus, the world’s largest AI supercomputer. Located in South Memphis, Tennessee, the facility represents an infrastructure investment of staggering proportions and ambition that continues to expand even as the researchers who were meant to use it depart.

Colossus was originally built in just 122 days, an achievement that Musk frequently cited as evidence of xAI’s ability to move faster than competitors. The initial configuration housed 100,000 Nvidia GPUs drawing approximately 150 megawatts of power. Within 92 days, the facility doubled to 200,000 GPUs, incorporating 50,000 of Nvidia’s H200 units with 141GB of HBM3e memory, pushing power consumption to 250 megawatts.

By early 2026, Colossus 1 alone housed over 230,000 GPUs, including more than 30,000 of Nvidia’s GB200 Blackwell chips. But the real scale of Musk’s ambition became clear in January 2026, when xAI announced the acquisition of a third Memphis building, expanding the total facility to 555,000 Nvidia GPUs across 2 gigawatts of power capacity—enough to power approximately 1.5 million homes. The GPU investment alone for this expanded facility is estimated at $18 billion.

The infrastructure continues to grow. On March 5, 2026, Teslarati reported that xAI filed plans for a $659 million building expansion at the Memphis site, targeting a 79-acre parcel at 5414 Tulane Road. The facility uses direct liquid cooling systems that pipe coolant directly to chips, enabling extreme hardware density, along with Tesla Megapacks for energy storage and power stability. Rather than relying on traditional grid infrastructure, xAI is constructing an on-site gas-fired power plant to provide self-contained power generation for the full 2-gigawatt load.

The paradox is striking: xAI is investing billions in expanding the physical infrastructure needed to train frontier AI models while simultaneously losing every researcher who understood how to use that infrastructure most effectively. As one industry observer noted, “You can buy GPUs. You cannot buy the institutional knowledge of how to train a model that competes with GPT-5 or Claude Opus.”

This does, from afar, look like a classic Musk failure case: Musk, more than anyone basically ever, understands the economic power of leveraging fixed cost investments, whether that be car plants, rocket ships, or satellites; what he seems to have considerably less dexterity for is software in general, and open-ended exploration of unbound spaces. AI sits in both worlds: there is the infrastructure part, which Musk excels at, and the exploration of the ineffable, which Musk does not. The end result is that SpaceXAI has a ton of compute, and no one to use it, either for R&D or inference.

Cursor, meanwhile, reached a peak of its own a year ago, when it was the only AI application other than ChatGPT earning meaningful revenue. It’s a real product with real customers, in a real trap: Cursor has to pay the frontier labs for API access to their models, which themselves offer heavily subsidized and increasingly agentic coding products on a subscription basis. The end result is a startup with real traction that is both bleeding money and leading edge customers.

What Cursor needs is two things: first, it needs its own frontier model that is fully integrated into its own harness, with its own agentic capabilities, and second it needs compute to be able to sell its product and actually make money while doing so.

There is reason to think that Cursor might be able to make a compelling model if they could afford to; CEO Michael Truell made the case in a Stratechery Interview last year ( https://stratechery.com/2025/an-interview-with-cursor-co-founder-and-ceo-michael-truell-about-coding-with-ai/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDI1L2FuLWludGVydmlldy13aXRoLWN1cnNvci1jby1mb3VuZGVyLWFuZC1jZW8tbWljaGFlbC10cnVlbGwtYWJvdXQtY29kaW5nLXdpdGgtYWkvIl19LCJleHAiOjE3Nzk0NDQxNTksImlhdCI6MTc3Njg1MjE1OSwiaXNzIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9hcHAucGFzc3BvcnQub25saW5lL29hdXRoIiwic2NvcGUiOiJmZWVkOnJlYWQgYXJ0aWNsZTpyZWFkIGFzc2V0OnJlYWQgY2F0ZWdvcnk6cmVhZCBlbnRpdGxlbWVudHMiLCJzdWIiOiIwMTk1MTZmMS1hMzQ2LTc2MTQtOWU5Ni1iNjNiZDExZmU4NTYiLCJ1c2UiOiJhY2Nlc3MifQ.OYn256WNkCD7tfMDPS44Ilvek8IRRHScdThj-IqaFz44ox4bXWugHg4MdzGALBwZ-L22AFaRGTGu07OkHq_8HVfReFkc4lQ-3MAhuJS2tnkBrLBA2xuPLBlom6aO1gzY0GTNVZNxWm_5bP9uzKFpXooOXWz01DgnUFkSgUQTUI-MS3c0zYheFReKGMn6uCFt-z722-QbDqcg9NGOJlMrWS7BbDDBvb2LO-x0Nbq8PKmcCET5S4N2fU4eEFxtW1cH8SE9EXbZroHWu3Esw6m-gmmImLi5OLgbTsfbGrM5OVmhcsjVOIPunRTz0_A5rFmLbp0qSHkdn_wFXZTA5Zf4lw ) that the company was acquiring highly differentiated data in terms of coding:

*Is that a real sustainable advantage for you going forward, where you can really dominate the space because you have the usage data, it’s not just calling out to an LLM, that got you started, but now you’re training your own models based on people using Cursor. You started out by having the whole context of the code, which is the first thing you need to do to even accomplish this, but now you have your own data to train on.*

*MT:* Yeah, I think it’s a big advantage, and I think these dynamics of high ceiling, you can kind of pick between products and then this kind of third dynamic of distribution then gets your data, which then helps you make the product better. I think all three of those things were shared by search at the end of the 90s and early 2000s, and so in many ways I think that actually, the competitive dynamics of our market mirror search more than normal enterprise software markets.

The theory of the case is that Cursor has a huge amount of proprietary data about coding and how people actually interact with LLMs, which should be a solid foundation for building the agentic model of the future. Again, however, training is exceptionally expensive, and serving even more so.

I think you can see where I am going here: setting aside the weird nature of this deal, what with the option-to-buy and all, and the numbers involved (which honestly, given the silly numbers Musk has attached to the acquisitions of first X and then xAI, who’s counting), there is really obvious synergy between SpaceXAI and Cursor: the former has compute, and the latter has a product, data, and a decent amount of distribution for the use case that is most important for AI. At a high level this tie-up makes a lot of sense.
