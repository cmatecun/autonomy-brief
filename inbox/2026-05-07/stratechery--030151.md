Good morning,

This week’s Stratechery Interview is with Joanna Stern ( https://x.com/JoannaStern ). Stern was, until just a couple of months ago, the Senior Personal Technology Columnist at the Wall Street Journal ( https://www.wsj.com/news/author/joanna-stern ). I previously interviewed Stern in February 2024 ( https://stratechery.com/2024/an-interview-with-joanna-stern-about-creating-content-and-the-apple-vision-pro/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDI0L2FuLWludGVydmlldy13aXRoLWpvYW5uYS1zdGVybi1hYm91dC1jcmVhdGluZy1jb250ZW50LWFuZC10aGUtYXBwbGUtdmlzaW9uLXByby8iXX0sImV4cCI6MTc4MDc0MDExMSwiaWF0IjoxNzc4MTQ4MTExLCJpc3MiOiJodHRwczovL2FwcC5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUvb2F1dGgiLCJzY29wZSI6ImZlZWQ6cmVhZCBhcnRpY2xlOnJlYWQgYXNzZXQ6cmVhZCBjYXRlZ29yeTpyZWFkIGVudGl0bGVtZW50cyIsInN1YiI6IjAxOTUxNmYxLWEzNDYtNzYxNC05ZTk2LWI2M2JkMTFmZTg1NiIsInVzZSI6ImFjY2VzcyJ9.pxNzoJwpsGCahSqOZ9ys1Cr1_uXfp2iJ_3dq_BueCccT2MyAjlZKGTibQnoCvfakW7DARo8HuFgtC2jJ1pH2lI5OH0-RMfd7dTWrOMSkkb6LUuHO7QqQgXUL0x5PiHaEWbzep3rzkLFvSshocSSC-dqS_5hTL-BYyu7RJSY7zoH20trzfkOl5tlcDSDg0bfoXFjDACN9Rf0OEYwsyRBqb08MS2GTT_zKWWNw8afzlNS-o_ZJxhN565jx1dyWCFi4H4xsd-ABKMvlvvK-ebNPYjKEWQexdhQ8opXx0YcQLHOMYjis18mfh13eWdrZvgJ7J2bpkMTxYHadl3FQWbym5Q ) , where we discussed not just her background and path to the Walt Mossberg-pioneered column seeking to explain tech to normal people, but also her process in terms of creating some of the most entertaining and compelling tech product videos in the industry.

Stern, I am happy to report, is still doing just that. First, Stern has started her own newsletter/column called New Things with Joanna Stern ( https://thenewthings.com ). Second, Stern has a new YouTube channel ( https://www.youtube.com/c/joannastern ). Third, Stern is partnering with NBC ( https://variety.com/2026/tv/news/joanna-stern-joins-nbc-news-chief-technology-analyst-1236691309/ ) to distribute her videos as their chief technology analyst.

We cover all of that in this Interview, but the number one reason to talk to Stern now is that she has just written her first book: I Am Not a Robot: My Year Using AI to Do (Almost) Everything ( https://joannastern.com ). You can pre-order the book now ( https://joannastern.com/#preorder ) , and it comes out on Tuesday, May 12.

As you will see in this interview, I really enjoyed the book. We talk about the overall concept for the book, why it’s about more than LLMs, and how Stern balances her technology obsession with empathy for normal people (and why that means the book isn’t out of date). Then we get into some philosophical questions about AI, including whether it can replace humans, what actually worries Stern about the future, and why both the overly optimistic and overly pessimistic get things wrong. Finally, we cover Stern’s new business ventures, and what she learned from 13 years at the Wall Street Journal.

Is the worst case scenario — because everyone talks about, there’s a huge product overhang, everything that exists today can be used to do a huge number of things that still need to be built — so is the worst case scenario that we get all the disruption of AI, but none of the upsides that everyone’s promising?*

*JS:* You mean all these amazing promises that I know people have talked about with you on your show and you’ve heard.

*Maybe we just lose a bunch of jobs and we don’t cure cancer.*

*JS:* Right, and we don’t cure cancer and our children don’t get personalized tutors and we don’t have all of this free time to not work. I guess too, it’s interesting to be on the other side of this and now six months after I stopped writing the book, because it has gotten better. We are seeing a lot of this impact, we’ve seen at least some of the job impact evolve since I finished writing.

*You talk in the book about AI helping you quit your job. I do have to ask, you said ChatGPT was the only one that was honest with you in that decision, people would sort of like talk around it and try to leave it up to you, they didn’t want to take responsibility, but ChatGPT said what you needed to hear. That feels in conflict with the sycophantic criticism, did ChatGPT actually tell you what you wanted to hear?*

*JS:* But I kept saying I didn’t want to hear that, right? I kept challenging it.

*Yeah, but maybe it knew you pretty well.*

*JS:* I know, and I kept challenging it to say, “Give me all the reasons I shouldn’t do this”, “What could go wrong?”, and it would give me some of that, but it was also ultimately like, “Look, you’ve got this deal coming through with NBC”, it knew all these parts, it knew things, I also couldn’t always, some humans I could tell in full, but others I couldn’t. It knew I had this deal with NBC coming through, it knew I could possibly have these hires and people who could really support me on this, it knew what kind of runway in terms of money I had. Of course, it really could, you’re right, it could have just been saying, “You can do this, Joanna, you’re you’re good”.

*But maybe that makes the point where AI will for the people who are truly creative and take initiative and don’t follow the rules, a little sycophantism could actually be useful, it’s like, “I just gotta go out and do it”.*

*JS:* Right, “Go out and do it”, but also it had a lot of notes, it had notes from everyone. The interesting thing I did here was I uploaded the notes, I’d spoken to so many people over the year.

*Well, you spoke to me after our interview in 2024, so you said you had been thinking about it for a year, but I can attest to you, you were thinking about it for longer than that.*

*JS:* I know, I don’t think I actually had notes from you, so maybe I should upload those and see what the decision would have been different, but I think actually you were encouraging. It did see all sides of it, it had notes from people at the Journal on what they were offering me, it had all of these notes of all of these people giving me advice or opportunities, and I just said, “What should I do?”, and so here we are.

*Here we are.*

*JS:* And so if I have to blame ChatGPT at the end of this…

*They can take it.*

Looking Backwards

*In your last Wall Street Journal column ( https://www.wsj.com/tech/personal-tech/12-years-consumer-tech-phones-ev-ai-74107804 ) , you sent a letter to your – was it 2013? That was when you started? It was 12 years prior.*

*JS:* Well, it’s funny. I actually started on New Year’s Day 2013, it was CES 2013, I will never forget.

*That was actually the year I started Stratechery, so what did you tell your 2013 self? I’ll link to the column, but what still stands out to you from writing that?*

*JS:* I think that for me, so many times over the period at the Journal, I remember thinking, “Tech is so boring now”, I had been covering tech, I’d been covering tech since 2010 — or no, I graduated college in 2006 — 2008 was when I really got my first tech writing job.

So I lived through the smartphones and I lived through the iPhone introduction and the iPad introduction and all of this exciting movement in the industry, and I think when I got to the Journal, the smartphone was kind of mature, the iPhone 6 was around then launched, and I kept saying, “Some days this feels so boring”, and I worked so hard to find stories that I didn’t think was boring. If I thought tech was so boring, I would have gotten out of it, I was still so excited by things, so for me, looking back on that decade plus at the Journal and seeing the rapid improvement of things.

*How much stuff actually did change, more than you realized as was happening.*

*JS:* Yeah, how much actually did change since the iPhone 6 and how much actually did change in the world of social media and cellular networks and all of these things was remarkable. We sometimes forget about that change and then obviously the last three years has been rapid change.

Part of the reason I wanted to take a leap at this moment was I think there’s something interesting about taking a career change at the same time as technological change because you can reshape what you’re doing and sometimes it’s hard to reshape what you’re doing when you’ve been doing the same thing for so long. Obviously, I think that’s going to be harder now that I started my own company, but I think you can keep reinventing yourself. You have, right? You use all different types of tech and all different types of platforms to tell stories and you’re not held back by anyone, which I have just been so blown away at how much freedom I have now to do things.

*For better or for worse.*

*JS:* For better or for worse. I mean, I’ve got an AI agent running rogue in our Slack right now, so let’s hope that goes okay.

*It was the iPhone 4, the only reason I know is because it was the iPhone 4 when I started Stratechery. Like I said, it was the same time. Is genuine contrarianism the final human differentiator? At the end of the day, “Thank you for the input AI, I’m going to make my own decision”.*

*JS:* I think it depends on what kind of human you are. I think making decisions is very tough. I’m not great — I mean, I’m great at making small decisions — I think big decisions really weigh on me, I have a lot of anxiety around making big decisions. I’ve always had that problem in my life, whether it was getting married, having kids.

*As recounted in the book.*

*JS:* Yes, exactly, as recounted in the book. But I think people can relate to that, it’s easy to make a decision about what you want to get for dinner or as I say in the book, even in the case of like ordering dinner, I’m usually like, “Oh, maybe I’m going to try something different, maybe I’m going to go like really out there”, and then I’m like, “No, I’ll just get the chicken, the plain chicken and some French fries would be great”, I think humans can relate to that.

But you have to come at all of this and all of these decisions and everything AI says with some questioning and I hope that’s one of the big takeaways from the book. One of the stories I keep telling is about the praying mantis and my son. I don’t know if that story resonated with you.

*It did, it was very funny. ChatGPT said it was pregnant, and it was dying.*

*JS:* Yes, my son has a praying mantis pet last summer and ChatGPT live view with video says that it’s definitely pregnant, my son gets super excited, even calls my dad and he’s like, “I’m going to be a grandpa, this thing’s going to have babies”, and then it dies like a few days later.

I think that was a really important lesson for him and it was an important lesson for me. We have to fact check everything that’s coming out of these and if we’re not using our own knowledge, we always have to remember that this thing is just not as certain as it says it is. I hope that’s a theme and lesson for readers of the book.

*You have to always be sharpening, your point, the brain can dull. That’s what you’re doing, like I said, I love the book, I thought it was great, it’s I’m Not A Robot, My Year Using AI To Do (Almost) Everything ( https://joannastern.com/#preorder ) , comes out May 12th, we’ll have a link in the show notes, the new site, New Things ( https://thenewthings.com/ ) with Joanna Stern. I agree, the art, the style is fantastic. I’m excited to see what you’re doing next.*

*JS:* Me too, I’ll take all the tips, Ben.

*We can talk later. Joanna Stern, great to talk to you again.*

*JS:* Thanks for having me.
