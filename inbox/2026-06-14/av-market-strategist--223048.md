👋 Welcome back everyone,
Europe spent years being told it had already lost the autonomy race. This week it answered. Not with a flashy deployment, but with the one thing it has actually been missing. Eighteen transport ministers sat down in Luxembourg and signed a plan to let an autonomous vehicle approved in one member state drive across the border into the next. That is not a robotaxi launch. For Europe, it is something more important: an attempt to fix the constraint that has kept every European pilot small.

Europe moves on the constraint that actually matters
Here is the thesis I want to carry through this whole section: Europe’s binding constraint was never the technology. It was fragmentation, the fact that an approval in one country buys you nothing in the next. This week gave us a wave of European activity, from London to Luxembourg to Bratislava, and the temptation is to read it as “Europe is finally doing stuff.” 
Here is the more honest read. Almost all of that activity is the usual pattern: individual pilots, one city or one country at a time. That is fragmentation in action, not a cure for it. Only one move this week actually targets the constraint itself, the Joint Declaration signed in Luxembourg. So the real story of the week is the contrast: a fresh pile of single-country pilots, alongside the first serious attempt to let an approval travel across borders. Hold that contrast in mind as we go.
London: Uber and Wayve open waitlist
Uber opened an interest list in the UK this week, letting riders raise their chances of being matched with a Wayve-powered autonomous vehicle. According to Wayve CEO Alex Kendall, Londoners can now sign up to experience autonomous rides on Uber, with services expected to launch “soon,” subject to final regulatory approval. Wayve provides the autonomy stack; Uber designed the in-cabin experience, including touchscreens supporting 64 languages. The vehicles will launch with a human safety operator behind the wheel before going fully driverless later.
Kaity Fischer, Vice President, Commercial and Operations at Wayve, didn’t hedge: “We’re ready to go, and can’t wait to get the public into our vehicles to experience Wayve technology first hand.” She called London’s roads the “ultimate testing ground for autonomous technology,” and the comparison she drew is the interesting part. Against San Francisco, the default robotaxi launch city, she put London at 20 times more roadworks and 10 times more vulnerable road users such as pedestrians and cyclists. Add 2,000-year-old streets that don’t follow a grid, with potholes and cobblestones, and you get what she called an “incredible proving ground.” London would be the first city in the world to carry commercial passengers on Wayve’s technology, with plans to expand to more than 10 cities globally with Uber, including Tokyo, later this year.
Two details matter more than the waitlist itself.
First, there is no dedicated autonomous tier. If you request a ride, you may be matched with a Wayve AV at no extra cost, and you can decline it for a human driver. You opt in through a preference setting, not a separate product. I’ll be honest about where I land: I don’t love it. We know from the Middle East, where Uber does run a dedicated autonomous option, that riders don’t want to play roulette over whether an AV shows up. A clear tier respects that. 
But the counter-argument deserves its due, and it comes down to one variable: how concentrated the AV market is. A dedicated AV tier only becomes a problem for Uber when a single provider dominates it. In most US cities, a dedicated AV tier would effectively be a Waymo tier, and that hands Waymo the brand. Riders start thinking “if I want an AV, I’ll just open the Waymo app,” and Uber has trained its own customers to route around it. 
That is the real reason Uber prefers to fold autonomy into “just another vehicle category,” and it is the same logic behind its 30-plus AV partnerships: it needs the market fragmented so that no single developer gains leverage. Flip the variable and the risk flips with it. In a genuinely fragmented market, where a dedicated tier could match you with five different providers, no single one gets elevated and the brand effect mostly washes out. There is probably a regulatory layer on top: with a safety driver still in the seat, you arguably can’t honestly market the ride as fully “autonomous” yet.
Second, and more interesting: Waymo is also in London. Since April it has been testing roughly 100 Jaguar I-Pace vehicles with safety operators across a 100-square-mile area. If both go commercial, Uber and Waymo become direct competitors in London, even as they remain partners in the US. We’ve been documenting the slow fraying of that US partnership for months; Harry Campbell ’s line from our podcast that “transactional relationships have a shorter expiration date” has aged well. London is where the fraying becomes visible. Wayve is, in effect, Uber’s hedge against the partner it is drifting away from. That is the story to watch this year.
One more London-adjacent note. Bloomberg reported that Wayve, last valued at $8.6 billion in February with Mercedes-Benz, Nvidia and Uber among its backers, is considering letting investors sell shares through PISCES, the UK’s new regulated private-stock market operated by the London Stock Exchange Group. It would be the largest company yet to use the framework. The interesting part isn’t the liquidity event. It’s why the UK built PISCES in the first place: to stop its best private tech companies from drifting to a US listing. Wayve testing the system is the sovereignty-and-capital-retention play in miniature, Britain trying to keep its champion at home.
Luxembourg, part one: Bolt, Pony AI and Stellantis launch a Living Lab
Bolt, the European-founded ride-hailing platform, launched its first autonomous pilot, partnering with Pony AI and Stellantis in Luxembourg. The setup: Pony’s seventh-generation (Gen-7) autonomous system, running on a Stellantis midsize van built on the automaker’s L4-Ready platform, integrated into Bolt’s ride-hailing app. 
Source: Bolt
Worth stating: the vehicles are not driverless yet. The pilot starts with five vehicles, and full driverless readiness is the goal for the end of the testing program, not the start. Founder Markus Villig framed it as Europe wanting to be at the forefront of scaling the technology; Pony CEO James Peng pointed to Luxembourg’s accommodating regulatory environment.
Bolt is explicit that this is a continent-wide strategy, and that the pilot is the first concrete step toward it. As the company put it, to get all this off the ground, they had to start somewhere. The longer-term ambition is to access the more complex areas of city centres and to offer autonomous driving services to airport passengers, not just run a closed loop in a quiet zone. The way they intend to get there is deliberately incremental: expand one operational design domain (ODD) at a time, proving the service in each environment before unlocking the next. It is designed as a public-facing living lab, a real-world test of everything a driverless ride-hailing service needs beyond the software itself: vehicle deployment, platform integration, fleet operations, and coordination with regulators.
I was at the launch event in Luxembourg, so let me add a couple of things that weren’t in the press release.
Source: Bolt
The scaling logic is built around remote-operator economics. The plan starts with those five vehicles and works toward roughly 30, and that number isn’t arbitrary. It maps to a target remote-operator ratio of about 1:30, the benchmark at which unit economics genuinely start to work, which doesn’t mean that there is no business case with lower ratios. 
The honest framing I picked up on-site: this is a long-term goal, not a one-year promise, because only a few AV providers anywhere are able to operate at a 1:30 ratio today. The path runs in deliberate steps: safety driver in the driver’s seat, then safety driver in the passenger seat, same for remote support at 1:1, then 1:2, and onward toward 1:30. Each step up the ratio improves the economics; 1:30 is simply where they get genuinely compelling. 
There is a fleet-utilization angle to the same number, too: run a fleet of roughly 30 vehicles in double shifts and you start putting serious daily mileage on each car, and that utilization is what produces both the operating data and the revenue a business case rests on. 
And there is a second, quieter reason the target gets stated out loud: it is a tool to convince European cities to permit fleets larger than the five-to-ten vehicles that have typically been the ceiling here. “Let us run five, evaluate, then scale” is easier to approve than “let us run thirty.” The number is as much a regulatory lever as an operational one.
Luxembourg, part two: 18 ministers sign the thing Europe has actually been missing
On 8 June in Luxembourg, transport ministers from 18 EU member states signed a Joint Declaration of Intent to create a cross-border testbed for automated vehicles, chaired by Executive Vice President Stéphane Séjourné and Transport Commissioner Apostolos Tzitzikostas. 
The Joint Declaration was signed by Austria, Belgium, Croatia, Cyprus, Czechia, Estonia, Finland, France, Germany, Greece, Hungary, Ireland, Italy, Lithuania, Luxembourg, the Netherlands, Poland, and Sweden. 
Strip away the diplomatic language and here is what they committed to strive toward: a common set of technical requirements for approval (built on existing EU type-approval regulation), mutual recognition of approved vehicles across the testbed, a single authority responsible for a given vehicle type’s approval, and the headline, easing “commercial operations without a safety driver” across borders. 
Source: European Commission / Joint Declaration of Intent 
There are also sovereignty strings: a preference for EU-made vehicles and components, a requirement that non-EU players partner with an EU stakeholder that has a “meaningful footprint,” and an expectation that testbed data stays inside the EU.
There are two ways to read this, and being explicit about both is the honest thing to do.
Read optimistically: this is the first coordinated move at the precise pressure point. The thing holding European AV back has not been the ability to build or validate a safe vehicle. It has been that a permit won in one city or country dies at the border, so no operator can ever assemble the multi-market footprint that fleet economics require. You cannot commercialize across a continent when every jurisdiction is its own island. Mutual recognition and cross-border driver-out operation are exactly the locks this declaration is trying to pick, and they are the right locks.
Read skeptically: it is a Declaration of Intent. The text says so itself. It is explicitly not a treaty, creates no rights or obligations, and can be amended or abandoned at any time. Europe does not have a shortage of non-binding wishlists signed by ministers. The verbs throughout are “will strive to,” “will seek to,” “should aim to.” None of that is a mechanism. 
So where do I land? I lean toward the optimistic read, but I hold it loosely. I lean that way because fragmentation is the right target, and most European AV interventions aim at the wrong problem while this one does not. I hold it loosely because intent is the cheap part. The threshold that would move me from “promising signal” to “genuine turning point” is concrete: the first vehicle type-approved in one member state and accepted by another without retesting, operating driver-out. Until then, this is the most encouraging piece of paper European AV has produced this year, and still only a piece of paper.
This is also the moment to take on the most common criticism of European AV, because it is at least half right. The argument goes like this: Europe doesn’t have a pilot problem, it has a commercialization problem. Lots of “firsts,” lots of announcements, minimal fleets, very limited commercial scale, while the US and China are already in the hard part, paid driver-out rides across large service areas under real unit-economics pressure. 
Augustin Friedel  made a sharp version of this case in a recent LinkedIn post, and honestly, it is legit. The scoreboard backs him up. The question is whether anything changes it. 
Source: Augustin Friedel
This is where the declaration matters, at least on paper. Its “ease of commercial operations without a safety driver” clause is aimed squarely at the commercialization gap Friedel describes, because that gap is downstream of fragmentation: you can’t run commercial driver-out services at scale when every border resets the permit. So the declaration is a plausible answer to the critique. The caveat is the obvious one. It only counts if it actually happens, if intent turns into approvals that travel. Signed today, it changes nothing this quarter. Executed over the next two years, it could change the very thing Friedel is rightly pointing at.
Europe wasn’t done yet: Switzerland’s PostBus moves toward passenger service
The Swiss Federal Roads Office (FEDRO) granted PostBus Switzerland a special permit for its self-driving operation across an 80-square-kilometre area covering St Gallen and the two Appenzell cantons. Since 1 June, the vehicles have been running “hands-free,” meaning the safety driver on board may take their hands off the wheel, with operators monitoring from a control centre and able to intervene. The forward-looking part is the milestone that matters: from 2027, the “AmiGo” vehicles are set to carry passengers, with regular operations of up to 25 vehicles without a safety driver planned for that year. The autonomy comes from Apollo Go, Baidu’s robotaxi arm, running at Level 4 once fully deployed.
If you want the texture behind this one, go back to my Autonomy Insiders conversation with Martin Neubauer, Head of Autonomous Mobility at PostAuto, on the AmiGo project [ https://substack.com/redirect/d831aa56-f167-4ba1-b90b-a35bc8e0f0d1?j=eyJ1IjoiMXlnZjcifQ.KOUIeBfeCRzhIwFnKXyQigR4j0VpF-SZzascYhSZunw ]. 
Slovakia: WeRide’s beachhead, and the wall it still hits
WeRide outlined a Level 4 rollout plan for Slovakia after a meeting with Prime Minister Robert Fico in Bratislava, with government backing. 
Source: WeRide
First vehicles are expected this month, starting in Bratislava before expanding to Košice and the High Tatras. The deployment is unusually broad, covering smart mobility, public transport, logistics and municipal sanitation, under a national initiative called ELEVATE Slovakia, with local partner DiusAI. Selected models are expected in public commercial operation in 2026, ahead of a fully driverless launch subject to Slovak and EU approval. Slovakia becomes WeRide’s fifth European country, alongside France, Switzerland, Belgium and Spain.
Two things to flag. First, read this as a strategic entry point rather than a volume market. A small EU member that moves quickly on permits is worth more to WeRide right now than a large one that moves slowly, and the guest list (Prime Minister, Transport Minister, Chinese Ambassador the same day) shows how much of the work here is the regulatory and diplomatic groundwork that later deployment depends on. It also feeds straight into the data-sovereignty questions the Joint Declaration above is trying to pre-empt. Second, the honest caveat: “commercial operations in 2026 ahead of fully driverless” still runs into the same wall everyone else does. No type-approved L4 vehicle exists anywhere yet. Even China’s well-funded European push has to clear European homologation, and that timeline doesn’t bend for anyone.
To stay with WeRide for a moment, it also moved in the Gulf this week. Jameel Motors announced a collaboration to deploy WeRide’s Robotaxi GXR fleet in the UAE, with Jameel investing in and financing the fleet through Farizon Auto electric vehicles, the commercial-vehicle arm of Geely. 
WeRide supplies the autonomous driving technology; Jameel supplies the capital and the local operational, regulatory and platform support. This is the asset-light model in its purest form: WeRide scales without owning the vehicles, and a deep-pocketed local partner carries the balance-sheet weight. 
The context is what makes it significant. WeRide has been running fully driverless commercial robotaxis in Dubai (Jumeirah and Umm Suqeim) since 31 March, after moving from supervised trials in December to a driverless permit in February, and it now operates close to 250 robotaxis across the Middle East. Dubai wants 25% of all journeys autonomous by 2030. So the Jameel deal is not a pilot announcement. It is the financing layer for a market that is already commercial, and it is the clearest sign yet that asset-light, paired with a local capital partner, is how the Chinese players intend to scale abroad.
One last European item, in an unusually European week
Portugal will allow autonomous vehicles to be tested on public roads from July, under a decree published. The framework is strict: testing is licensed, compulsory insurance is set at four times the usual minimum, operators must submit a risk-mitigation plan and demonstrate cybersecurity measures, and tests must be notified in advance with a report filed afterward. As a Portuguese, I’ll admit it is nice to see my own country on this list. It is a small move, a testing decree rather than a deployment, but it is another data point in the same direction.
What this means and what to watch
This was the week Europe started addressing the precondition for deployment, not just deployment itself. London showed the demand layer, Luxembourg showed both the commercial structure (Bolt, Pony AI, Stellantis) and the regulatory ambition (the Joint Declaration), and Switzerland and Slovakia showed the cautious, transit-and-government-led path Europe actually runs. But be precise about what was what: most of these were single-country pilots, the very pattern that keeps Europe sub-scale. None of them changes the scoreboard against the US and China this quarter. The one move that could change the trajectory is the declaration, and only if intent becomes mechanism.
Two things to watch from here. First, the European signal that matters is recognition, not announcement. The Luxembourg declaration only counts if it produces a single vehicle approved in one member state and accepted in another without retesting, operating driver-out. Watch for the first cross-border approval, not the next pilot launch. Second, London is where the Uber-Waymo partnership gets tested for real: partners in the US, competitors in the UK, with Wayve as Uber’s hedge. If both go commercial there this year, the strain we’ve tracked for months becomes a public contest, and that will tell us more about the future of the ride-hail-plus-AV model than any single deployment.

Waymo builds the moat, Tesla builds the narrative
No dedicated China Corner this week. Europe earned the space. But the US gave us a clean contrast between two strategies: one company spending on operational depth, the other spending on optionality and story.
Waymo buys Apple’s proving ground
Waymo acquired a 5,500-acre proving ground in Arizona for $220 million, the closed test course Apple bought for $125 million in 2021 for its now-defunct Project Titan, and which Fiat Chrysler used before that. It includes a 115-acre city course, a four-mile oval, a vehicle-dynamics area and a purpose-built freeway course, and it dwarfs Waymo’s existing facilities in California and Ohio. Waymo says it will use it for rider-only testing, motion-control testing and operational training as it scales toward a fleet of tens of thousands.
The “so what” is twofold. Operationally, this is a move into the unsexy infrastructure of scale, the controlled environment you need to validate new vehicle platforms (the Zeekr van, the Hyundai Ioniq 5) before they hit public roads. Symbolically, it is hard to miss: Apple spent years and billions on autonomy, gave up, and Waymo just bought the field it left behind. The asset that was a monument to one company’s exit becomes infrastructure for another’s expansion.
Waymo’s loyalty program, and why I think it’s smart
Waymo launched Waymo Premier, a roughly $30/month tier offering priority pickups, 10% cash back on every trip, early access in new cities, and up to five free cancellations a month, initially in San Francisco, LA and Phoenix. Harry Campbell ’s read is that priority pickup is the real draw and the move is a smart way to segment customers, and I see it the same way. The deeper point is structural: Waymo has fewer supply-and-demand levers than Uber or Lyft, because it can’t summon more human drivers with incentives during a demand spike. A subscription does two things at once: it prioritizes the most loyal riders and creates a recurring revenue line that smooths the brutal capital intensity of a self-owned fleet. For a company that owns its cars, recurring revenue is not a nice-to-have. It is a margin lifeline.
Waymo’s new benchmark: transparency as a weapon, with an asterisk
Waymo, with TU Delft, published a paper in Nature Communications introducing a new model of human driving behavior it calls the Reference Driver, built on “active inference,” the idea that a careful, competent human driver is constantly simulating possible futures and steering toward the safest one. The purpose is to give the industry a more realistic behavioral benchmark for grading how an AV performs against a good human in conflict scenarios.
This fits a thesis I’ve been building for a while: in this phase of the industry, transparency is becoming a competitive weapon. The company that defines the safety benchmark shapes the regulatory conversation. That said, and this is the counterweight, there is an obvious tension in a company publishing the benchmark by which its own vehicles will be judged. Waymo previously used its prior model to argue that in the Santa Monica case where one of its vehicles struck a child, an attentive human would have hit the child at around 14 mph versus the Waymo’s 6 mph after braking. That incident is still under federal investigation, and I would treat any company grading its own homework with appropriate caution. Both things can be true: a better behavioral model genuinely advances the field, and it is not neutral that Waymo is the one holding the pen. I would want to see independent bodies adopt or challenge the Reference Driver before treating it as the industry standard.
Tesla files for 5,000 robotaxis in Las Vegas. Look at the other number.
Tesla applied to Nevada regulators for an Autonomous Vehicle Network Company permit to operate up to 5,000 robotaxis across Clark County, Las Vegas and its airports within the first 12 months. For scale, that is against roughly 6,000 to 7,000 Uber and Lyft vehicles serving the area today. It is one of Tesla’s largest AV proposals yet.
Here is the number that matters more: Tesla’s actual unsupervised fleet in Austin is estimated at 20 to 25 vehicles, and reportedly contracting, even as it expanded its Austin geofence to the full 245-square-mile metro. So we have a company simultaneously expanding its map, shrinking its fleet, and filing for permission to run 5,000 vehicles in a market where it operates none. 
The Road to Autonomy team read this the way I do: a 5,000-vehicle filing is a permit ceiling, not an operational commitment. You do not have to deploy what you are approved for. It is a future-looking market-saturation signal, paired with an inverted playbook. Where the conventional rollout scales vehicles, geofence and operations together, Tesla is securing regulatory and operational optionality first, betting that the software arrives to fill it later. To be fair, that is a legitimate strategy, not a con. But it is a coverage narrative, and the gap between the filing and the fleet is the whole story. 
Tesla FSD clears Denmark, and a skeptic flips
Denmark provisionally approved Tesla’s FSD (Supervised), becoming the fourth European country to clear it in roughly eight weeks, after the Netherlands, Lithuania and Estonia. The mechanism is the one we’ve been tracking: Denmark accepted the original Dutch RDW type approval through national recognition, bypassing the slower EU-wide path, though it stressed it ran its own independent review first, and was clear that the system does not make the car self-driving.
What makes Denmark notable is that it is a converted skeptic. Denmark was previously among the Nordic states, with Sweden, Finland and Norway, that raised objections to FSD at the EU level, citing its tendency to exceed speed limits, its performance on ice, and whether the “Full Self-Driving” name oversells the capability. For one of those skeptics to grant national recognition anyway is a real signal: the documentation was good enough to move a doubter. Tesla deserves credit for that.
But the same mechanism that is winning Tesla fast approvals is also the fragility. National recognition compresses approval from years to weeks, and it builds a patchwork, not a single market. There is still no EU-wide approval. If the EU Commission ultimately rejects the system, the Dutch provisional approval becomes invalid after six months, and the Danish clearance falls with it. FSD Supervised would then no longer be permitted for sale in any EU country.
The Musk-universe item: another hint at a SpaceX-Tesla merger
I’ve flagged in previous editions that a Tesla-SpaceX merger is worth taking seriously, and that the pieces of Musk’s empire tend to get pulled together over time. I won’t relitigate that case here. What is new this week is the evidence, and it is no longer just speculation.
Start with the IPO itself. SpaceX debuted more than 30% above its $135 offering price, valuing the company at roughly $2 trillion, which makes it worth more than Meta or Saudi Aramco. With the listing live, SpaceX President and COO Gwynne Shotwell told CNBC that a merger “might make Elon’s life a little easier.” More concretely, SpaceX amended its S-1 to warn that it “may issue a significant amount of equity in connection with future transactions,” the kind of dilution language you do not write for a small deal.
Then there is Alexandra Merz, a long-time Tesla investor and CEO of L&F Investor Services, who runs the @TeslaBoomerMama account followed by nearly 240,000 people. She told Bloomberg Television she deliberately skipped the SpaceX IPO because she expects the two companies to combine: “My thesis is that Tesla and SpaceX will merge. As an all-in Tesla investor I would have had to sell Tesla, which I was not going to do. So my thesis is I am going to hold these and in a couple of short weeks, if I am right, they will announce the merger that will then be consummated in the first half of 2027.” 
She is not alone. SpaceX backer Peter Diamandis has argued that Musk’s desire for control over his companies would push a tie-up, the same logic behind folding xAI into SpaceX this year. The betting lines have moved with the narrative: a Kalshi contract on a merger announcement before May 2027 trades around 55%, up from 26% earlier this year, and Wedbush’s Dan Ives now puts the odds at 80% to 90% by the first half of 2027. The caveat the bulls tend to skip: such a combination could be around 28% dilutive to Tesla shareholders.
What this means and what to watch
The US gave us the cleanest version of the contrast we keep returning to. Waymo is spending on depth, on a proving ground, a loyalty engine and a safety benchmark, the operational layer that compounds. Tesla is spending on breadth and optionality, on geofences, permits and narrative, betting the software catches up. Both are coherent strategies. Only one of them is currently moving paying passengers at scale.
Two threads to watch. First, transparency is hardening into a competitive weapon, and the question is who holds the pen. Waymo defining the safety benchmark is an advantage only if independent bodies adopt it; if they do not, it is a company grading its own homework. The players that open their data to outside scrutiny will earn regulatory trust, the ones who define the test themselves will earn skepticism. Second, watch the two answers to fragmentation diverge: Tesla’s national-recognition sprint is fast and fragile, Europe’s harmonization push is slow and durable. Over the next year we will learn which one actually delivers a single market, and whether they collide.
