Wayve wins second OEM, Waymo’s bad week, and another FSD Unsupervised Promise from Elon
Wayve and Stellantis: the second OEM lands
Wayve announced on May 21 that Stellantis will integrate the Wayve AI Driver into the STLA AutoDrive platform. The first vehicle hits North America in 2028, targeting hands-free Level 2++ on both highways and city streets. Wayve says it can stand up a prototype on a new Stellantis platform in under two months.
Source: Wayve
This is Wayve’s second OEM deal. The first was Nissan, announced earlier this year for ProPilot at Level 2+ in 2027 production vehicles. Stellantis is a volume opportunity. Jeep, Ram, Dodge, Chrysler, Fiat, Peugeot. 
The architectural angle matters. Wayve’s pitch is hardware-agnostic, the self-driving system isn’t tied to particular sensors, chips, or high-definition maps. This is a compelling value proposition for cost-sensitive automakers. The system learns from real-world driving rather than being hand-coded to follow rules. 
Read the Wayve deal in isolation and you miss the bigger story. Stellantis is now running parallel AV bets. In October 2025, Stellantis signed a nonbinding agreement with Pony.ai to develop robotaxis for Europe. The collaboration integrates Pony's L4 self-driving software into Stellantis' electric medium-size van platform, starting with test vehicles based on the Peugeot e-Traveller. The same week as the Wayve announcement, Stellantis also extended its partnership with Applied Intuition, building on their existing work on STLA SmartCockpit to support the development of the next generation of Stellantis’ STLA Brain intelligent vehicle platform. 
So the Wayve deal is L2++ for now, but Wayve’s ambitions go well beyond hands-free highway. Wayve is launching an L4 robotaxi service with Uber in London later this year, with plans to scale to more than 10 markets. Worth noting that Wayve’s $1.2B Series D in February counted Mercedes-Benz, Nissan, and Stellantis itself as investors. Stellantis was already a Wayve shareholder before signing this OEM product deal.
Pull the strategy together and the hedge is obvious. Pony.ai gives Stellantis a proven L4 robotaxi stack for Europe in the short term, on a Chinese player that has commercial driverless miles on the board. Wayve gives Stellantis a European-headquartered AI partner whose unified software architecture can scale from L2++ in 2028 production vehicles to L4 robotaxis over time, with the consumer launch as the platform familiarization on-ramp.
Which brings us back to Wayve's L4 robotaxi service in London. The UK opened the regulatory door this week.
London opens the door for AV
On May 15, the UK's Automated Vehicles (Permits for Automated Passenger Services) Regulations 2026 came into force. The Driver and Vehicle Standards Agency (DVSA), acting on behalf of the Department for Transport, started accepting applications from operators that want to run autonomous taxis, buses, and private-hire vehicles. Pilots can launch this spring, wider deployment from 2027.
This is one of the biggest regulatory unlocks of the year. London is one of the largest taxi markets in the world. The city is denser and more complex than any market where US AVs operate at scale today. And it is the first major Western city where US, UK, and Chinese AVs will compete head-to-head.
Confirmed players in the queue: Wayve (on Uber), Waymo (on Waymo), and Baidu Apollo Go (on Uber & Lyft). 
The permit process has a real consent gate. If a service looks like a taxi or PHV, each licensing authority in whose area the service runs must give consent. The six-week clock only handles silence: if the authority does not respond, consent is deemed granted. But if it actively refuses, no permit. In London that means TfL holds a real veto on the city, not a soft buffer. The DVSA writes the permit, but the local licensing authority decides whether the service can actually run in its area.
London is split: Westminster pro-jobs, TfL skeptical
The political picture in the UK is not one-sided. Westminster has been the loudest pro-AV voice, framing the regulations as a jobs and investment story. The government has said operators will create “thousands of jobs” by bringing AVs to Britain’s roads. The pitch is productivity, mobility access, and tech leadership in a market where the UK has a real domestic player in Wayve.
TfL and City Hall sit on the other side. Sir Sadiq Khan’s spokesperson says the mayor wants to harness the technology while protecting jobs and reducing congestion. Seb Dance, deputy mayor for transport, called AV technology “unproven” and said “an awful lot of work” remains to determine whether AVs would deliver a net safety benefit. Andy Lord, the TfL transport commissioner, said the new operators would have to meet current regulatory requirements. The FT also reports TfL has begun asking more detailed questions in recent talks with operators that are anxious to launch this year.
The Harlesden incident from last week did not help. A Waymo vehicle went through a live police cordon during an investigation of a double stabbing. Waymo says the car was being driven manually by a validation driver, who has since been suspended. The Brent Green Party started a petition (about 300 signatures so far). The App Drivers and Couriers Union ran a protest in Brent against driverless cars.
I’m not a regulatory expert, but I think the split matters because, in my reading, TfL holds a hard consent power for taxi-like services, as flagged in the section above. Westminster can write the regulations and DVSA can process the permits, but London's licensing authority can still refuse consent.
Waymo's bad week
Start with the flooding. Waymo paused operations in Atlanta, Dallas, Houston, San Antonio, after vehicles drove into floodwater. As the week closed, the company extended the pause to Austin and Nashville too. That's six cities. The root cause has been on the table for weeks. 
On May 12, Waymo filed a voluntary recall with NHTSA covering all 3,791 vehicles in the fleet, both fifth- and sixth-generation, after an unoccupied robotaxi was swept away in San Antonio on April 20. 
The interim fix was an ODD restriction: exclude high-flood-risk areas and times. Critically, that exclusion depended on National Weather Service flash flood warnings to trigger. May storms hit several cities before NWS issued the warnings, so the workaround did not engage in time. NHTSA notes Waymo is still "developing the final remedy."
The recall language flags higher driving speeds as a contributing factor, which points to a deeper problem: the robotaxi does not recognize a flood as a flood until it is too late to stop. Cameras struggle with water depth. LiDAR sees a flat surface. There is no quick technical fix for this class of edge case.
Source: X/Sean Keenan
Separately, Waymo paused freeway service in San Francisco, Los Angeles, Phoenix, and Miami. The cause is construction zones. 
Phil Koopman has more details in his article (featured in the Worth Reading List). Two days before the suspension, a passenger publicly reported a Waymo "freaking out" and accelerating to highway speeds through a construction zone during a chaotic merge, allegedly with police pursuing. Waymo's official statement framed the pause around “recent technical learnings into our software and expect to resume these routes soon.” 
Two pauses in one week. Waymo's stated goal is one million paid rides per week by the end of 2026. The gating constraint is not capital, not regulatory clearance, not consumer demand. It is reliability at the edges.
The brand-damage question deserves to be asked plainly. Waymo trained riders to use it. If you live in San Francisco and you've started routing the airport run through a freeway-capable Waymo, losing that capability with little notice is not a neutral event. Same for the Atlanta or Austin user who learned to count on it. Each pause is, mechanically, a downgrade in service quality. Conditional availability is a different product from the full thing, and customers notice.
The pattern underneath the headlines is that Waymo fixes are not sticking. The flood ODD-restriction fix failed within weeks. Two prior school-bus fixes failed after NHTSA recalls, and the case is now under NTSB investigation. CMU safety expert Phil Koopman calls this out directly: a simple "trust us, it's fixed" has lost credibility, and Waymo needs to "ease up on their scale-up throttle while they get their house in order." With thousands of vehicles in many cities, incident frequency is no longer a growing-pain anomaly. Edge cases are the new normal at scale. As Koopman puts it, "scaling is the other 99% of the work."
There is also a narrative cost. The Waymo story for the last few months has been about scale: more cities, more rides, more fleet. Two simultaneous pauses don’t support that scaling story very well. That may have implications for unit economics, for capital efficiency, and for how investors model the path to one million rides per week.
Tesla: a promise, a shortcut, and a hiring drive
Three Tesla moves in one week. 
First, on May 18, Musk told the Smart Mobility Summit in Tel Aviv that unsupervised FSD will be “widespread in the US by the end of this year.” This came less than a month after the Q1 earnings call where Musk said unsupervised consumer FSD now slips to Q4 2026 “at the earliest”
Today Tesla operates 39 unsupervised robotaxis across three Texas cities, according to Robotaxi Tracker. Going from <40 to "widespread" in seven months would require fleet growth and operational scaling that nothing in the data supports.
Track the pattern, not the headline. Musk has been promising imminent FSD for a decade. Every prediction has slipped. Every new prediction frames the slip as a temporary detour. The Tel Aviv claim is the same script. The Q1 admission was the rare honest version. Now the script has been rewound.
Here are some of Musk’s FSD promises in case you can’t remember:
2015: Musk predicted, “Tesla vehicles will drive themselves with full autonomy within two years”.
2018: On a shareholder call, Musk promised a cross-country fully autonomous ride within “three to six months.”
2019: Musk told investors Tesla would have “over a million robotaxis on the road” by 2020 and that FSD would be “feature complete” by year-end.
January 2025: Musk promised unsupervised FSD would launch by June 2025.
June 2025: The robotaxi service launched in Austin — but with safety monitors inside the vehicles, not unsupervised.
July 2025: “I think we'll probably have autonomous ride hailing in probably half the population of the U.S. by the end of the year”
October  2025: “Tesla will have 500 Robotaxis in Austin by the end of the year”
January 2026: Musk moved the goalpost again, saying Tesla needed 10 billion miles of data for safe unsupervised driving — up from the 6 billion he had previously cited.
Second, Tesla launched FSD Supervised in Lithuania. Lithuania is now the 9th country globally and the 2nd in Europe after the Netherlands.
Source: X/Tesla
The interesting part is not Lithuania. The interesting part is that Lithuania did not run its own approval process. It accepted the Dutch RDW certification under EU mutual recognition rules. The Netherlands approved FSD on April 10 after 18 months of testing. Five weeks later, the second EU country was activated without retesting.
This is the actual regulatory story for Tesla in Europe. EU mutual recognition compresses the per-country approval cycle from years to weeks. Every EU member state can in principle do what Lithuania just did. Tesla’s European footprint expands at a much faster cadence than country-by-country approvals would have allowed.
The pipeline is worth watching closely, but the timeline is messier than Tesla's communications suggest. Tesla itself projects Germany (via KBA), France, and Italy to issue national recognitions within four to eight weeks of the Dutch approval. KBA reportedly may require additional high-speed Autobahn testing above 130 km/h to cover phantom braking and lane-keeping at speed, which could add months. Greece's transport ministry has said an upcoming bill will grant approval, putting Greece in line to be the third European country. Belgium is expected to follow the RDW recognition route. Ireland's Department of Transport has confirmed Tesla is actively engaging with the National Standards Authority of Ireland. Italy is cautious and looks likely to wait for the EU-wide vote.
The Scandinavian read is different. Regulators in Sweden, Finland, Denmark, and Norway have publicly raised concerns about FSD's tendency to exceed speed limits and its performance on icy roads.
EU-wide approval is the other lever. That requires a qualified-majority vote in the Technical Committee on Motor Vehicles (TCMV): 15 of 27 member states representing 65% of the EU population. The 117th TCMV meeting on May 5 did not vote. The RDW presented its Article 39 file in a one-hour session and national authorities are now reviewing. The earliest realistic vote is the next TCMV session on June 30, with after-summer or the provisional October meeting being more likely. Whether Tesla gets country-by-country mutual recognitions first or a TCMV-driven EU-wide approval second will shape both the rollout pace and which national markets matter for the FSD subscription P&L.
Third, on May 21 Tesla officially confirmed FSD Supervised availability for the China market. Same day, Bloomberg reported "urgent" job postings for Autopilot test engineers, data labelers, and real-test operators across nine Chinese cities. Beijing, Shanghai, Tianjin, Wuhan, Guangzhou, Chongqing, Chengdu, Shenzhen, and others. Tesla previously targeted a nationwide rollout by February. That target has now slipped to Q3 2026.
And to be completely clear, even though I suspect every reader of this newsletter already knows: FSD Supervised is still an L2+ system, not L4. The driver remains responsible at all times. Everything in this section is about Tesla unlocking an L2+ subscription product in new markets, not about unsupervised autonomy. 
What to watch 
Waymo's scaling trajectory and the news drumbeat. Watch how quickly freeway service comes back, whether the next flooding incident triggers a third pause. The bigger question is whether the one-million-paid-rides-per-week target for end of 2026 holds. The leading indicator is the news cycle itself. If flood, freeway, school-bus, and police-scene edge cases keep landing in headlines every few weeks, the brand erodes independently of how the cars perform on aggregate safety metrics. Reliability at the edges is now a marketing exposure, not just an engineering one.
Wayve's London launch, the third OEM, and how TfL exercises its consent power. The London L4 launch is the credibility moment for Wayve. A third OEM (Mercedes is the obvious watch) would validate the supplier model even more. The London regulatory layer is its own watch item. TfL has real consent power on taxi and PHV-like services, and the political posture so far has been skeptical even as Westminster pushes the pro-jobs framing. Watch whether TfL grants, slow-walks, or actively refuses initial operator permits, and whether early incidents push the licensing authority to attach tighter conditions. That outcome shapes whether London becomes a flagship Western AV market in 2026 or a delayed one.
Tesla, watch which earnings call walks back the "widespread unsupervised FSD by year-end" line. Base case has to be another slip. Going from fewer than 40 unsupervised robotaxis today to "widespread" by December would require fleet growth and operational scaling that nothing in the data supports, and Musk's decade-long pattern is that these timelines slip. The signal to watch is the language in Q2 and Q3 earnings calls and how the Texas footprint actually expands to new metros.

CHINA: Apollo Go scales, WeRide removes front-row seat safety driver in Zurich, XPeng joins the race vision-only
Baidu Apollo Go’s Q1 disclosure
Apollo Go delivered 3.2 million driverless rides in Q1 2026. 
That’s up more than 120% year over year. Weekly rides peaked above 350,000 in March, roughly 70% of the 500,000 weekly rides Waymo provides. Cumulative autonomous kilometers crossed 330 million, with 220 million of those fully driverless. Apollo Go is now in 27 cities globally. Cumulative public rides exceed 22 million as of April.
WeRide Zurich removes the front-seat safety driver
WeRide's robobus at Zurich Airport began operating without a front-row safety driver in May 2026. The vehicle is now overseen by remote operations teams and a trained observer in the rear seat. The robobus has been running at the airport since early 2025.
This is a step forward for WeRide to operate without a driver in the front in Switzerland. Worth flagging though, that a trained observer in the rear is still a safety driver. The same critique most industry observers made when Tesla launched its robotaxi service with a safety monitor in the passenger seat applies here too. Moving the human out of the driver's seat is not the same thing as L4. If a human is in the vehicle and can intervene, that is still a supervised operation. This is a meaningful operational step, but it is not fully driverless.
Where I do separate the WeRide case from the Tesla critique is on capability. WeRide has already demonstrated true driverless L4 operation (no safety driver) in multiple cities, across China and the UAE. So the supervisor in the back at Zurich is a regulatory and operational caution, not a sign that WeRide cannot remove the human. The constraint is the Swiss regulator getting comfortable, not the stack. My base case is that WeRide moves to full driver-out in Switzerland in the coming quarters.
XPeng's first mass-produced robotaxi
On May 18, XPeng rolled the first mass-produced robotaxi off its production line in Guangzhou. Built on the XPeng GX platform, powered by four self-developed Turing AI chips delivering 3,000 TOPS. Vision-only. No LiDAR. No HD maps. Runs on XPeng's VLA 2.0 end-to-end AI model with system response latency under 80 milliseconds.
Source: Gasgoo
The architectural bet is the Tesla bet, executed by a vertically integrated Chinese automaker. XPeng makes the vehicle (GX platform), the chips, and the software (VLA 2.0). If the vision-only architecture works at scale, manufacturing throughput becomes the only structural constraint.
The hard truth that needs to be in any honest read: XPeng is three years behind the leaders (Baidu, WeRide, Pony.ai) on operational data. No driverless operations have started yet. Pilot operations are slated for the second half of 2026. Fully driverless target is early 2027. Chinese regulators have not yet approved any vision-only L4 robotaxi, and L4 testing permits are currently paused after the Baidu freezing incident in March. XPeng was grandfathered in via a permit granted in January.
The Europe angle is hard to ignore. Volkswagen is a minority investor in XPeng. International press has reported VW and XPeng are in talks to use a European VW factory to support XPeng’s manufacturing push. If that lands, XPeng goes from a China-only player to a transatlantic challenger.
The bigger pivot, and why everyone is showing up to it in 2026
XPeng’s robotaxi launch is not happening in a vacuum. It is part of a coordinated industry-wide pivot toward robotaxi. 
Who is showing up. The list of new entrants in the last few months reads like an industry-wide repositioning. On the OEM side: XPeng with the mass-produced GX-platform vehicle, and Geely with the Eva Cab prototype unveiled at Auto China 2026. Eva Cab is notable because it is “subtractive” rather than “additive”. No steering wheel, no accelerator, no brake pedal. Designed for robotaxi from the chassis up. 
On the supplier side, Yihang Intelligence showed its first robotaxi prototype at Auto China. SenseAuto is partnering with T3 Go. Lenovo is partnering with WeRide. Desay SV and ECARX are both moving. The most telling moves are Momenta and DeepRoute.ai, both former L4 players that pivoted to mass-production ADAS when the L4 sector cooled around 2022. Both are now back. Companies with active ADAS revenue and OEM cash flow do not casually shift focus back to L4 unless they think the timing has changed.
Why now. Three things converged in 2026. First, the technology route. The "progressive" L2-to-L4 path is now being framed as proven viable by the L2 leaders entering the L4 game. Yihang Intelligence's CEO Chen Yuhang puts it plainly: localized robotaxi operation in specific cities allows existing autonomous capabilities to form a commercial closed loop in a controlled area, where nationwide driverless still requires long-term work.
Worth pausing on this. In past episodes, both the WeRide and Pony.ai CEOs argued the opposite, that L2 and L4 are fundamentally different problems and you cannot just scale one into the other. Today’s quotes are sourced largely from Tier 1 supplier executives who are entering the L4 game, leaning the other way. As a critical analyst, the honest read is that every CEO making either claim is talking their book. WeRide saying “you cannot just scale L2 to L4” advantages WeRide, which already operates L4. Yihang or Momenta saying “you can scale” advantages the ADAS leaders that need the entry argument to hold.
The on-the-ground evidence is mixed. WeRide is the clearest proof point that one company can do both. Its L4 robotaxis run driverless in eight countries, and its WRD 3.0 ADAS just took its fifth consecutive championship at the China Urban Intelligent Driving Competition with Chery’s Exeed Sterra ES in May. 
Source: LinkedIn/WeRide
Notably, WeRide says WRD 3.0 is trained on its L4 real-world road data via the GENESIS simulation world model. That points to a unified architecture, but in the direction the L2 leaders do not love to talk about: L4 data feeding L2 quality, not the other way around. XPeng, on the other side, has shipped mass-production ADAS at scale but has not yet demonstrated driverless L4 operation. The pilot does not start until the second half of this year and full driverless is targeted for early 2027. If the progressive thesis were already fully proven, you would expect more L2 leaders to have shipped L4. So far, the converse case (the L4 leader that also wins L2 benchmarks) is the cleaner story. That said, the WeRide CEO would have an incentive to frame it that way too, so weight accordingly. 
Second, the cost curve bent down hard. The single biggest input is LiDAR. Around 2020, a mechanical LiDAR unit cost in the tens of thousands of yuan, roughly the price of an entry-level vehicle. RoboSense’s ADAS LiDAR averaged about 2,600 yuan in 2024 and fell to 1,800 yuan in 2025. RoboSense’s M platform and Hesai’s ATX series have already dipped into the $200 range.
Hesai’s ATX, designed for the ADAS mass market, ships at roughly $150 per unit. To make the magnitude concrete, Hesai installed 328,246 units in Q1 2026 alone (34.9% market share), with Huawei second at 32.3%. The two of them are 67.2% of the Chinese passenger-vehicle LiDAR market.
The cost decline is no longer about premium vehicles. LiDAR is now standard equipment in cheap cars. Huajing S made LiDAR standard across the 120,000-150,000 yuan price range and supports Urban NOA. L2 driver assist penetration in China’s new passenger vehicles hit 69.15% in the first two months of 2026. When the same sensor that a robotaxi needs is shipping at 200,000+ unit volume into entry-level consumer cars, the supply chain economics flip. Robotaxi BOM stops being a hand-built luxury and starts looking like a configurable trim level.
You can see the cost curve in the per-vehicle numbers. Pony.ai's seventh-generation autonomous driving kit cut BOM by 70% versus the previous generation. The 2027 Pony.ai robotaxi total vehicle cost will land below 230,000 yuan (≈$33,000-$34,000), cheaper than a base Tesla Model 3. WeRide's GXR robotaxi hardware is currently around $40,000 and is expected to drop another 15%. Baidu Apollo Go's sixth-generation vehicle is just over 200,000 (≈$29,000) yuan.
The unit economics are starting to work. This is the part that matters most. For years the bear case was that robotaxi could never make per-ride money. The 2025 disclosures break that thesis. Pony.ai has achieved positive unit economics in Guangzhou and Shenzhen. WeRide reached unit economics breakeven in Abu Dhabi. None of these are "company profitable" yet, but unit economics in real cities is no longer hypothetical. 
Ni Licheng, CEO of SAIC Mobility, framed it cleanly. Robotaxi deployment scenarios have existed for years, but true large-scale scaling has not arrived yet, "though that day is coming soon." His prediction: the next year or two marks the starting point for large-scale robotaxi development. Goldman Sachs raised its 2026 China robotaxi fleet forecast from 13,000 to 14,000 units, up from about 5,000 in 2025.
The structural advantage that gets missed. It is worth zooming out and asking why so many Chinese AV players are moving in lockstep right now, and why Western incumbents are not. Zoox CEO Aicha Evans gave a useful read on the Rapid Response podcast this week. Her argument: the Chinese AV pack inherits a hardware-software-systems integration mindset from the smartphone era. Companies like Xiaomi (still a consumer tech company) and BYD (started as a phone battery supplier) had to be good at running hardware, software, and system-level architecture together as table stakes. That foundation transfers directly into EVs, and then into AVs. Evans framed the jump from EV to AV as moving from "a machine with wheels to a computer on wheels," with software as a first-class citizen in the architecture rather than a layer bolted on top.
The cautionary side. Not everything works yet. In late March, Baidu Apollo Go had a major system failure in Wuhan where multiple robotaxis stopped en route and left passengers stranded. Apollo Go suspended Wuhan service. Stock prices for Pony.ai and WeRide are falling while revenue rises, because the market is pricing the gap between single-vehicle profitability and company profitability. Chinese mobility is also no longer a growth market in aggregate. Ride-hailing saturation has been flagged for over a year. None of this kills the thesis. All of it complicates the path.
My take
China stays interesting. The combination of an industry-wide pivot, a commoditized sensor supply chain, proven per-vehicle unit economics, and a saturated home mobility market produces a specific dynamic that is worth watching closely over the coming quarters.
The competitive pressure inside China is intensifying, not easing. XPeng, Geely, the Tier 1 suppliers re-entering, plus the existing Baidu / WeRide / Pony.ai pack all going after the same pool of cities, riders, and capital. With the domestic ride-hailing market no longer growing in aggregate and stock prices punishing the gap between unit economics and company profit, the squeeze on margins at home is real. The natural answer for the players who have a credible operational record is to push overseas, where margins can be structurally higher, where competition for early permits is thinner, and where being first to a city anchors the regulator relationship.
