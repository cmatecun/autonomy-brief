Rocket Lab Enters the SpaceX Arena
Yesterday, the launch services and space systems company Rocket Lab agreed to purchase satellite manufacturer/ operator Iridium in a cash-and-stock deal that values the company at $8B, or ~20% more than the share price at Friday’s close. Iridium pioneered LEO satellites — launching its first ones some 30 years ago — and today operates a fleet of 66 satellites “that connect handsets and other devices used by ships, mining sites, U.S. government agencies, and other customers,” per WSJ [ https://substack.com/redirect/ec52962c-18f4-41d1-86bb-5b6187cce3b7?j=eyJ1IjoiMXlnZjcifQ.KOUIeBfeCRzhIwFnKXyQigR4j0VpF-SZzascYhSZunw ].
The move represents yet more vertical integration at Rocket Lab. Over the years, the company has grown from a niche small-satellite launch provider to an integrated space powerhouse. It now designs satellites and aerospace components, serves the defense primes, has the second-most launched US rocket annually (the Electron rocket), and is currently developing a reusable medium-lift launch vehicle.
With the Iridium acquisition, Rocket Lab is positioning itself to compete directly with SpaceX, which already operates a ~10,000-satellite fleet. Rocket Lab has its work cut out for it.
Comcast Splits
In 2011, Comcast — which was primarily a cable TV, internet, and phone provider to something like 30 million Americans — bought a 51% stake in NBCUniversal from General Electric. In 2013, it purchased the remaining 49%. Since then, Comcast has acquired the European media company Sky, the movie studio DreamWorks Animation, and through NBCUniversal spun up Peacock (among other activity in the entertainment space).
Yesterday, WSJ reported [ https://substack.com/redirect/df339e78-d243-4b54-95f6-62baf581dd12?j=eyJ1IjoiMXlnZjcifQ.KOUIeBfeCRzhIwFnKXyQigR4j0VpF-SZzascYhSZunw ] that Comcast’s decade-plus-long foray into trying to be a content + distribution company is coming to an end. It’s planning to split its business in two: the Comcast entity will once again become a connectivity-only business, and NBCUniversal will focus entirely on content.
There’s a recent history of telecom/ connectivity companies rolling up entertainment businesses, then spinning them out or getting rid of them. Over the past decade, Verizon bought AOL and Yahoo, reorganized the two into an entity called Verizon Media, and ultimately sold Verizon Media to Apollo in 2021. Over roughly the same period, AT&T bought DirecTV and Time Warner, but ended up fully exiting its position in DirecTV and spun off Time Warner (as WarnerMedia) into its own entity in 2022.
Clip Spotlight: AI Agents are catching stokes before people know they’re having them
From yesterday’s show: Cadence founder Chris Altchek is building a way to automate care for chronic diseases like heart failure, hypertension, and diabetes.
They manage nearly 100,000 patients. And they’re catching ~20 strokes a week before patients know they’re having one.
How it works:
A doctor orders Cadence. The patient gets connected devices at home, like a blood pressure cuff or scale. Cadence tracks daily vitals, reads medical records, labs, allergies, and symptoms, then uses AI agents with humans in the loop to adjust meds, triage risks, and coach patients on their care plan.
If a patient’s blood pressure spikes on a Saturday night, Cadence can call within 2.5 minutes to check symptoms and decide what needs to happen next.
One patient in rural North Carolina had been in and out of the hospital three times in six months. After Cadence got him stabilized and on the right meds, he played golf again for the first time in three years.
