XBOX Cuts
---------

From Bloomberg ( https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-06/microsoft-s-xbox-to-cut-3-200-jobs-divest-five-studios-in-major-overhaul ) :

Microsoft Corp.’s XBOX plans to eliminate 3,200 jobs, or around 20% of its staff over the next year, as part of a massive reorganization to spur growth in the struggling gaming division. XBOX will also divest four of its video-game development studios and is beginning the process to part ways with a fifth. “Our business today is not healthy,” Chief Executive Officer Asha Sharma wrote in a note to staff Monday morning, adding that XBOX is operating at margins three to 10 times lower than comparable businesses. “We must reset XBOX.”

Sharma’s note was bracing ( https://x.com/asha_shar/status/2074124008795369482 ) in terms of the picture it painted of XBOX’s ( now ALL CAPS ( https://news.xbox.com/en-us/2026/04/23/we-are-xbox/ ) ) precarious position. The full paragraph containing the above quotes was right at the top of her email:

Our business today is not healthy. We are operating at margins that are 3–10x lower than comparable platform and publishing businesses. We entered Gen 9 with a smaller install base and a higher cost structure. To grow, we bet on Game Pass, multi-platform, and a broader portfolio of content. While those businesses have created meaningful value, they did not grow at the pace we expected. As that happened, our core business weakened, and we added more teams, more investment, and more time, hoping for a better outcome. And now the industry is facing the most severe hardware crisis in its history. We must reset XBOX.

I explained what happened to XBOX in the context of the overall evolution of console gaming in 2022’s Consoles and Competition ( https://stratechery.com/2022/consoles-and-competition/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDIyL2NvbnNvbGVzLWFuZC1jb21wZXRpdGlvbi8iXX0sImV4cCI6MTc4NjA5NzA3NSwiaWF0IjoxNzgzNTA1MDc1LCJpc3MiOiJodHRwczovL2FwcC5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUvb2F1dGgiLCJzY29wZSI6ImZlZWQ6cmVhZCBhcnRpY2xlOnJlYWQgYXNzZXQ6cmVhZCBjYXRlZ29yeTpyZWFkIGVudGl0bGVtZW50cyIsInN1YiI6IjAxOTUxNmYxLWEzNDYtNzYxNC05ZTk2LWI2M2JkMTFmZTg1NiIsInVzZSI6ImFjY2VzcyJ9.N4xAHslFPMgxDj03zXLo_ZW8VTNSjtWv8Ov9n3jAqfdWoxx-C7R4hKVf9niHdVIwRES7HJMS9QaPWN9IQML21PfyPNuc7IW48cr5THtxYGW48osqNaNWuaKYUw23x7CAc7EIJe1aMHXGL2JutWpHx7IASese6J11ib-Bwq4h1Z1dzp8roM8aVFLqePG3aFAYl3hIRhqb8DvjFGHFU4__mCflu4jTxVrf4ukjLO63T7P6rcYmJP_6EaG6G4MrdBT4JjCfyHq5o7javq1Tor55noTNOJ_lgmWLDtbEYHXVklBjiME0c5IXD4s43EWxyA61nc7glRDxuFavGNbd1PJsJw ). In brief:

* Consoles used to be differentiated by hardware, and developers generally committed to one platform.
* The shift to 3D and high definition graphics drove the emergence of game engines on one hand, and increased the cost of asset creation on the other; developers responded by making games cross-platform, which particularly burned Sony’s PlayStation 3, which was predicated on hardware differentiation.
* Sony responded by buying multiple studios and making their games exclusive, which made PlayStation 4 and then 5 clear winners over XBOX (Nintendo, as always, was doing their own thing with the Switch).

That Article was written in the context of Microsoft’s acquisition of Activision, one of the first 3rd-party developers, an important part of the company’s effort to compete with Sony by brute-forcing a new business model; from the Article:

Microsoft is willing to cannibalize itself to build a new business model for video games, and it’s a business model that is pretty darn attractive for consumers. It’s also a business model that Activision wouldn’t pursue on its own, because it has its own profits to protect. Most importantly, though, it’s a business model that is anathema to Sony: making titles broadly available to consumers on a subscription basis is the exact opposite of the company’s exclusive strategy, which is all about locking consumers into Sony’s platform.

Unfortunately, what is clear is that the attempt to shift gaming from an up-front purchase model — which increased the barrier to entry for games, but maximized revenue per game — to a subscription-based services model — which gave you all of the games, at the cost of the most popular games’ profitability — was anathema to actually making money. Or, to put it another way, this was a bundling disaster.

Bundling and the Internet Solvent
---------------------------------

Everyone is familiar with that famous Jim Barksdale quote, “There are only two ways I know to make money in business: bundling and unbundling.” Barksdale was most well-known as the CEO of Netscape in the late 1990s, and reportedly coined the aphorism during Netscape’s 1995 IPO road show. And, in the three decades since then, it’s just sort of been taken as a given that you can make money in either direction. However, I’m not sure that that is actually the case.

Consider television, the most famous bundle of all. The unbundling of the pay-TV ecosystem is well-documented by this point — and Netflix made a lot of money in the unbundling — but it’s not at all clear that things will re-bundle, at least in the way that people understand bundling. Yes, there are various special offers to subscribe to this or that collection of streaming services, but as long as everything is available a la carte it seems unlikely that there will ever be a collective offering like before; bundles work best when you don’t have a choice.

One counter to this is that Netflix didn’t just unbundle, they also bundled: ideally you can subscribe to Netflix and get something for everyone, including an ever increasing amount of low-budget filler content ( https://variety.com/2026/tv/news/netflix-variety-how-well-do-they-know-video-licensing-1236803102/ ). This bundle, however, isn’t at all comparable to the cable bundle: no matter how much stuff Netflix gets, content you want will be streaming elsewhere; bundles work best when they have everything.

This is the key to what I think is the only real bundle that has been created by the Internet: Spotify specifically, and music streaming services generally. Technically, you still have a choice to buy music a la carte; in reality, the value proposition of Spotify is so compelling that for most people it really isn’t a choice at all. For a relatively low monthly price you get basically everything , thanks to the technological breakthrough that is streaming and cellular connectivity. This is in fact critical: economics don’t make bundles work; technological changes do, particularly when the technological change doesn’t just provide carrots but also sticks in the form of piracy (and before you bring up Amazon Prime, that is first and foremost a logistics-based bundle, i.e. it’s rooted in the physical world).

Go back to TV: the technological change that mattered there was the initial creation of cable networks to connect rural communities to large antennas capturing signals from far-off cities, followed by satellites that beamed the signals anywhere in the world. The bundling was enabled by technology — that’s the only bundling that works. The same story applies to print: newspapers were bundles that were enabled by the centralizing nature of printing presses; on the Internet, where anyone can publish, bundles are impossible.

With this framing it’s much more obvious why Game Pass failed. Microsoft’s case for Game Pass was purely an economic one: get more games for less money, and make it up in volume. What limited that volume, however, was not the fact that games were too expensive, but that the people who cared to game already did; now they simply paid less, and Microsoft lost the difference. From the Wall Street Journal ( https://www.wsj.com/tech/microsoft-layoffs-xbox-59e7f6df ) :

Microsoft bought game production companies including Activision Blizzard to beef up the offerings on Game Pass, its Netflix-style subscription service. The company had projected Game Pass subscriptions would reach around 77 million this year, according to a document revealed during legal proceedings related to the Activision acquisition. It currently has about 30 million, a person familiar with the matter said.

Game Pass existed in a world of choice, it didn’t have everything, and it was fighting technological paradigms; is it any wonder it failed?

More generally, the Internet, by virtue of eliminating distribution costs, is a bundle solvent; you make money by unbundling, and lose money by trying to fight gravity. What actually works is an approach that starts with the assumption of abundance, which is to say markets. The companies that make the most money in gaming are the market makers, i.e. the storefronts, like Apple’s App Store or Valve’s Steam. Markets benefit from abundance and liquidity and maximizing consumer choice and are the most natural way to make money on the Internet.

Transaction, Coordination, and Sunk Costs
-----------------------------------------

Two more paragraphs from Sharma’s email:

Since 2018, we have aggressively expanded our studio portfolio while the number of games created each month across the industry now outpaces the last ten years combined. We now find ourselves competing not only with the largest publishers, but also with smaller independent studios. It is neither possible nor desirable to own every great independent studio. We have also learned that we are not the best home for every type of studio; in a typical year, we lost 64 cents for every dollar we invested. As we reset XBOX, we will help independent creators succeed by providing open development tools and audiences to realize their vision…

We know that great technology gets better when it gets simpler, not bigger. Today, in some parts of the company, work passes through as many as 14 layers of management. Our platform teams are 40% larger than they were at the start of this generation, even as our player base and playtime have declined. That complexity has slowed decisions, blurred accountability, and made it harder to deliver for players. As we reset XBOX, we will simplify.

That first paragraph recognizes the reality I just described: trying to own everything on the Internet is impossible; it is better to own demand and facilitate users buying what they want.

The cost of fighting gravity, however, is more than the money lost: it’s the internal complexity that comes from trying to own everything. Ronald Coase wrote a famous treatise about The Nature of the Firm ( https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1111/j.1468-0335.1937.tb00002.x ) , which posited that companies exist because markets have costs: searching, negotiating, contracting, monitoring, enforcing, coordinating etc. are all costly transactions that can be more easily accomplished by internalizing them in a command-and-control structure. At the same time, that command-and-control structure had costs of its own — coordination costs, Coase called them — and the natural size of a firm was the point at which coordination costs roughly equaled market transaction costs.

Technology’s impact on this equilibrium has cut in both directions. On one hand, it is easier to scale because of technology; on the other, it is easier to create efficient markets because of technology. The way this has played out is that there are massive market makers on one end of the spectrum — the Aggregators — and a plethora of small actors on the other; it’s companies in the middle that have gotten squeezed.

XBOX’s Game Pass strategy, unfortunately, pushed the company right into this middle zone. Trying to own too much meant Microsoft was simultaneously not big enough to serve the smallest players (contra Steam) and also too large to efficiently serve the customers that they had. What made all of this worse is that the way that Microsoft got itself into the position of paying massive coordination costs was by also paying a massive premium to override market signals that, in the end, were perfectly rational (i.e. no independent studio wanted to join Game Pass). It really was the worst of both worlds!

This analysis is, to be clear, being done with the benefit of hindsight; I was amenable to the company’s Game Pass plan, and obviously should have considered many of these points previously. The best I can do now, then, is point out what went wrong. The considerably more difficult challenge for Microsoft is to figure out what to do with the mess they have on their hands.

There are two options that make sense. The first is to accept that the studios they bought are sunk costs, and go all-in on an exclusive strategy, even as they end Game Pass and charge full price for their titles. The other is to give up on Xbox (I haven’t even touched on the memory crisis) and simply become a game publisher. I have, for years ( https://stratechery.com/2014/time-kill-surface/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDE0L3RpbWUta2lsbC1zdXJmYWNlLyJdfSwiZXhwIjoxNzg2MDk3MDc1LCJpYXQiOjE3ODM1MDUwNzUsImlzcyI6Imh0dHBzOi8vYXBwLnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZS9vYXV0aCIsInNjb3BlIjoiZmVlZDpyZWFkIGFydGljbGU6cmVhZCBhc3NldDpyZWFkIGNhdGVnb3J5OnJlYWQgZW50aXRsZW1lbnRzIiwic3ViIjoiMDE5NTE2ZjEtYTM0Ni03NjE0LTllOTYtYjYzYmQxMWZlODU2IiwidXNlIjoiYWNjZXNzIn0.Ec8xkl_oKUkxiUjM9OTgLUEa9L3Xh5CWsgydN8yPdY-JXD5ScvjAX8nRXL3_KENLtEzUJDQ4JEETYHWTEtl84Ijya2HE5VD17pqgXih9zYbXqCyHxNhn-q1mK-vpuv2ti1L-vvEcKveATB_LfJMI_-UWn38tPodTEnkbGVzitn1v2hCwWSd-6_culOXoEi9aytl1icUkx2hklUkNyGu9NUaa7J5E0RdWHj0OsWzBziRtGh_jsEuuddgPUPIoZ1gPrnDUIJ-jfXbbG6aEhaAUDUAxJW5j0K-ExQb785hhXrnZPHTjX1g4xipwLL4Z0YuWaza35MDE9q9qxpxi-Pujow ) , favored the latter, and I don’t see any reason to change. The allure of Game Pass is that it at least had some theoretical alignment with Microsoft’s services strategy; it’s hard to see any sort of synergy otherwise. Indeed, maybe that points to where we are actually headed: unwinding and/or spinning out the division completely. Sometimes it’s Game Over.
