Waymo Buys Options Across Europe, Four Entities in Two Weeks
Waymo is getting serious about Europe, and the paper trail proves it.
Last week, we covered that Waymo had registered a German legal entity, Waymo Germany GmbH in Munich. This week we learned the German filing was not a one-off. Public corporate records show Waymo has registered Waymo Iberia SL in Spain, Waymo France in Paris, and Waymo Netherlands B.V. in Amsterdam. Three of the four were first uncovered by the colleagues at The Road to Autonomy, so kudos to them for the catch.
The details are worth the look. Waymo Iberia SL was incorporated through a notarial deed signed in Madrid on June 9 and entered the commercial registry on June 11. Note the name: Iberia, not Spain. That naming choice suggests the entity could oversee operations across both Spain and Portugal. Waymo France followed on June 23, registered in the 8th arrondissement of Paris with 100,000 euros in share capital, and its corporate purpose is explicit: on-demand passenger transport using autonomous vehicles, plus supporting third-party robotaxi services. Two US-based managers, Kelly Francis and Steven Hahn, were named as gérants.
The Dutch filing is the newest piece. Waymo Netherlands B.V. was registered in Amsterdam on June 12 and entered the Dutch business register three days later. Its listed activities cover retail sale of motor vehicles, support activities for land transportation, and, notably, computing infrastructure and data processing. The register also names Waymo LLC as sole shareholder, with the same directors as Waymo Germany GmbH.
Four filings, one shareholder, one set of directors. This is a single program executed country by country, not a scattering of local experiments.
The Portugal timing is what makes the Iberia filing interesting. To be clear, nothing in the filing names Portugal. The link is my reading of the name, not a company statement. But the timing makes that reading hard to ignore: a few weeks ago I covered that Portugal opened its roads to autonomous vehicle testing, and the mechanism matters. Decreto-Lei n.º 113/2026, in force this month, allows licensed AV testing on public roads under a dedicated legal framework for the first time. Before this, testing opportunities in Portugal were narrow. The legal door opens, and within weeks an entity named for the whole peninsula appears in Madrid’s registry.
France is compelling for the opposite reason: the door there has been open for a long time. France has permitted commercial L4 driverless operations within approved areas since September 2022 under Ordonnance n° 2021-443, one of the few European jurisdictions where commercial robotaxi deployment is already legally possible today. WeRide has been operating a driverless minibus in Valence under that framework, and the national strategy through 2027 explicitly aims to make France Europe’s preferred AV deployment location.
Let me be precise about what this is and is not. Four legal entities are not four launches, and Waymo has announced no timelines. The entities are options, and the information is in the pattern of where and when they were bought. Look at the June timeline. Iberia on the 9th, the Netherlands on the 12th, Germany’s register entry on the 15th, France on the 23rd. Four European economies in two weeks.
Three of the four sit exactly where Europe’s L4 regulation is furthest along: Germany with its Autonomous Driving Act and AFGBV, France with its ordonnance, Iberia, if the name means what it appears to mean, just as Portugal’s framework switches on. The fourth breaks the pattern, and that makes it the interesting one. The Netherlands has not changed its national traffic law for L4 operations, even though the RDW has shown itself to be one of Europe’s most pragmatic approval authorities (see the Tesla FSD approval, which, worth remembering, is an L2 system). 
What stands out instead is Waymo’s Dutch entity’s stated purpose: computing infrastructure and data processing, alongside transport support. Read together with the EU’s data-residency expectations, we covered in CW25, Amsterdam looks like the infrastructure layer of this expansion. Whether it also becomes a launch market is not something a registry entry can tell you, and it may well end up being both. That is not a company wandering into Europe. That is a company mapping the regulatory terrain, buying an option on every ready market, and standing up the plumbing behind them.
And on a personal note: Germany is on the list for certain, and if I’m reading the Iberia name right, Portugal is too. My two home countries. I LIKE!
Waymo is not the only new GmbH in town
Niulinx, whose CEO Luca Foresti we had on the podcast just last week [ https://substack.com/redirect/f81d4b2c-deb4-488b-bc23-7e1ca7e8cfe1?j=eyJ1IjoiMXlnZjcifQ.KOUIeBfeCRzhIwFnKXyQigR4j0VpF-SZzascYhSZunw ], has founded a German entity of its own. Niulinx GmbH, based in Munich, was formalized on July 1 with CFO Gian Marco Felice signing the incorporation documents. The stated goal is the immediate launch of on-road testing to achieve German homologation of its L4 system as fast as possible. If you heard Luca on the show, this is his “regulatory-native” thesis executing in real time: build the technology to be homologable from day one, and prove it in the strictest framework Europe offers.

Bolt: no London this year, and a deliberate quietness
More European expansion signals came from Bolt. Charlotte Eisner, Bolt’s Head of Commercialisation and Corporate Partnerships for Autonomous Vehicles (and our very first podcast guest back in EP01), spoke with Zag Daily about what comes next.
London is out for 2026. “To target London and Paris, that’s a really big ask,” Eisner said. “For PR, it’s great value. But to actually deploy and get started, I think if you were to ask Waymo, Uber and others, they would need to wait a long time to get the permits.” Refreshingly honest, given how many operators treat announcements as free marketing.
What is in: Europe, almost certainly the EU, with medium-sized cities above 300,000 people as the target profile. Luxembourg remains the first testing market. Bolt is aiming for two announced European pilots this year, Luxembourg included, and “maybe one more somewhere else.” Driverless operations in Luxembourg are slated for 2027, though Charlotte noted other markets could move quicker.
The most interesting part was her explanation of why Bolt stays quiet about future cities: “We want the city to be the one welcoming us.” Announcements that are not anchored locally create problems with fire departments, police and public opinion, she argued, and Bolt already has MOUs and permits underway in several cities it chooses not to name. In an industry that often announces first and deploys never, inverting that order is a strategy in itself.
What this means and what to watch
Europe is getting crowded, and I consider that the healthiest signal this region has produced in years. In one week: filings by the world’s robotaxi leader surfaced in four countries, an Italian startup incorporated in Munich to chase homologation, an Estonian platform disclosed pilots and permits in unnamed cities. My position: the era of Europe as the AV industry’s afterthought is ending.
Watch which Waymo entity moves first. Germany has the potential operating partner (Transdev, as we noted last week) and job listings on the ground. The Netherlands reads like the infrastructure play, and Iberia is a bet on regulatory timing.

Uber and Waymo’s divorce continues
Regular readers know this breakup did not come out of nowhere. We have been tracking the cooling Uber-Waymo relationship for weeks in this newsletter, and we dedicated an entire podcast episode to it with our friend Harry Campbell  of The Driverless Digest. Harry’s line from that conversation has aged well: transactional relationships have a shorter expiration date. This week, the first one expired.
What actually happened
Waymo robotaxis are no longer available on Uber’s app in Phoenix. Both companies confirmed the end of the partnership to TechCrunch, closing a collaboration that began in 2023. Phoenix was a special market in one respect: it was the only city where Waymo operated its own app alongside Uber’s, the hybrid setup that made it a live experiment in who owns the rider.
The company statements are worth reading closely. Waymo called it “a productive pilot that paved the way for future expansions,” and said the vehicles have already been folded back into its own Phoenix fleet, where they will keep serving riders through the Waymo app, the Via public-transit integration, and the DoorDash delivery deal. Uber said Phoenix “was an intentionally limited deployment, reaching just over a dozen vehicles dedicated to the program.”
Pause on that last quote. This is the first time Uber has talked about how small the Phoenix deployment actually was, and it did so only after the partnership ended. While the deal was alive, the scope stayed vague. 
Now convert the numbers into meaning. Just over a dozen vehicles, against a Waymo fleet of roughly 4,000 cars serving more than 500,000 paid trips per week. Phoenix was well under half a percent of Waymo’s fleet. Operationally, this split is a rounding error. Strategically, it is a siren.
Uber stays in Phoenix. With whom?
Uber says it is preparing a new autonomous vehicle partnership in Phoenix and declined to name the partner. The honest starting point: we do not know who it will be. And the longer I sit with the question, the more interesting it gets, because the short-term answer might be nobody.
Run through Uber’s bench. Whoever backfills Phoenix needs to put real numbers of vehicles on the road, and delivering even 100 cars into a new market within six months is currently beyond almost every operator not named Waymo. Uber’s Chinese partners are shut out of the US market entirely. Wayve’s technology is not validated for L4 driving in America. And for Nuro, the most advanced partner in Uber’s owned-fleet program, getting 100 to 150 cars on the road in California, its home market, would already count as the win of the year.
That said, the breadcrumbs point somewhere. Days after the news broke, Dara Khosrowshahi appeared in a video riding with Nuro’s co-founder in one of the program’s vehicles, and timing like that is rarely an accident. 
Source: X/Nuro
Uber is also already invested in exactly this pairing: it licenses Nuro’s L4 system and has committed to at least 35,000 Lucid vehicles. Geography helps too. Lucid is building its first production-validation robotaxis in Casa Grande, Arizona, with regular robotaxi production targeted for the fourth quarter, and Nuro has spent years validating in hot, sprawling, car-dependent metros. Phoenix is that profile.
But the case against is real, and it got stronger this week. Phoenix appears nowhere in the program’s stated roadmap, which names the Bay Area for late 2026 and Houston for mid-2027. The service is not yet commercial anywhere, and key permits are still missing. And Lucid itself just went through a major restructuring, as Bloomberg reported this week, with EV sales below expectations and a new CFO among several executive changes.
And there is a third reading worth taking seriously: maybe Uber feels no pressure to backfill Phoenix at all. The program’s next announced market may have nothing to do with Phoenix. So we are left with no confirmed partner, no candidate that can deliver at short notice, and one heavily invested pairing with fresh headwinds. Whoever it turns out to be, the announcement will tell us more about Uber’s post-Waymo playbook than the split itself did. I am genuinely curious.
The hardest signal yet, and why Atlanta and Austin are probably next
This is the strongest evidence to date that the Uber-Waymo partnership is winding down entirely, and I am not alone in thinking Atlanta and Austin will follow. The Road to Autonomy team, who broke the Phoenix story, expects the remaining markets to roll off sequentially. I agree, and the structural logic matters more than anyone’s prediction.
Phoenix ran for roughly 948 days, a testing-era deal signed when robotaxis at scale were still a hypothesis. Austin and Atlanta are younger, exclusive to Uber, and run hundreds of Waymo vehicles rather than a dozen. Which is precisely the point: those markets have not yet hit the density where Waymo can walk. Uber’s value to an AV developer is highest at market entry, when the developer has no local brand, no rider habit, and no utilization data. Every ride closes that gap. Once fleet utilization, brand recognition and demand density cross a threshold, sharing 20 to 30 percent of every fare with a platform stops making sense for a company that has its own app, its own operations muscle, and Alphabet’s balance sheet behind it.
That platform take is the quiet economic engine of this story. Every Phoenix ride Waymo now serves directly returns the roughly 20 to 30 percent it previously shared. To be clear, with a dozen cars this will not move any needle, in Phoenix or anywhere else. But the mechanism matters more than the magnitude, because the same math is waiting in Austin and Atlanta, where hundreds of Waymo vehicles still run exclusively on Uber’s app. That is where reclaiming the take stops being a rounding error and starts being a margin story.
Does this blow up Uber’s demand-aggregation narrative, as some are arguing this week? I would put it more carefully. There are two ways to read Phoenix. Read one way, Waymo just proved that a scaled AV operator does not need Uber, and every developer’s endgame is now visible. Read another way, Uber’s 30 plus AV partnerships exist precisely because most developers are not Waymo. They lack capital, permits, operations experience and consumer trust, and for them the platform is load-bearing, not optional. Both reads are true at once. Demand aggregation is a bridge, not a moat. Waymo could afford to cross it. Most others, for now, cannot.
The global chessboard: Uber’s China question
Here is where this gets interesting from a global perspective, and it is the part of this story I think is underexamined.
Uber’s international AV bench leans heavily on Chinese developers: Baidu, WeRide, Pony.ai and others across Europe, the Middle East and Asia. And in Europe, the partnership logic has an extra layer that goes beyond demand aggregation. Chinese AV brands are unknown to European riders, and some European consumers may hesitate to knowingly hail a Chinese-operated robotaxi. When you call an Uber and a WeRide shows up, you are not really making that connection. You asked Uber for a ride. Fifteen years of familiarity, whatever its bumps, made Uber the trusted front end. For Chinese developers entering Europe, Uber is not just demand. It is a familiar face.
But the Phoenix playbook travels. A player like Baidu, for example, has its own consumer super-app DNA and, arguably, the capital to vertically integrate and run its own front door once its European operations mature. Whether it would want to operate under a Chinese brand in Europe is a different question, and I suspect the answer keeps the Uber partnerships alive there longer than pure economics would suggest.
Southeast Asia looks different. In markets where Chinese consumer brands are already established and the hesitation is lower, the Uber bridge is shorter. Developers that build enough fleet density there could follow Waymo’s path from partner to competitor much sooner. The pattern to internalize: wherever a developer crosses the brand-and-density threshold, the platform relationship flips from symbiosis to expiration countdown. Phoenix is what the end of that countdown looks like.
One more question Uber will eventually have to answer. With 20 plus AV partners competing for slots on one app, who decides which partner gets which market, and on what terms? If ride allocation follows commercial terms rather than ride quality, the marketplace inherits a self-preferencing problem that platforms in other industries know well. Uber has not given the market clarity on how multi-partner allocation will work. At some point, its partners will demand it.
Tesla, meanwhile, produced headlines of its own
While Waymo and Uber were untangling, Tesla had a genuinely notable week, and I want to give it the two-lens treatment it deserves.
The real milestone first. Tesla has started testing a production version of the Cybercab in Austin: two seats, no steering wheel, no pedals, and self-certified by Tesla as SAE Level 4. There is a person in the right passenger seat, but be precise about the role. With no wheel and no pedals, that person cannot take over the vehicle. All they can do is probably press a button that pulls the car over. The test comes nearly two years after the Cybercab reveal, and after weeks of prototype versions (with steering wheels) running in several US cities while hundreds of the vehicles accumulated in parking lots.
Two more signals landed the same week. Tesla published a first-responders guide for the Cybercab, the kind of document you release when deployment is near rather than hypothetical. And as we covered in CW27, NHTSA has proposed letting vehicles designed exclusively for automated driving skip the brake pedal entirely, with the proposal expected to go through later this year. One constraint remains firmly in place: without a federal exemption, Tesla cannot charge for these rides. Still, vehicle, factory output and rulemaking are visibly lining up. That is real.
Then came the second headline: Tesla published a Robotaxi service-area map for Miami. The geofence covers a small slice of the metro, roughly West Miami plus a strip toward Doral and Sweetwater, bounded by the Palmetto to the north and US-41 to the south. No downtown, no Miami Beach, no airport. Miami was one of five cities Tesla named for a first-half 2026 launch, a timeline that has since softened to “preparations underway.”
Source: Electrek
Now the other lens. Publishing a geofence is the easy part, and Tesla has made a habit of it. We have documented this pattern before: in Austin, the service area grew to cover the entire metro while the actual unsupervised fleet shrank. Musk himself told investors on the Q1 call that safety validation is the limiting factor and that scaling waits on FSD v15. Expanding a geofence costs nothing. Expanding a fleet requires solving the safety problem, and by Tesla’s own admission it is not solved yet.
Why keep drawing boxes, then? Because every new geofence is an autonomy headline, and autonomy carries a very large share of Tesla’s valuation. Analysts across the street have long put more than half of Tesla’s market value on the autonomy and robotics story. When that much of the story is priced on the promise, the map is the message.
What makes this genuinely hard to call is that market observers are currently split into two camps. One camp holds that safety is the binding constraint, and it rests on Musk’s own words: scaling waits on FSD v15. The other camp argues the opposite for the Cybercab specifically: the factory is ready, the validation fleet has done its miles, and what gates deployment is regulation, namely the federal framework that would let a vehicle without controls operate and charge for rides across all 50 states. In that read, the expected rollout runs Austin, Dallas, Houston, and then Phoenix or Miami, which would make the Miami geofence look like preparation rather than theater. Both camps fit the visible evidence. I lean toward the first camp, for a simple reason: Musk said it himself. When the CEO whose valuation hangs on the scaling story tells investors that safety validation is what holds it back, that admission carries more weight than any outside read.
So, honestly held: the Cybercab test is a real step toward the purpose-built, low-cost robotaxi Tesla has always argued it needs, and the Miami service area is undecidable on current evidence. The useful news is that this dispute resolves itself. If the brake-pedal rule is finalized and the Cybercab ramp follows, the regulatory read was right. If the rule lands and the fleet still does not grow, then safety was the constraint all along, whatever the map says.
What this means and what to watch
Watch whether Atlanta and Austin follow. The same density logic that ended Phoenix is at work in both markets, just earlier on the curve. I think they will eventually see the same divorce, but that is a view on direction, not a timetable, and it remains to be seen. The quiet signals will matter more than announcements here: whether those agreements renew or simply lapse, the way Phoenix did.
Watch who succeeds Waymo in Phoenix. Uber says a new partnership is coming, and the field of candidates who could actually deliver vehicles at short notice is thin. The breadcrumbs point to Nuro and Lucid, the headwinds point away from them, and the honest answer is we do not know. Whoever it turns out to be, and however long Phoenix stays without a successor, both answers will tell us a lot about Uber’s post-Waymo playbook.
Uber’s multi-partner allocation problem is the next disclosure fight. The more partners Uber signs, the less avoidable the question of who gets priority and why. 
For Tesla, the brake-pedal rule is the tell. If NHTSA finalizes the proposal this year and Cybercab production actually ramps, the purpose-built thesis gets its first real test. Until the unsupervised fleet in Austin grows instead of shrinking, treat every new geofence as a valuation instrument, not a launch.
