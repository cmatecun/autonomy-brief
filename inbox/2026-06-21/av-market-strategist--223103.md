# The $12.8B race to run AV fleets, WeRide & Uber to launch Robotaxis in Zurich and Mobileye turns Operator

**From:** The AV Market Strategist <avmarketstrategist@substack.com>  
**Date:** 2026-06-22T05:31:03.000Z

👋 Welcome back everyone,
Six names in autonomy signed deals that looked like they were arguing with each other. Waymo, Mobileye, Stellantis, Wayve, WeRide, Uber. Read the headlines and you get a pile of unrelated robotaxi announcements. Read the structure and you find them lined up along two questions.
The first: do you disaggregate the stack, build the vehicle, the autonomy and the demand with partners, and rent the operating layer (the depots, charging, local labor, regulatory relationships) from a specialist? Or do you own all of it? Almost everyone this week chose to disaggregate. Mobileye chose to own the whole thing.
The second question sits underneath the first: who owns the rider. Hand the demand layer to Uber, or build your own front door. Let’s start with the company that just bet against the crowd on both counts.

Six deals, two questions. Disaggregate or own it, and who owns the rider
Mobileye from licensing the brain to own the car
Mobileye said it will launch a direct-to-consumer robotaxi service in a major US city in 2027. It starts small, an initial fleet of roughly 100 vehicles phased in through the year, and targets an aggressive ramp to about 17,000 vehicles over the following five years. The service pairs Mobileye Drive, its L4 system, with its Moovit subsidiary for rider booking, trip planning and fleet management. Mobileye already runs around 100 autonomous ID. Buzz vehicles across six cities, but only to validate the technology, not to carry paying riders.
The thing to understand is what kind of company Mobileye has been until now, a  publicly traded supplier. It sells the autonomy stack, L2+ and L4, into other people’s vehicles and other people’s networks, including Volkswagen and its MOIA subsidiary. This announcement flips that. Founder and CEO Amnon Shashua framed it around the industry becoming “increasingly dependent on a small number of technology providers and business models.” In plain terms: Mobileye is moving from licensor to operator, which also means sometimes from supplier to competitor.
And here is why this is the contrarian move of the week. The rest of the industry is disaggregating. The Chinese players go asset-light: WeRide and Pony license or sell the driver and let local partners finance and own the fleet and run operations. Waymo owns its vehicles but rents the operating layer (more on that next). Mobileye is doing the opposite of all of them. It wants to own the driver, own the demand through Moovit, and own the operations, carrying the whole capital-intensive balance sheet itself.
Why now? The Road to Autonomy team made the most convincing case on their Autonomy Signals show this week, and I land in roughly the same place. The human read is fear of missing out, and I mean that without the sneer. When the general conversation about robotaxis happens, the names are Waymo, WeRide, Pony, Baidu, and Tesla, occasionally Zoox or Avride. Mobileye almost never comes up. So the strategic logic is that selling components no longer captures enough of the value being created, that vertical integration is back in vogue, and that running your own fleet generates the operational learnings that make the stack itself better and more sellable to the OEMs still unsure Mobileye belongs in the L4 conversation.
That is the narrative. The execution is where I get skeptical, and the discipline here is the one we apply to everyone. The press release names no city. There are no disclosed permit filings, no facility lease, no standalone SEC trail as of mid-June. A 2027 launch with none of the operational scaffolding visible is, on that test, structurally premature.
The deeper problem is capital, and it is the reason the asset-heavy choice is so striking. Alphabet and its partners have put billions into Waymo to get it here. Tesla has spent also billions. Becoming an operator means absorbing depots, charging, maintenance and insurance onto a balance sheet that has never carried them. So the question is not whether Mobileye wants to operate. It is whether its investors have the conviction to fund years of that, through the first incident and the first paused fleet, while everyone else is busy offloading exactly those costs.
Waymo’s answer: own the cars, rent the operating layer
On 15 June, Element Fleet Management, the world’s largest publicly traded automotive fleet manager, announced a multi-year partnership with Waymo. It begins with end-to-end fleet operations in San Diego, a new Waymo market, with more cities to follow. 
The division of labor is explicit, and it is the whole point. Waymo keeps the Waymo Driver and keeps the rider, continuing to run the service through its own app and oversee the autonomous system. Element takes the behind-the-scenes layer: vehicle lifecycle management, charging infrastructure, energy management, maintenance coordination and fleet optimization. As Waymo’s Nicole Gavel put it, “scaling autonomous mobility requires partners with proven operational discipline and global fleet expertise.”
Element is a different kind of counterparty. Roughly 1.56 million vehicles under management globally. About $1.2 billion in 2025 net revenue. A 56% adjusted operating margin. A funding relationship with Blackstone and a market cap near $8 billion. Its data infrastructure identified $1.6 billion in cost savings across its clients last year.
And it is not arriving into an empty field. Element is the fifth horse in a race that barely existed eighteen months ago. Waymo runs roughly 4,000 vehicles across eleven cities at more than 500,000 paid rides a week, on track to perhaps double that footprint by the end of 2026, and it maintains almost none of them itself. Four operators were already competing to do that work, each from a completely different starting point.
Moove.io, a fintech that began financing Uber drivers’ cars in Lagos, now runs Waymo vehicles in Phoenix, Miami and London and raised $1.2 billion in debt to fund the push, with the open question of whether it starts owning the vehicles outright. Avomo, the AV arm of a Spanish fleet manager, runs Austin and Atlanta off a European depot-and-technician playbook. Avis, with seventy-five years and 15,000 vehicles across 50-plus Dallas sites alone, is using Waymo to reposition from a rental company into what its CEO calls a mobility-infrastructure provider. Lyft runs Nashville through its Flexdrive subsidiary, the only player holding both the demand app and the operations. Element enters as: public, institutionally capitalized, disciplined by quarterly disclosure.
And there is one more, the operator almost nobody writes about, which happens to have been doing this the longest. Transdev, a French multi-modal transit operator that moves around 12.8 million passengers a day across buses, trains, ferries and trams, has been running Waymo’s fleets through its TAS subsidiary since 2019, before any of the names above existed in this business. More than a thousand of its people already handle depots, maintenance, charging, remote supervision and regulatory liaison across Phoenix, San Francisco, Los Angeles and Austin. 
And it points to something the horse-race framing misses: this is not one race, it is several. Most of the operators above were built or reshaped for this opportunity in the recent capital cycle, whether AV-native startups, a rental giant repositioning, or a public fleet manager like Element. Transdev is a different species, a legacy transit operator for whom robotaxis are one service line inside a contracted-revenue business. The distinction matters because operating maturity is the one thing you cannot raise in a funding round. Capital arrives in months. The workforce systems, training pipelines and regulatory relationships Transdev has built over six years do not.
That field tells you what Waymo is actually shopping for, and why Element fits. The priorities are reliability at 99%-plus uptime, the speed to stand up a new city in weeks rather than months, capital efficiency if a partner will carry the vehicles on its own balance sheet, and low management overhead. Element answers most of that list at once. Be precise about what this is not, though. Waymo is not turning into a pure licensor here. It still owns the cars and still owns the customer. What it is renting is the operating layer.
And that layer is becoming an industry of its own. Global Market Insights projects the AV fleet-operations market at $12.8 billion by 2034. Probably, this will consolidate on the current form toward a handful of dominant players. The cleanest analogy is one we have discussed with Crijn Bouman, CEO of Rocsys, on the Autonomy Insiders Podcast: in aviation, the durable money sits less with the airlines than with the ground handlers, the Swissports and Menzies that fuel, clean and turn the aircraft around between flights. Robotaxis are heading the same way. Element, Moove, Avis, and the rest are positioning to be the Swissport of autonomy, and Waymo’s willingness to rent the entire layer rather than build it is the clearest sign yet that this is a real, separable business. Mobileye is betting the exact reverse, that you must own all of it.
The unglamorous part of scaling: highways, and a fourth recall
A serious operating layer is not only depots and fleet partners. It is also the freeway, because highways are where robotaxis earn the trip times and route coverage that make the economics work. Which is why this week’s other Waymo headline matters, and why it stings a little.
On 17 June, Waymo filed a recall with NHTSA covering 3,871 vehicles, effectively its entire US fifth-generation fleet. The defect: under certain conditions, the vehicle could enter and drive at speed into freeway construction zones, either by failing to recognize a closed zone or by deprioritizing it while avoiding another hazard. The trigger events are documented. 
On 11 and 19 April, Waymo vehicles in Phoenix drove past ramp-closure signs into pre-planned construction zones. On 18 May, seven vehicles in the San Francisco Bay Area drove between cones into active lane closures. The Field Safety Committee restricted freeway operations, the Safety Board reviewed on 1 June, and Waymo decided on 8 June to file. No software fix exists yet; the remedy is, in NHTSA’s words, under development. The interim measure is blunt: all vehicles are restricted from freeways for now.
Back in November, when Waymo first unlocked freeway driving across a 260-square-mile slice of the Bay Area, I called it the removal of the last structural constraint separating a robotaxi from a normal ride-hail trip. This recall puts that constraint back. The capability we treated as a milestone is, for now, switched off.
There are two ways to read a recall like this, and I hold both. Read one way, it is exactly how a safety-led operator should behave: catch a pattern, restrict proactively, notify regulators, file voluntarily. Read another way, a company that just hired a fleet manager to scale faster has simultaneously pulled itself off freeways with no fix in hand, capping the very network it is trying to expand. Both are true. The recall is evidence of discipline and evidence of how hard the operating layer is, at the same time.
New competition arrives: Nuro, Lucid and Uber pick Houston
On 16 June, Nuro, Lucid and Uber said they will bring a robotaxi service to Houston in 2027, with a mid-2027 target. 
Source: Nuro
Houston is the partnership’s second planned market, with dozens more flagged over the coming years. Nuro is already running 24/7 on-road testing with safety operators in Houston and the San Francisco Bay Area, and Uber has secured a 50,000-square-foot Houston depot plus an additional pitstop to support fleet operations.
Notice what Uber locked down first: the depot. Houston also becomes the second market where this group goes head-to-head with Waymo. The competitive map is filling in market by market.
As always, that was not Uber’s only move this week
On 17 June, Stellantis, Wayve and Uber announced a global partnership to develop and deploy L4 robotaxis at scale. The division of labor is clean. Stellantis designs and manufactures the vehicles on its L4-Ready platforms, engineered for high-utilization driverless duty. Wayve provides the AI driver, built end-to-end so it adapts across regions without city-by-city mapping. Uber supplies the demand through its global network. It builds on relationships already in place: the Stellantis-Wayve L2++ agreement, and the Wayve-Uber deal to put autonomous rides on Uber in London, Tokyo and ten other cities starting this year.
And one more: WeRide and Uber to Launch Robotaxi Service in Zurich
The same day, WeRide and Uber announced a commercial robotaxi launch in greater Zurich, with Swiss operator Rydera running ground operations. Zurich is the fifth announced city under this deal and the second European market, two weeks after the duo named Madrid (with AVOMO). The structure is by now familiar, the three-layer split with a local operator carrying the ground work, the asset-light pattern the Chinese players keep repeating.
Source: WeRide
The timeline is the interesting part. WeRide’s, Eric Dong, told Zag Daily that fare-charging operations will launch in both Zurich and Madrid around December this year, with safety drivers behind the wheel, Madrid likely first. The combined testing fleet is being built toward roughly 36 vehicles, and Zurich’s fare-charging service will launch with about 12 vehicles this year at prices “comparable to Uber’s regular offering.” Riders request an AV in the Uber app the way they would an UberX, though wait times may run longer.
Then the line that connects straight back to a thesis we have been building for months. “The ultimate goal is always to remove the safety driver from the car,” Dong said. “Until that point, you’re not able to expand the fleet size materially because there’s labour costs.” WeRide aims to pull the safety driver six months after launch, targeting fully driverless service by mid-2027. The driver-out date is the real scaling date, and everything before it is preparation.
Dong was also candid about why Zurich, and it is a clean piece of unit-economics reasoning. “Zurich is a premium market with a very good profile for unit economics,” he said.
Then there is Tesla, taking the other side of every variable
Two Tesla items this week, and they connect tightly. First, Electrek, building on a Reuters public-records investigation, reported that Tesla presented self-published, inflated FSD safety statistics directly to regulators in Sweden and the Netherlands while lobbying for European approval. The headline figure, that FSD could have “saved 32,000 lives,” rests on the absurd assumption that every vehicle in the US, freight trucks and motorcycles included, would be replaced by an FSD-enabled Tesla. Ten of eleven independent traffic-safety researchers who reviewed the methodology called it misleading marketing, not a safety study. In a November 2024 letter to the Dutch authority RDW, Tesla linked the report and claimed greater FSD usage “leads to safer roads.” RDW approved FSD in April and is now seeking EU-wide approval on Tesla’s behalf.
Second, and this is where the thread tightens, Reuters reported that Sweden may oppose that EU-wide rollout outright. The Swedish Transport Administration recommended the EU’s Technical Committee on Motor Vehicles vote against approval unless Tesla removes the system’s ability to exceed posted speed limits, arguing that letting an automated system systematically speed undermines both the legal framework and the safety case for automation. The committee meets again on 30 June. So one of the two countries that received the inflated data is now the one threatening to block the rollout.
Put it against the momentum Tesla built through national recognition. As we covered in CW25, four European countries cleared FSD in roughly eight weeks, most recently Denmark, by accepting the original Dutch RDW approval rather than waiting for the EU. That path is fast, and it is fragile. If the EU ultimately rejects the system, the Dutch provisional approval lapses after six months and the national clearances built on it fall with it. Sweden’s objection is the first real sign the fast path can hit a wall.
And the pressure is not only in Europe. On 20 June, a driver in Katy, Texas told investigators his Tesla was on Autopilot when it failed to make a turn, accelerated through the brick wall of a home and killed a 76-year-old woman inside. 
Source: Electrek
This one needs care. It is the driver’s account, and investigators have not confirmed which system was involved, whether it was Autopilot or FSD (Supervised), or even that any of it was engaged at the time. And neither system is autonomous: both require an attentive driver ready to take over at all times. So I will not pin this on the technology. 
But the conditional is worth stating plainly. If it turns out FSD was active and still drove into that house, it becomes another instance of a distinction we keep returning to: a system can be good enough to make driving easier and more comfortable, and still nowhere near good enough to be trusted on the road unsupervised. Those are not the same threshold. 
Either way, the crash lands while NHTSA’s probe into roughly 2.9 million Teslas over FSD running red lights and driving the wrong way sits at an engineering analysis, the last formal step before a possible recall, with a separate probe examining whether Tesla reported Autopilot and FSD crashes properly at all. Tesla’s safety credibility is being tested on both sides of the Atlantic at once.
What this means and what to watch
Five positions to take from the week.
First, the operating layer is now a category, not a footnote. Avomo in Madrid, Rydera in Zurich, Element for Waymo in San Diego, an unnamed operator inside the Stellantis-Wayve-Uber framework. The operator is always local, and almost always separate. If you are mapping the value chain, give operations its own box.
Second, the integration question splits the field. The Chinese players (WeRide, Pony) go asset-light and let partners carry the fleet and operations. Waymo owns its cars but rents the operating layer to a specialist. Mobileye is betting it must own all of it. Watch whose model funds itself: asset-light spreads the capital, asset-heavy concentrates it.
Third, Uber stays true to its doctrine: maximize fragmentation so no single AV developer gains leverage. Partnering with WeRide, Wayve, Nuro and Stellantis at once is not indecision. It is a strategy to keep the market split.
Fourth, Waymo keeps advancing, but the recall is a reminder that the operating layer fights back. Hiring a fleet manager and pulling off freeways in the same week, with the freeway capability we celebrated in November now switched off, is the whole industry in miniature: scale and fragility, together.
Fifth, the question to watch is the one that opened this section. Who hands the rider to Uber, and who builds their own front door (the Waymo app, Mobileye’s Moovit). The next twelve months of deals will tell us whether owning the rider relationship is necessary to capture full value, or whether disaggregation becomes the template. And the near-term tell on Mobileye is concrete: a named city, a permit filing, a facility lease. Until those appear, it is a press release, not a plan.

🇨🇳 100K Robovans by 2030, and the OEMs moved in
Start with the news, this week DHL announced it has put its first licensed autonomous RoboVan into commercial service on public roads in China, enabled by Zelostech. 
Source: Zelostech
The vehicle, a Zelostech Z5 with a payload over one ton built for all-weather running, is not in a trial. It holds both public-road authorization and operational approval, sits in Changzhou’s first batch of commercial autonomous pilots, and runs a fixed 38-kilometre route between a DHL Express service center and a customer’s production site, roughly 90 minutes a trip, in continuous commercial service.
DHL’s regional manager Tian Haitian framed it as a step toward “normalizing intelligent logistics operations.” The detail easy to miss is the strategic one: DHL, a logistics company spanning 220-plus countries, is running a Chinese L4 stack in daily operations, building on Zelostech’s win at DHL’s Fast Forward Challenge in Dubai and earlier live trials in Singapore. A Western logistics giant is scaling Chinese autonomy, market by market.
And Changzhou is not the only place this partnership is now live. At DHL’s Advanced Regional Center in Singapore, the same Zelostech vehicles have moved from challenge pilot to daily operations: fully electric and driverless, running around the clock for hub-to-hub transfers between facilities on campus. Each carries up to 1.5 tons, is dispatched by app, and averages 40 trips and 28 kilometres a day. The number that matters is the operating cost, roughly half that of a diesel truck, with staff shifting from supervising drivers to managing the system and its data. That is the whole promise of the category in a single site: lower cost, continuous uptime, and a labor model that scales.
This is a category we have barely touched, and the numbers say we should have. Data from the China Communications and Transportation Association shows the country’s unmanned delivery fleet surged to 47,000 units in Q1 2026, up from roughly 10,000 in 2024, with more than 100 cities now permitting these vehicles on public roads. Zelos leads with a 53.2% share and 25,000 units in operation, followed by Neolix at 36.2% and 17,000 units. Soochow Securities projects the national fleet will pass 100,000 units by the end of this year; Tsinghua’s Zhang Tao puts demand at 1 million units by 2030. This is no longer a pilot category. It is one of the clearest closed commercial loops anywhere in autonomy, and the DHL route is what that loop looks like in practice.
Startups like Zelos and Neolix built the early lead on mature algorithms and operational data, but they hit a wall on mass production and supply chain. So the traditional automakers are moving in to do the part the startups cannot. Zelos co-founder Zhou Qing put the new division of labor cleanly at the OpenVAN launch: “Let vehicle specialists build the cars, and L4 experts safeguard them.”
The collaborations are stacking up fast. Dongfeng’s OpenVAN, which we logged in CW25, secured over 4,000 orders from eight partners including China Logistics Group, with first deliveries in August. GAC Commercial Vehicle struck an agreement with Neolix and rolled out RoboTruck, RoboVAN and RoboBUS platforms, debuting the X6T last-mile van with 6 cubic metres of cargo space. Changan’s light-commercial arm worked with JD Logistics on robovans that reached mass delivery in April, backed by 820 service stations nationwide. Wuling’s subsidiary rolled out its first mass-produced batch at the end of May. The pattern is consistent: AV specialist supplies the brain, automaker supplies the manufacturing and service network.
And the economics already land. A ZTO Express outlet in Guangxi cut delivery costs by more than 50% by deploying 12 Neolix robovans to handle 70% of local transit cargo, while solving a chronic shortage of night-shift couriers. That is the complete loop, lower cost and a labor gap filled, in a single deployment.
The forward-looking read: China’s unmanned-delivery sector has moved past whether the technology works to who can build it at scale, and the answer is OEM-plus-specialist partnerships, not startups alone.
On the car side rather than delivery WeRide’s WRD 3.0 system, co-developed with Bosch and running in a Chery Exeed Sterra ES, won the Tianjin round of the Second China Urban Intelligent Driving Competition, a sixth straight win and the only such streak in the contest’s history (we logged the previous five in our Q4 deep dive). It has now secured mass-production design wins across more than 30 vehicle types from Chery and GAC, and it is live proof of WeRide’s “double flywheel,” where L4 road data trains the L2+ ADAS that keeps winning these events.
And a second robotaxi move from China this week. Cao Cao Mobility, the Geely-affiliated ride-hailing platform, said it will launch a robotaxi service in Hong Kong, positioning the city as the global showcase for what it calls its RoboX strategy. The pieces have been assembling for a while. Earlier, at the Hong Kong International Auto Expo, Cao Cao presented Eva Cab, which it bills as China’s first natively developed robotaxi, and formally unveiled RoboX itself: a company-wide pivot to AI, aimed at building a physical AI mobility platform, anchored by a Double 100,000 Plan to put 100,000 robotaxis and 100,000 robovans on the road by 2030. It has since moved to back the strategy with structure, standing up an independent AI business unit, advertising for a Chief AI Officer, and registering a dedicated AI entity in Shanghai. The Hong Kong launch is how that strategy goes from slideware to street: a ride-hailing operator remaking itself into an AI-native robotaxi company, with Hong Kong as the international proving ground.
