Today’s interview starts with an overview of Seufert’s master’s thesis, which described how companies can build models to measure the effectiveness of creative (thus unlocking the value of generative AI) using ad platform black boxes. We then move on to why chat checkout failed, how Google has shifted search to AI, and the rapid evolution of ChatGPT ads. We also touch on Apple, Amazon, and AppLovin, and close with a fascinating thesis: is being pro-advertising correlated with being optimistic about AI and its impact on humanity?

*I know! It’s very exciting that we’re going to be the first outlet for this. *ES:* Three years ago, I think like a lot of people, I kind awoke to this idea that we’re standing at the precipice of this inflection point, not just in digital advertising, but I think in technology broadly and society broadly, and I got nervous that I just wouldn’t be able to operate at the frontier to provide commentary without better understanding machine learning, particularly in the digital advertising space. *(laughing) Went to school in Boston, as it were.*

I think it made it a lot more concrete and a lot more actionable, but I don’t think I would recommend this for anybody who is in my particular set of circumstances with remote work and my wife works remotely, and my wife is like a saint and allowing me to do this kind of stuff and young kids and all that, but it works for me.

So what I wrote about was we’re sitting in a stage of the digital advertising market where essentially all of the big platforms are converging to this total end-to-end automation paradigm.

*The black boxes.*

*ES:* The black boxes. But if you kind of dive into a lot of the recommendation system research, which is essentially like the same as ad serving, it’s all the retrieval and ranking methodologies that are applied for selecting ad candidates, it’s the same as it’s used for selecting content candidates in just normal recommendation systems.

*It’s always been weird, it happened for historical reasons, that these were two different tech trees, but it’s been clear for actually a long time that if you’re recommending an ad, you can recommend content and “What’s the actual difference?”.*

*ES:* Right, it’s just the objective function that’s chosen. One is, “I want to optimize for this conversion”, and then the other is, “I want to optimize for some sort of engagement metric”, but essentially you’re using the same technologies, the same methodologies, and so if you dig in all that, it’s gotten supremely sophisticated.

But all of this sits behind this event horizon past which light cannot escape, with the light being your visibility into what the ad platform is doing. It’s totally opaque, and it’s gotten to the point where like you essentially have one lever of control, maybe two. The one lever of control is the creative and then the other lever of control is whatever your objective is that you tell the ad platform. And I say “maybe one”, because you might not have creative.

*That’s right, because generative AI’s coming along and it’s the most obvious application of just make the creative for you and it’s seemed like it’s been the case for a while, that if you’re an advertiser, “Just embrace the void”, has been the answer and that’s part of what makes this interesting. It’s kind of like a, “Maybe not, maybe that’s not the only way”.*

*ES:* I think it is, that’s ultimately the point of this DeCANT’s methodology. But yeah, what are you going to do? Are you going to fight the ocean?

*Right.*

*ES:* This is the direction of travel, you are a single advertiser, you control some amount of budget — this is where things are heading. It’s obviously better for the platforms, it is probably better for you, so whether you like it or not, whether you would agree with it or not, whether you think things could be done in a superior way or not, it’s kind of irrelevant, this is where things are headed, and it’s obviously working out for the platforms, I’m sure we’ll talk about earnings in a second.

So if you accept that, and if you say, “Look, I have no control over this”, and also, “I understand that the cost of creative production is going to zero”, whether the creative production is handled by the ad platform itself, or whether I’m still creating my creatives, if I’m just using third-party tools to do that, it’s trending towards zero.

For another reason, you’re going to work with more than one platform and you want to have consistency across them. *It’s the concept though. It’s using the network’s ad server as a teacher for your own models, because they’ve reduced so many variables that actually it’s just like an objective function.*

*ES:* Exactly, and the idea of pre-testing, right? So just to test against some sort of model that should mimic what the broader system does.

*Exactly.*

*ES:* But I think there’s another piece here too — if I’m talking about this to a room of brand marketers, I say, “Hey, good news, this allows you to take control of creative production and control your brand messaging everywhere”, they get really happy. If I talk to the hardcore DTC performance marketers, I say, “Good news, make everything, build everything, you can just shoot the fire hose at this thing and it’s not going to blow up your testing account, it’s not going to blow up your testing budget, but you should be making everything, you should be exploring the entire surface area of all possible messaging and pixel combinations that could represent your product — shoot the fire hose, blast everything at it because the cost of producing creative is essentially zero”.
