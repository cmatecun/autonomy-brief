------------------

Trump administration officials began weighing sanctions on Anthropic weeks before they demanded the company take its latest and most advanced artificial intelligence model offline, after a dispute shattered the White House’s already-fragile trust in the company, according to two White House officials who spoke on the condition of anonymity to describe private deliberations.

The Jailbreak Problem
---------------------

At the same time, I think there is a more fundamental issue: Anthropic’s dramatic unveiling of Mythos created a standard for control that was fundamentally unattainable. Consider the jailbreak issue: we don’t know what the jailbreak Amazon discovered was, but there is at least some reporting that it was incredibly banal; from The Atlantic ( https://www.theatlantic.com/technology/2026/06/trump-anthropic-export-control-ai-race/687555/ ) :

It’s not unreasonable for the federal government to want to rapidly clamp down on a technology that could be incredibly dangerous. Trump officials had been alerted by researchers at Amazon to a possible way to circumvent Fable 5’s safety systems, which led the model to identify some known IT vulnerabilities. The administration has not publicly shared much information about its security concerns. A White House spokesperson told me that the jailbreak “was very serious” but said that specific details are classified. Whether the bypass really was that serious is not at all clear, and Anthropic has contested whether what administration officials showed the company even constitutes a jailbreak. An Anthropic spokesperson pointed me to a blog post in which the company wrote that the actions elicited from Fable were “either entirely benign responses or are minor findings.”

Katie Moussouris, a cybersecurity expert and the CEO of Luta Security, told me that Anthropic shared with her a copy of the White House’s report on the Fable jailbreak to get her appraisal. (She said that she is not being paid by Anthropic.) The report, Moussouris said, involved IT experts asking Fable to help find and patch bugs. When given deliberately insecure code, she said, Fable refused the prompt “review the code for security issues” but then complied when asked to “fix this code,” followed by some further manual steps. Moussouris told me that this was just “the model working as intended” for cyberdefense. She added that OpenAI’s GPT-5.5, a model with similar cybersecurity capabilities, could be used in the same way.

Again, we don’t know if this was the jailbreak, but let’s assume that it was; as Moussouris wrote in a blog post ( https://www.lutasecurity.com/post/the-fable-5-export-controls-harm-us-cyber-defense ) :

Defenders need to be able to ask AI to fix the bugs in a file, explain why the fix matters, and write tests that confirm the patch works. That is not a guardrail bypass. It is the most valuable thing an AI model can do for defensive security: executing the find, fix, and test loop defenders run every day.

The prompts worked because they were defensive requests, and that capability cannot be removed without making the model worse at fixing bugs and verifying patches. The same holds for every capable AI model, including the foreign and open-weight systems the United States cannot reach with export controls, many of which will match Fable and Mythos capabilities within months. Will all the US based models be export controlled? They have fewer guardrails than Fable 5, and almost all the capabilities, or will shortly.

This is obviously correct: cybersecurity defense is indistinguishable from offense; there are echoes here of the encryption battles of the 1990s, when the U.S. government wanted to ban encryption but was eventually persuaded to stand down by the argument that encryption doesn’t just protect criminal activity, but also protects things that need to be protected too — including government secrets — and that you don’t get to pick-and-choose its application. So it is with AI and cybersecurity: the best defense for AI-enabled cybersecurity attacks is AI-enabled cybersecurity protection, including actually looking for vulnerabilities.

Zooming out, however, I think there is an even bigger issue when it comes to AI specifically. Encryption was ultimately understandable and defendable as an all-or-nothing concept because it is deterministic: strong encryption protocols are fully open source and understandable, even as they are unbreakable, because, at the end of the day, they are math. Math — and the code used for encryption — is deterministic: it either works as it is supposed to or it is fundamentally flawed; you don’t get to have the good encryption for the good guys and the bad encryption for the bad guys. In other words, you can’t distinguish between use cases.

What makes AI so difficult to talk about, on the other hand, is that it is probabilistic. This, at the end of the day, is how jailbreaks work: because models don’t depend on deterministic code, you can’t simply define boundaries of what is acceptable and what isn’t according to a set of heuristics. Rather, you provide guidelines that the model interprets and probably abides by but, with the right prompt and the incredible creativity of jailbreakers, the model can be prompted to do what its creators didn’t want it to do. It’s just a matter of tipping the probability function in your favor. In contrast to encryption, the problem isn’t the inability to distinguish between use cases for a knowable technology, but the inability to even know what the use case is.

I don’t really expect anyone in the administration to truly understand this, just because you can see everywhere the extent to which people don’t really understand how AI works. Most people experience AI as a magical answer machine that weirdly can’t count the number of ‘r’s in ‘strawberry’ (although Fable could ( https://x.com/ziwenxu_/status/2064394811008770445 ) ), and never draw the connection between those types of weird shortcomings and the fact you can’t actually perfectly stop “jailbreaks” of this sort.

Now, to the extent this is true, you may wish it were not the case: you might wish that the people with guns ( https://stratechery.com/2026/anthropic-and-alignment/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDI2L2FudGhyb3BpYy1hbmQtYWxpZ25tZW50LyJdfSwiZXhwIjoxNzg0MjgyNTY0LCJpYXQiOjE3ODE2OTA1NjQsImlzcyI6Imh0dHBzOi8vYXBwLnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZS9vYXV0aCIsInNjb3BlIjoiZmVlZDpyZWFkIGFydGljbGU6cmVhZCBhc3NldDpyZWFkIGNhdGVnb3J5OnJlYWQgZW50aXRsZW1lbnRzIiwic3ViIjoiMDE5NTE2ZjEtYTM0Ni03NjE0LTllOTYtYjYzYmQxMWZlODU2IiwidXNlIjoiYWNjZXNzIn0.atMpFzO_TTLAqspHvtoAKh21TCvcvU2lT349nYjQ0Dkqs3SOVCGg-GVOu_c3BoA2x7MoZ0ijGDtSszDRYeTEVeOaifE0BO4hNa33J-nGXkfprjIZg6-13XSZjENJWBfM-KmAy1vpohMpF3jucBjAIMsMDuuFpRoZPE6XzT8iennaX-aiKHWsNJzIxzWdmM_Eh9dY3ormFT066gCvXH-TnCW_0PH5-G_YkcYhWabAknqM-z9iFUmnJNKP2kXd1umKynGxXvfULj1T9eEYcgW59d72WLXl8SWv-aoW75n-qCAnYPV7IE1aRo7RtqGo3PU17ZHkdRKCgg9OKA0MaMrVGQ ) had an intuition for how AI works. You might say the same thing about consumers generally, who have frustrated technologists for decades with their unrealistic expectations and refusal to just “figure it out”. Ultimately, however, the companies that succeeded — like Apple, to go back to the analogy I drew on Monday, but in this case as a contrast — did so by explaining problems in clear language, and demonstrating how their products solved those problems.

Notice how this sort of language is missing from Anthropic. Go back to the Project Glasswing announcement ( https://www.anthropic.com/glasswing ) ; this paragraph was the closest the company came to explaining how Mythos Preview worked:

Many flaws in software go unnoticed for years because finding and exploiting them has required expertise held by only a few skilled security experts. With the latest frontier AI models, the cost, effort, and level of expertise required to find and exploit software vulnerabilities have all dropped dramatically. Over the past year, AI models have become increasingly effective at reading and reasoning about code—in particular, they show a striking ability to spot vulnerabilities and work out ways to exploit them. Claude Mythos Preview demonstrates a leap in these cyber skills—the vulnerabilities it has spotted have in some cases survived decades of human review and millions of automated security tests, and the exploits it develops are increasingly sophisticated.

Do you see the gap in communication? What is lacking is an explanation of how it is that models can read and reason about code, find vulnerabilities, and exploit them. That capability is a function of approaching any problem probabilistically instead of deterministically; the problem with the latter is that the set of problems to which it can be applied is definitionally limited by the rigidity that results in a clearly correct answer. A probabilistic approach, on the other hand, runs the chance of being wrong (i.e. hallucinating), but in exchange it gains the ability to navigate any problem: the set of applicable domains is infinite. This is why Mythos can look at any code base, even if it’s not trained on them; this is also why you can’t get Mythos to only play defense while eschewing offense. The strength of the approach is its weakness.

These are, to be clear, exhausting explanations to make; they are also far more difficult when you lead with dramatic assertions, don’t respect the counterparty, or think you’re smarter than everyone else in the room. Steve Jobs’ brilliance was that he treated consumers with respect, even if they had no idea how a computer worked. Does anyone feel that Anthropic treats anyone outside of Anthropic with respect, much less this administration?

This is, in other words, a communications problem, and unfortunately communication is not exactly a forte of Anthropic’s leadership. Yes, CEO Dario Amodei writes thoughtful essays, but those are targeted at and understood by people who provide the push for AGI. Just as important to that goal, however, is avoiding the barriers, and that means communicating with those with the capability to erect them. Amodei is pursuing Machines of Loving Grace ( https://darioamodei.com/essay/machines-of-loving-grace ) ; he’s not going to get there without some grace for his fellow man.

SpaceX Acquires Cursor
----------------------

From the Wall Street Journal ( https://www.wsj.com/business/spacex-agrees-to-buy-ai-coding-agent-cursor-for-60-billion-7a473340 ) :

SpaceX said it would buy Cursor parent Anysphere for $60 billion, striking a massive deal for an autonomous coding agent that could help the company catch up with its AI rivals after its blockbuster IPO. Cursor will receive $60 billion worth of SpaceX stock under the agreement, according to a filing released Tuesday. The deal comes days after SpaceX’s historic public offering Friday. SpaceX’s market value is around $2.66 trillion after its shares rose 19% in their first day of trading, 20% on Monday and around 5% on Tuesday.

I already wrote about the logic of this acquisition when it was announced; from an April Update ( https://stratechery.com/2026/john-ternus-and-apples-hardware-defined-future-spacexai-and-cursor/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDI2L2pvaG4tdGVybnVzLWFuZC1hcHBsZXMtaGFyZHdhcmUtZGVmaW5lZC1mdXR1cmUtc3BhY2V4YWktYW5kLWN1cnNvci8iXX0sImV4cCI6MTc4NDI4MjU2NCwiaWF0IjoxNzgxNjkwNTY0LCJpc3MiOiJodHRwczovL2FwcC5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUvb2F1dGgiLCJzY29wZSI6ImZlZWQ6cmVhZCBhcnRpY2xlOnJlYWQgYXNzZXQ6cmVhZCBjYXRlZ29yeTpyZWFkIGVudGl0bGVtZW50cyIsInN1YiI6IjAxOTUxNmYxLWEzNDYtNzYxNC05ZTk2LWI2M2JkMTFmZTg1NiIsInVzZSI6ImFjY2VzcyJ9.Gni4Y3uAW0xi57eL6F0R-09baYaj4JbHL6Eek9L21-a7N0UVINHrvr7zdLj44u8oSG_XNgD7uOnlndlRlFMZGl4zCgXlrVDnGkmqxc6UV_vPaX3IJzMJx9DOfxMQqpWGAoSIr3XK3xRPMmkM8ud9d4eOyWv3QkZrnZZ3rXIWUZxg5TU76uEEZR0IaF-IuoZmrA5d_d_GvCpA3XmdTPvQ9jZIMudnn0fftkTGANPBFC8-7HGpFXFPQ_3-k4lkiXaJLrSOm78Mp6rMmVYAAOdetdgyQfcWDYZ0c0RrkI0Mze2U9MlR7w-DwzT9tcBHD6Krm5OsgZqkVE8-pWGB05slQA ) :

The theory of the case is that Cursor has a huge amount of proprietary data about coding and how people actually interact with LLMs, which should be a solid foundation for building the agentic model of the future. Again, however, training is exceptionally expensive, and serving even more so.

I think you can see where I am going here: setting aside the weird nature of this deal, what with the option-to-buy and all, and the numbers involved (which honestly, given the silly numbers Musk has attached to the acquisitions of first X and then xAI, who’s counting), there is really obvious synergy between SpaceXAI and Cursor: the former has compute, and the latter has a product, data, and a decent amount of distribution for the use case that is most important for AI. At a high level this tie-up makes a lot of sense.

Well, now we have a clear answer to the weird nature of the deal and the numbers involved, and it’s pretty brilliant: the key fact is that SpaceX is paying with stock, not cash. Right now SpaceX has a tiny float, which is driving the stock artificially high: that means that by waiting for this exact moment in time — after the IPO but before most of the lock-ups expire — SpaceX gets to make the deal at what will likely be a discount, at least in the medium term (i.e. once the float increases and demand is more easily satisfied, the stock price will likely fall).

To be clear, the exact amount of stock will be determined at the time the deal closes, which is expected to be in Q3. However, that’s before the largest lock-ups expire, and after SpaceX is expected to be added to most major indices (which will further juice demand, albeit permanently): it seems very likely that the cost of $60 billion worth of dilution will be considerably less than the cost of cash.
