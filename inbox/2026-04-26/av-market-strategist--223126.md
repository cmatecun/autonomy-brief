Tesla’s Q1/2026 Earnings Call: $25B Capex, Robotaxi Timeline and HW3 Admission
Tesla's Q1 looked fine on the headline numbers. The hour-long call was the most subdued Musk performance in years. Normally a Tesla earnings call is wildly optimistic timelines and framings like "robots will solve poverty". This one was different. As Bloomberg put it, instead of the usual rhapsodies, Musk "spent much of the call talking about constraints on his vision for Tesla." That tone shift is the most important thing that happened on the call. Hold it in mind. We will come back to it. Numbers first.
Source: App Economy Insights  
Revenue $22.4B, up 15.8% YoY, after a difficult 2025 Automotive revenue is up 16%. The Energy segment saw a 12% revenue decline but “Services and Other” is up 42% to $3.8B. This was supported by moving from $8,000 for lifetime access to $99/month for the FSD subscription, plus strong Supercharger usage. FSD subscriptions hit 1.28 million, up 51% YoY.
Gross margin reached 21% (+5pp YoY), Operating margin improved to 4% (+2pp YoY), Free cash flow was a surprise positive $1.4B. Quarter-end cash, equivalents, and short-term investments at $44.7B.
A $1.4B FCF surprise plus $44.7B in cash should look like comfort. Musk used the call to argue Tesla needs this war chest because Tesla is actively transforming from an EV company into "an AI and robotics company," and the cost of that transition is showing up in capex. 2026 capex is now guided at over $25B, up from the $20B given last quarter.
Source: Bloomberg
The three named buckets: FSD, the Cybercab and Robotaxi network, and Optimus/Dojo. Management warned free cash flow will likely turn negative for the rest of 2026 once the capex hits. That setup primes everything that comes next.
Robotaxi: Dallas and Houston live, the other five quietly delayed
Tesla launched Robotaxi service in Dallas and Houston on April 18, the first expansion since Austin. So far, so on-promise. Three months ago, the prior shareholder letter committed to seven US cities by end of 1H 2026: Dallas, Houston, Phoenix, Miami, Orlando, Tampa, Las Vegas.
Source: Tesla Shareholder Presentation Q4/2025
As of this week, only the first two are live. There was a wording change in the newest shareholder letter: the remaining five have moved from "1H 2026" to a vaguer "preparations underway." Two months left in the half. This looks like a slip on five of seven announced markets.
Source: Tesla Shareholder Presentation Q1/2026
These deployments are still extremely small. According to Brad Templeton, Independent observers have noticed only 2 different vehicles in each city, based on licence plate tracking, and they are only in operation some of the day. The number of vehicles in Austin has increased from 9 to 13 recently, but also not all are operating at all times.
The most striking line on the call was Musk saying Robotaxi "likely will not see material revenue until at least 2027." This is the same Musk who previously claimed half the US population would have access by year-end. 
Year-end becomes a new promise of "dozen states" on this call. With five of seven announced cities already seeming to slip and the financial contribution explicitly written off through 2026, the gap between January's rhetoric and April's rhetoric is huge.
On the upside: Cybercab production started. First unit February 17, continuous production now underway. The Road to Autonomy team toured the Giga Texas line and described it as a serious build, with capacity being readied for "hundreds of units per week" and 60 units already on campus. 
HW3: the most expensive admission Tesla has buried in an earnings call
The biggest admission of the quarter: Hardware 3 will not deliver unsupervised FSD.  Tesla is offering HW3 owners two paths: a discounted trade-in for an AI4 vehicle, or a free computer-and-camera retrofit, with "microfactories" being stood up in cities to handle the swap. A v14 Lite version of FSD is promised for HW3 by end of June so those cars keep supervised updates. But the unsupervised promise, the one that justified $8K FSD packages sold to roughly four million owners since 2019, is officially withdrawn.
This will ripple. Existing class-action and individual claims already cite the 2016, 2018, and 2019 statements that the cars had “all the hardware necessary” for full autonomy. Tesla just admitted, on its own call, that this was not true for HW3. The retrofit pathway is the attempt to cap legal exposure: take the upgrade, the original promise is fulfilled in a different form. Open questions: how many of those four million owners actually paid for FSD, what per-unit retrofit cost is at scale, and whether the microfactory rollout moves fast enough to stop the legal claims from compounding.
Add this to the capex picture and the math gets uglier. Every dollar swapping a 2019 car’s compute is a dollar not training the next FSD model or scaling the Cybercab line. The HW3 retrofit is a distraction tax on the autonomy roadmap Tesla just told us it needs to pour $25B into.
So why was Musk so moderate?
This is the question worth sitting with. We are used to optimism and aggressive timelines. This call was constraints, "incredibly fast pace would be four months" production-line metaphors, and a 2027 revenue caveat on the product Musk previously said would put a Tesla in front of half of America. Three hypothetical explanations:
Theory 1: Mindset shift. 
Musk has decided to stop making promises he cannot keep. Possible but unlikely. The pattern is too consistent across two decades.
Theory 2: Measured posture for the SpaceX IPO. 
SpaceX is targeting an IPO as early as mid-June. If Musk shows up to a Tesla call making outrageous claims, every claim he makes during the SpaceX roadshow gets discounted by association. Looking measured on Tesla protects the credibility he needs for the SpaceX prospectus. This is plausible and probably explains some of the tone.
Theory 3: He is talking Tesla down because SpaceX is merging with Tesla. 
Earlier this year, a SpaceX-xAI merger was rumored alongside a Tesla merger. The SpaceX-xAI merger then materialized. Tesla and SpaceX already overlap operationally (the TERAFAB chip JV is the clearest tell). Tesla holds a stake in xAI. Tesla has the highest free cash flow profile of the three. 
Musk owns roughly 20% of Tesla, while at SpaceX he holds about 42% of the equity but controls roughly 79% of the voting power through super-voting shares. In theory, if Tesla were merged into SpaceX at a $2T valuation and that voting structure carried over, Musk could extend that control to the combined entity despite not owning a majority of the economics. Whether such a structure would be feasible is another question entirely, but as a strategic thought experiment, I find it interesting.
The structural logic for a "merge-up" runs in Musk's favor if Tesla's standalone valuation is lower at the moment of merger than the optimistic Tesla narrative implies. Wedbush's Dan Ives pegs merger probability at 70 to 80% with a 2027 timeline conditional on SpaceX IPO. Polymarket has the implied probability of a June-30 announcement under 5%. Both can be true. 
The honest read of Q1: Tesla's autonomy story is now an AI-spend story for 2026 and 2027. The FSD subscription line is real and high-margin. The Robotaxi geographic story is slipping. The Cybercab production line is an option, not a forecast. HW3 is a liability, and the most strategically interesting thing on the call was a tone shift.
Now let’s look at what other US players are doing.
Zoox is doing airport trips and expands SF operating area
Zoox is testing robotaxi rides with employees as passengers to and from the Las Vegas airport. The plan is to offer the service publicly in the near future.
In other news, Zoox has announced they are significantly expanding its service area in San Francisco. Zoox cars will be available in many of the Eastern neighborhoods in the city, including Marina, North Beach, Chinatown, Pacific Heights, and the Embarcadero, quadrupling the service area.
Zoox is still not available to everyone in San Francisco. You can sign up for the waitlist if you are interested in trying a Zoox vehicle. Early riders are part of the “Zoox Explores” program, as the company slowly rolls out the vehicles in San Francisco.
Avride’s fleet size: 200 vehicles
Avride posted on LinkedIn that it has a fleet of 200 robotaxis. 
Source: LinkedIN
I think they are counting every vehicle in their fleet, not only the ones providing rides, but still interesting to see the number.
MOIA Expands Into New US Markets via Strategic Partnership with Beep
The most strategically interesting US announcement of the week, and the one that got the least US press. MOIA America and Beep announced a multi-year partnership to deploy ID. Buzz vehicles with Mobileye's self-driving system in US public-transit markets, starting in Orlando. 
Validation testing has begun in Lake Nona, initial rides in 2026 with onboard operators. Combined target: up to 5,000 vehicles in multiple US markets over the next decade. 
What to make out of all this?
Tesla’s Q1 is not a disaster and it is not a victory. It is a confession quarter. The HW3 admission is the most expensive line item Tesla has buried inside a “we are moving forward” narrative. The year-end city scorecard is below the bar Tesla set in January.
And the competitors are moving. Zoox is out of hibernation. We had not heard  serious announcements from them for a long time, but the last weeks suggest they’re back. MOIA, just landed Beep as a US partner with a 5,000-vehicle target and validation already underway in Orlando.
The race is not over yet, and it keeps being interesting!

China: Another Big News Week for Pony.ai
Pony.ai stacked four announcements into one week, and each one tightens the same thesis I have been writing for months. The Chinese AV players are not just expanding. They are building the global export model for autonomy.
Pony.AI goes driverless in Dubai 
Pony AI has deployed driverless robotaxis in Dubai, marking a key step in its Middle East expansion. The clear next step: a public, fare-charging service in 2H 2026, scaling to "several hundred vehicles." This follows a partnership agreement reached with Dubai's Roads and Transport Authority (RTA) in May 2025 to deploy its robotaxi fleet in the region and the testing permit obtained in September 2025 from the RTA.
3,000 vehicles by year-end, half overseas. 
On the back of mass-production of the Toyota bZ4X Gen-7 with GAC Toyota, Pony.ai reaffirmed a 3,000+ robotaxi global-fleet target by end of 2026, roughly half deployed overseas. The split across Dubai, Luxembourg, Singapore, Zagreb, and other markets has not been disclosed.
Chinese AV operators are not building for China alone. They are positioning to be the global exporter of autonomy. Outbound is the strategic priority.
The economic logic underneath the export push is straightforward. Mainland China is huge but the most competitive AV market on earth, with Baidu, WeRide, Didi and others all fighting for the same Tier-1 cities. Labor is cheap enough that human-driven taxi fares set a hard ceiling on what robotaxi fares can charge. Margin per ride is structurally better where labor costs are higher. Dubai, Luxembourg, and Singapore all qualify. Mainland China is the validation lab. The money is offshore.
The $33,000 Pony AI Robotaxi
Pony.ai took the stage at Auto China 2026 on April 24, and CEO James Peng dropped the headline number: the total vehicle cost (base vehicle plus AD kit) for Pony.ai's 2027-generation robotaxi in China will fall below RMB 230,000. That is roughly $33,000 in USD. Lower than the starting price of a locally-produced Tesla Model 3 in China. This is the number Western OEMs will struggle to match.
For overseas markets, Pony.ai will offer a tailored version of the platform, presumably with hardware adjustments for local regulatory and certification regimes. So the cost for the overseas vehicles will probably be slightly higher.
The L4 light-duty truck nobody saw coming
Pony was not done. Also at Auto China 2026, Pony unveiled an L4 electric light-duty truck, billed as the world’s first 100% automotive-grade, fully redundant light truck purpose-built for L4 autonomous driving, developed in collaboration with CATL. Specs that matter:
Built on CATL’s Kunshi chassis platform, with 18 cubic meters of cargo capacity.
Battery range 320 to 450 km.
Engineered for full-scenario, all-weather L4 driving.
Service life of 600,000 km or 20,000 hours.
Claimed 40% to 50% reduction in freight cost per kilometer compared with human-driven equivalents.
Target scenarios: parcel distribution, supermarket restocking, and cold-chain foodservice delivery.
Source: Pony AI
The detail undersold in the press releases: this truck runs the same technology stack as Pony.ai’s Gen-7 Robotaxis and is intended for deployment in the same operating regions, enabling both platforms to leverage the same operational backbone, including energy replenishment, ground support, service centers, and remote assistance.
Pony is not building a separate truck company. It is amortizing one stack across two vehicle categories. That is exactly the move you want when the long-term thesis is “we are the global exporter of autonomy.”
And there was more on the trucking side
April 16 in Guangzhou, Pony.ai signed a strategic partnership with GAC Commercial Vehicle. GAC brings the vehicle platforms, manufacturing systems, and steer-by-wire chassis; Pony brings the L4 stack and operational scenarios. Stated goal: large-scale commercialization of autonomous trucks in the Greater Bay Area and nationwide logistics. This is another partnership with a Chinese truck OEM for Pony AI.
DeepWay: $310M pre-IPO, global investor base, same playbook
DeepWay expanded its pre-IPO round to over $310M. They build smart electric heavy-duty trucks running Baidu Apollo's AD stack.
They already have L4 platooning running production cargo across multiple Chinese regions via the Tianji Yanxing intelligent platooning system. By June 30, 2025, DeepWay had delivered roughly 6,400 vehicles, all from commercial orders.
The cap table is the part most people will skim past, and it is the most important detail of the round. The latest tranche was led by UAE-based Stone, with participation from Australian superannuation fund NGS Super and Xiamen Guosheng Fund. Existing shareholders ABC Impact and Nanjing Ronghe Capital increased their stakes. 
Stated use of proceeds: core operations, technology, and explicitly named deployment in the Middle East, Europe, the US, Australia, and Asia-Pacific. This is the moment a Chinese autonomous-trucking company stops being a Chinese story. The investor base is global, the expansion plan is global, and the capital is being raised on the assumption that the export model works. Hong Kong remains the working IPO-venue assumption.
Same playbook across both Pony.ai and DeepWay: build the stack in China, prove unit economics on Chinese routes, export.
What this means
Pony.ai compressed its full strategic narrative into a one-week run of announcements. Sub-$33K total robotaxi cost on a 2027 horizon. A 3,000-vehicle global fleet with half overseas. A second commercial-vehicle OEM partnership. A first-of-its-kind L4 light truck on the same operational backbone as the robotaxi. 
Compare that to the Tesla week, where the news was a $25B capex commitment, a HW3 admission, a five-city slip, and a Musk who told investors not to expect material Robotaxi revenue until 2027.
The Pony.ai story is "we have figured it out, we are now scaling and exporting." That gap will keep widening for at least the next few months. Next data point: the Q1 2026 earnings from Pony and WeRide.
Same playbook visible in trucking with DeepWay, except with a global investor base writing the checks. The Chinese export model for autonomy is not a thesis anymore. It is the operating reality.

🎧 Autonomy Insiders
