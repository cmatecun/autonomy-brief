Uber’s pitch: turn millions of drivers into a moving sensor grid
One important news item of the week came from Uber CTO Praveen Neppalli Naga at TechCrunch’s StrictlyVC event. Uber wants to eventually outfit its drivers’ personal vehicles with cameras and sensors to soak up real-world data for AV companies and other AI model trainers. Naga framed it as a longer-term extension of AV Labs, the program Uber announced in late January. For now, AV Labs runs on a small fleet of sensor-equipped cars Uber operates itself, separate from the driver network. The driver-as-sensor expansion is “the direction we want to go eventually,” Naga said, with two pre-conditions: Uber needs to figure out the sensor kits and how they work, and every state needs regulatory clarity on what “sensors” and “data sharing” mean.
Naga’s framing of the bottleneck is the part to take seriously. “The bottleneck is data,” he said. AV companies need to physically drive cars to collect specific scenarios, and most of them lack the capital to deploy fleets at the geographic scope they actually need. He gave the school-intersection example: a partner could request “data at this time of day, at this San Francisco intersection” to train a model, instead of building the data-collection capacity itself. Around this insight Uber is building what Naga called an “AV cloud,” a library of labeled sensor data partners can query. There is also a “shadow mode” feature where a partner’s trained model runs against real Uber trips, simulating how an AV would have performed without putting one on the road.
On the commercial framing, Naga said: “Our goal is not to make money out of this data. We want to democratize it.” But given the obvious commercial value of what Uber is building, let’s see how long this positioning will last. Free and democratized is the launch positioning. Once partners are integrated into the AV cloud and running shadow-mode simulations against Uber trips, switching costs go up and Uber’s ability to monetize, whether through usage fees, equity stakes, exclusivity premiums, or revenue share, goes up with them. Uber already has equity in numerous AV players. Treat the democratization claim as accurate today and entirely revisable tomorrow.
The execution problem is also harder than the pitch. Independent drivers are not a controllable fleet. Sensor calibration, vehicle uptime, data quality, regional coverage, and compensation all become variable across millions of small business owners, which is partly why Naga himself said the regulatory and sensor-kit work has to come first. Harry Campbell  of The Driverless Digest made this point cleanly: turning a decentralized contractor base into a reliable, high-quality training network is messy and hard to standardize. The idea works better with fleets first. Uber already works with Alto, Tower, VGM, and other fleet partners running thousands of vehicles. Standardizing sensor hardware across a few dozen fleet operators is an order of magnitude easier than across 8 million drivers, and the data quality is higher. 
The interesting comparison is Lyft. Lyft owns Flexdrive, an internal fleet of approximately 15,000 vehicles, and is therefore structurally better positioned to run a controlled sensor-grid pilot than Uber on day one. Speaking of Lyft…
Lyft’s data play: maps, fleet management, and a hybrid ecosystem
Lyft put its own framing on the same problem this week with a blog post titled “Lyft’s mobility expertise powers AV partners.” The argument is structural and is worth reading on its own. Knowing a partner with training data, or with marketplace access, is not enough. AV scaling requires the end-to-end experience of operating mobility at scale: logistics, regulation, field operations, custom mapping, rider trust, plus rich road data. Lyft is positioning against partners who can offer one piece versus the full stack.
The numbers in the post are the part to take seriously. Flexdrive operates roughly 15,000 highly-utilized vehicles that drive more than 550 million miles per year. Lyft says those vehicles have already delivered nearly 3 million miles of AV-relevant training data to autonomy partners, equivalent to over 157,000 hours of real-world operation. That sits on top of billions of additional miles from non-Flexdrive drivers on the platform each year. The data-quality framing is more pointed than the typical pitch: the most valuable AV training data is not highway miles, it is edge cases. Construction zones, fallen objects, unexpected lane closures, gated communities, tolls, complex urban intersections. Active rideshare fleets with real pickups and dropoffs encounter those scenarios at meaningfully higher density than dedicated data-collection fleets, because Flexdrive vehicles are already running the operational profile AV partners actually need to learn.
The mapping product is where the post breaks the most new ground. Lyft Maps powers pickup and dropoff selection, real-time road conditions, and Lyft’s proprietary street-level imagery program FleetView, which Lyft says covers 95% of roads it operates on, with 2-week data freshness that outpaces other major mapping platforms. Lyft claims FleetView captures millions of miles per day across hundreds of markets at a 6-7x cost advantage over the industry standard. 
Source: Lyft
Behind it sits a team of hundreds of geospatial experts curating data continuously, and Lyft cites more than 2.5 million OpenStreetMap edits in 2025 alone, making it one of the top OSM contributors globally.
The offer to AV partners is bundled into five concrete components. Fleet management via Flexdrive (44,000+ vehicles managed across the platform’s history, with sourcing, maintenance, AV mission control, field support, and depot operations). Training data, both existing real-world ridehail data and custom-fit collection fleets equipped with camera, radar, and lidar to partner specs. Marketplace and mapping expertise to optimize AV routes and reduce partners’ own mapping work. Lyft’s pickup and dropoff insights, real-time road conditions, and FleetView imagery can reduce thousands of miles of mapping requirements and improve reliability. Policy, regulatory, and relationship expertise built from years of rideshare, bikes, and scooters work with regulators, venues, and airports. Field support operations, including manual assists from Lyft’s hybrid driver network when an AV gets stuck.
The strategic read is that Lyft is making a different bet than Uber on the same underlying problem. Uber pitched a moving sensor grid via 8 million independent drivers (eventually) and an AV cloud partners can query (now). Lyft is selling the operating system around AV partners. The Flexdrive fleet, the maps, the OSM edits, the field ops, the regulatory liaison. Both companies are converging on the same conclusion: the autonomy stack is upstream, the operating layer is where the margin sits for both companies.

Staying with Uber and partners: Nuro permits, Lucid’s 75 vehicles, and Avride under Investigation
Nuro had a productive regulatory week. The California DMV modified Nuro’s driverless AV permit to include Lucid Gravity vehicles, authorizing testing in Santa Clara and San Mateo counties at speeds up to 45 mph, day or night, without a safety operator. Nuro also secured a CPUC drivered pilot permit, which authorizes pilot testing with a safety driver and passengers, no paid rides yet. Driverless testing is expected to start later this year. This is real operational progress, but Nuro still needs two more approvals before Uber can offer paid driverless rides in California: a deployment permit from the DMV and a driverless ride-hailing permit from the CPUC. Neither has been filed.
Lucid, Nuros OEM partner, disclosed in its Q1 earnings call this week that it has delivered 75 engineering vehicles to Nuro and Uber, with mileage accumulation underway in several cities. Lucid says it remains on track for commercial robotaxi operations in late 2026.
Source: Lucid Q1 Earnings Presentation
Lucid's broader Q1 was rough: revenue $282.5 million up 20% from Q1 2025 but widely missed Wall St expectations, 5,500 vehicles produced, only 3,093 delivered, $3.2 billion in liquidity, and a roughly $1 billion net loss compared to $366 million a year ago. 
The robotaxi line is the bright spot. Uber expanded its commitment to Lucid to a minimum of 35,000 vehicles (10,000+ Gravity SUVs, 25,000 EVs on Lucid's upcoming mid-size platform). Total Uber investment is now $500 million in equity plus the multi-year vehicle purchase commitment. Lucid's stock-market story is increasingly an Uber-Nuro robotaxi call option layered on a struggling EV business.
For another Uber AV partner, the week wasn’t that good.
NHTSA's Office of Defects Investigation opened a probe into Avride after identifying 16 crashes, including one minor injury, all involving Avride-equipped robotaxis under safety-monitor supervision. ODI said the crashes go to "the competence" of Avride's self-driving system, with specific failure modes including lane changes into the path of adjacent vehicles, failure to slow or stop for slow-moving or stopped vehicles ahead, failure to avoid vehicles entering the lane ahead, and striking stationary objects partially obstructing the path.
Many of the reported crashes happened in Dallas, where Uber and Avride launched their robotaxi service in December 2025, with additional incidents in Austin. The minor-injury crash, in December in Dallas, involved an Avride-equipped Hyundai Ioniq 5 that clipped the open driver-side door of a parked pickup truck. At least one reported crash involved a passenger on board. Only one of the 16 crashes describes the safety monitor attempting to intervene.
Avride pointed out the crashes were reported as required under NHTSA's 2021 Standing General Order on automated driving and said it has "implemented targeted technical and operational mitigations" and that incident frequency relative to mileage has "steadily declined."
The harder fact is the safety-monitor non-intervention pattern. Sixteen crashes with a paid human at the wheel and only one documented intervention attempt is a meaningful operational data point and the obvious question Uber and Avride will get from regulators going forward.
The Avride probe is a reminder that partner aggregation as a strategy carries partner-quality risk that headline numbers like “30+ AV partners” obscure.
Uber Q1 earnings: a beat, a strong outlook, and AV commentary worth parsing
Uber posted a strong Q1 on Tuesday. Per Bloomberg, gross bookings grew 25% to $53.7 billion, the fastest pace since late 2022, beating the $52.9 billion analyst estimate. Q2 guidance came in at $56.25-57.75 billion versus a $56.23 billion consensus. CEO Dara Khosrowshahi said the core US rideshare business will accelerate further this year as moderating insurance costs unwind the fare pressure that had weighed on San Francisco and Los Angeles. The stock jumped 7.6% to $78.50, the biggest intraday gain since June 2025.
On AVs, the call had three substantive points. AV Mobility trips grew more than 10x year over year. Uber is on track for up to 15 cities by year-end and "significantly more than that going into next year." The platform now has more than 30 global autonomous partners.
Source: Uber Q1 2026 Earnings Presentation
Analyst Justin Post of Bank of America raised an interesting question on the call: “I know Waymo is launching a bunch of southern cities. Just wondering what you're seeing in those cities, any changes to your growth rate?”
Dara Khosrowshahi answer: “We continue to believe AVs are huge opportunities for the entire industry. This is, you know, we think another trillion-dollar TAM. We don't see this as being a winner-take-all market. We certainly see Waymo moving very quickly as we are moving very quickly….At this point, we don't see any effect of the Waymo launches on our overall business, and we continue to see Waymo, the performance of our businesses with Waymo in Austin and Atlanta continue to be strong. Driver earnings are up. More drivers are joining those platforms as well. If you look at kind of markets where Waymo has been launching, has been around for some period of time, San Francisco and L.A., for example, our category position both in San Francisco and L.A. is higher today than it was six months ago.”
No framing like “Waymo is our partner”. 
Growing tension between Uber and Waymo
Behind the operational confidence, there are growing public signals of strain in the Uber-Waymo relationship. The structural points are accumulating. Waymo has announced expansion into roughly 25 US cities in 2026 without Uber, while running its Lyft partnership in Nashville on a similar app-and-platform model. In London, Uber is leaning on Wayve and Baidu’s Apollo Go for upcoming robotaxi launches, while Waymo enters independently through its own app. In New York, Uber has publicly suggested the city should slow down on AV deployment, while Waymo is pushing forward. Uber framed that as worker protections, but the competitive read is hard to miss. The viral incident last week sharpened the picture.
Uber CTO Praveen Neppalli Naga posted a video on X of a Waymo near-miss in San Francisco, complete with horn-honking and on-camera narration. 
Source: X
As Harry Campbell pointed out, it is hard to recall a comparable case in the past decade where a C-suite executive at a major mobility platform publicly amplified criticism of a critical commercial partner. The choice to post is itself a signal. The partnership is not over. But the relationship is shifting. I will talk with Harry in detail about this topic in an upcoming podcast. Stay tuned for the episode.
Tesla hits 10 billion FSD miles, Belgium eyes FSD approval
Staying with the data theme, Tesla crossed 10 billion cumulative FSD miles this week. The number matters because in January, Musk told investors on X that “roughly 10 billion miles of training data is needed to achieve safe unsupervised self-driving.” The implication at the time was that crossing the threshold would unlock a flip from FSD (Supervised) to FSD (Unsupervised) on the existing customer fleet.
That flip did not happen this week. FSD remains a Level 2 driver-assist system that requires a fully attentive human at the wheel. Musk’s January framing was itself a moved goalpost. He had previously suggested 6 billion miles would be sufficient. On the most recent earnings call (CW17), he pushed unsupervised FSD for consumer cars to Q4 2026 at the earliest. There is no magical mileage threshold that flips a Level 2 system into Level 4.
The hard problem is not data volume, it is the long tail of edge cases and the legal question of who pays when an unsupervised vehicle causes a crash. When a Waymo causes one, Waymo pays. Tesla's terms of service still put liability on the owner under the Level 2 framing. The unresolved question is what happens to that liability the moment FSD becomes unsupervised across more than a million customer vehicles. The Florida federal jury verdict from last year, which found Tesla partly liable for a 2019 crash and ordered $243 million in damages, gives a real-money preview. Tesla appealed, the appeal was rejected.
Tesla claims FSD-equipped vehicles drive 5.5 million miles between major collisions versus 660,000 for the average US driver. 
Source: Tesla Safety Page
But researchers have flagged that the methodology does not adjust for the fact that FSD usage skews highway, where crashes are less common.
In Europe, Tesla's supervised FSD got a boost from Flanders. Per Reuters, Flemish transport minister Annick De Ridder said her team would decide by end of last week whether to fast-track approval after the Netherlands provisionally cleared FSD last month. Tesla has filed a similar request to Wallonia and Brussels has not yet responded. The Belgian central transport ministry confirmed this is a regional rather than federal matter. Several Nordic regulators remain skeptical. Read it as Tesla picking off small, friendly EU jurisdictions one at a time to build a patchwork of supervised FSD approval before the bloc converges on common rules.

My take on the week. The thread connecting every piece of this story is data, and specifically who controls it. Uber pitching its driver fleet as a moving sensor grid and an AV cloud partners can query, free for now. Lyft countering with Flexdrive's 3 million AV-relevant training miles, the FleetView street-level imagery program at a claimed 6-7x cost advantage, and 2.5 million OpenStreetMap edits in 2025. Tesla hitting Musk's self-set 10-billion-mile threshold without unlocking unsupervised driving. The platforms are racing to build the operating layer (fleets, maps, regulatory liaison, field ops). The next 12 months will be defined by who converts this activity into actual commercial leverage, not by who has the loudest milestone announcement.

AURORA AND McLANE LAUNCH DRIVERLESS COMMERCIAL OPERATIONS IN TEXAS
