On to the Update:

Data Center Discontent
----------------------

From Politico ( https://www.politico.com/news/2026/05/16/texas-county-data-center-construction-ban-00922493 ) :

A Texas county southwest of Dallas this week passed what may be the state’s first county-level moratorium on data centers, seeking to buy time for lawmakers to soften the blow of development sweeping across rural areas. Hill County’s Commissioners Court voted 3-2 Tuesday to put a yearlong moratorium on data center and power plant construction in unincorporated areas, citing an influx of as many as eight data centers planned there, many of which could have their own power plants…

Opposition to data centers is spreading in regions led by both Democrats and Republicans, as politicians try to balance economic development with increasingly vocal landowners who want protections. In Missouri, one small town unhappy over its city council’s approval of a data center voted last month to oust all four incumbents running for reelection. In North Carolina, Gov. Josh Stein (D) has made a point of saying that sales tax exemptions for data centers cost the state up to $57 million every year.

Texas has hundreds of data center locations operating or in development, second only to Virginia among U.S. states. The growth has stirred pushback from environmentalists and rural residents who worry about the effects on water supplies, the electric grid and their quality of life. Officials in states across the country are starting to have second thoughts about data centers, and some are looking to roll back tax incentives.

I chose this story just because it happened to have happened over the weekend; in truth, there are an exploding number of options, including one just up the highway from me in DeForest, Wisconsin ( https://www.wpr.org/news/deforest-wont-move-forward-data-center ) , and they are hardly isolated sentiments. From Gallup ( https://news.gallup.com/poll/709772/americans-oppose-data-centers-area.aspx ) :

Seven in 10 Americans oppose constructing data centers for artificial intelligence in their local area, including nearly half, 48%, who are strongly opposed. Barely a quarter favor these projects, with 7% strongly in favor.

[…]The data center question parallels the wording Gallup uses to ask about local nuclear power plant construction. In the same March survey, 53% of Americans say they oppose building a nuclear energy plant in their area, far less than the 71% opposed to data center construction. Since Gallup first asked the nuclear power plant question in 2001, the high point in opposition has been 63%.

Not building out nuclear fifty years ago was, of course, one of the greatest mistakes humanity has made, and one that contributes directly to data center opposition today, given questions about the impact on energy bills. Moreover, the concern is bipartisan:

Majorities of all major demographic groups, including all party groups, say they would oppose having a data center built where they live. However, Democrats are much more likely than Republicans to be strongly opposed, 56% vs. 39%, with independents between the two at 48%.

So what is going on?

Understanding the Opposition
----------------------------

The chief concern amongst data center defenders is misinformation, and for understandable reasons. Yes, data centers use water — although most modern data centers put water in a closed loop, instead of using evaporative cooling — but relatively speaking to other recreational, industrial, or agricultural uses, not that much; they also obviously use power, but there is opposition to data centers building their own power, too. To be clear, I’m not dismissing these points, just noting that there is also a lot of misinformation about them (particularly water).

The point of starting with the misinformation point, however, is not to legislate it: there are plenty of folks doing just that, on both sides. Rather, it’s to note that (1) misinformation takes root in the minds of people who are receptive to it and seeking confirmation for what they already want to believe, and therefore (2) trying to solve the misinformation problem through education — or simply calling people stupid — is likely not going to work.

This leads to the second issue: there really wasn’t widespread opposition to data centers when they were being built for the Internet generally; indeed, local governments would compete for them, eager for the economic benefits that came during their construction and the long-term tax benefits once they were operational. In other words, it seems pretty clear that the current wave of opposition can’t be divorced from AI — you know, the technology that in the best case scenario is going to take everyone’s jobs, and in the worst case scenario end the human race.

I do not, I don’t think, need to explain to you why this is not a very compelling message, and, in the very long run, I do think that humans will have economic value ( https://stratechery.com/2026/ai-and-the-human-condition/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDI2L2FpLWFuZC10aGUtaHVtYW4tY29uZGl0aW9uLyJdfSwiZXhwIjoxNzgxNjkwNTc4LCJpYXQiOjE3NzkwOTg1NzgsImlzcyI6Imh0dHBzOi8vYXBwLnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZS9vYXV0aCIsInNjb3BlIjoiZmVlZDpyZWFkIGFydGljbGU6cmVhZCBhc3NldDpyZWFkIGNhdGVnb3J5OnJlYWQgZW50aXRsZW1lbnRzIiwic3ViIjoiMDE5NTE2ZjEtYTM0Ni03NjE0LTllOTYtYjYzYmQxMWZlODU2IiwidXNlIjoiYWNjZXNzIn0.cIALgJypcyFCaKfa7qDWeOiLnnHmmDSGu_QOuYiG8VbIdTZBMw9UDO_qMoDnd9SD1925RGuq7McSpm0e0LpeWpZ4RWwH3m9RjSZ0b2qxwp73Otdzpje1Fw4KirHMyv4OqCSVT6-L0VXI5pD-dbk0TZTUxP7SBPD6S4_xI1My8eyG9N7SVnX2duJoM5f4GpdJP2C_i_CWmEEcSZNoEeO1kQ1zB9jBGQCMMJ5nFfQQhctEyyjONPsF0ERdZWAdNcfjs5HasVmKEIGcmZqQOHk54vwLUg08MSEKHcV1fGHMT-_elm3ZYXQC1FOoYQcVZeDtz8GdaCjxNP66-PNaTNfSMQ ). At the same time, it’s hard to explain the huge amount of investment that is going into AI, not just at the model level but also in terms of data centers, without admitting that a big part of the thesis is that AI can not just increase the top line for companies, but also increase the bottom line — i.e. replace the jobs of some number of humans. Again, I’m generally an optimist here, but this already happened with the first wave of IT ( https://stratechery.com/2024/enterprise-philosophy-and-the-first-wave-of-ai/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDI0L2VudGVycHJpc2UtcGhpbG9zb3BoeS1hbmQtdGhlLWZpcnN0LXdhdmUtb2YtYWkvIl19LCJleHAiOjE3ODE2OTA1NzgsImlhdCI6MTc3OTA5ODU3OCwiaXNzIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9hcHAucGFzc3BvcnQub25saW5lL29hdXRoIiwic2NvcGUiOiJmZWVkOnJlYWQgYXJ0aWNsZTpyZWFkIGFzc2V0OnJlYWQgY2F0ZWdvcnk6cmVhZCBlbnRpdGxlbWVudHMiLCJzdWIiOiIwMTk1MTZmMS1hMzQ2LTc2MTQtOWU5Ni1iNjNiZDExZmU4NTYiLCJ1c2UiOiJhY2Nlc3MifQ.YeBvu-FGInl0jy72JNNEOAm2j_m6QtDYhk5jgOT1LtIr47SsD5mlbPyLSdxZc1lkmoxFb9QZvBlQlQXRd7aY5nvfdKWtp63QqZT0TRezXYKCkI15MGyl6xVoec-fb5A5TzS1V7wPezPVytS4hxEXsndIfSnh-jbkW91vAv6x8pEvuqWPZ_gKPt35sYV-5bw9ULy4sUKCeLsae5sgDlpT0fSUx0D2YgUEVMzMazlapfoxrYErRLQX5vWErSUkkErnEXP9OOnMgIGI87zHQQRt1bKaUoB9xPNiGEPgvDpMXM9xPH4sGx1m8V2bJbFyzDEDLHy7XvuSS303gShSKzDl2A ).

That leads to an interesting contrast to globalization: when companies were closing down American factories and laying off workers and moving operations to China, none of the affected towns or workers had a say. They just suddenly no longer had a job, and a huge number of cities across the Rust Belt no longer had a reason to exist. People simply had to move, or worse, retreat to things like alcohol or drugs. AI, however, is the opposite: building data centers requires permission, which is to say that people actually have a say. Again, I am not at all saying that these people are well informed about data centers, or about the economic impact on their communities, much less the economic impact of AI generally; what I am noting is that people who didn’t have a say in globalization are suddenly finding they do have a say about AI, and it’s not a surprise they are expressing their disapproval by blocking data centers.

The third issue is a bit more fraught, particularly given this publication’s desire to generally not engage in partisan politics. The fact of the matter, however, is that, generally speaking, you would expect the left to be more opposed to technological progress, more concerned about job losses, more suspicious of big business, etc. That is why, traditionally, big business types have been associated with the Republican Party, which better represented their interests. Tech, however, went the opposite direction in the 2010s, culminating in what the right generally considered censorship of their viewpoints, particularly during COVID. The end result is that the tech industry has no constituency in partisan politics: the left is opposed to Big Tech, because of course they are; meanwhile, while some big names in tech have worked to ally themselves with the Trump administration, the issue with data centers is the base, and the base on the right hates Big Tech almost as much as the left does!

This is not, to be clear, an argument that one side or the other is right or wrong. Rather, what I am trying to explain is how it is that data centers are more unpopular than nuclear ever was, and why the opposition to them is so bipartisan.

Fixing the Problem
------------------

So how is this problem fixed?

First, this sounds obvious, but tech needs to fix its messaging problem. The issue — and if an answer seems obvious, then there surely must be some other problem at play! — is threefold.

* First, a good number of people in tech, particularly at (one of) the leading AI labs, genuinely believe that most jobs are going away. They could lie more effectively, but beyond being dishonest, it’s also a betrayal of the fanatical devotion with which they are pursuing AI despite obstacles, including the challenge of spending billions and billions of dollars on models that are obsolete in months if not weeks.
* Second, it is extremely hard to describe the benefits of inventions not yet made, cures not yet discovered, economic activity not yet engaged in, etc. This is always the burden of those arguing in favor of progress, and the sheer potential of AI actually makes the problem even harder, given how fantastical many upside scenarios seem.
* Third, tech is and always has been terrible at understanding and relating to the rest of society. I go back to how Silicon Valley was eternally skeptical of Facebook, a company predicated on connecting with friends and family, precisely because it’s filled with people running away from their friends and family. You can, optimistically, say that people in tech live in the future; you can also, more cynically, say they live in opposition to and denial of humanity, for better and, in this case, for worse.

Second, tech could control the misinformation, which, after all, is spreading on tech’s own platforms. There are also, however, some problems with this as well:

* First, one of the major vectors for data center opposition is TikTok, the algorithm of which is still controlled by a Chinese company. The problem of a Chinese company controlling what Americans see — without any way for anyone to know whether or not that control was being exerted — was why I supported the TikTok ban ( https://stratechery.com/2020/the-tiktok-war/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDIwL3RoZS10aWt0b2std2FyLyJdfSwiZXhwIjoxNzgxNjkwNTc4LCJpYXQiOjE3NzkwOTg1NzgsImlzcyI6Imh0dHBzOi8vYXBwLnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZS9vYXV0aCIsInNjb3BlIjoiZmVlZDpyZWFkIGFydGljbGU6cmVhZCBhc3NldDpyZWFkIGNhdGVnb3J5OnJlYWQgZW50aXRsZW1lbnRzIiwic3ViIjoiMDE5NTE2ZjEtYTM0Ni03NjE0LTllOTYtYjYzYmQxMWZlODU2IiwidXNlIjoiYWNjZXNzIn0.fQJzEPOCMMGKsA6I3ZJQUF0qO1nFkYzKG9zJSfAe1XwQvQNWh9TeOca8TSHOPxc3mVvhKToLItMqL02LYTDxNauQrcxVwECXlelShMbZwWE9_SBZlGNTvZ-KH-xYIWg4U9rXauNXNcv1mmGoS8ExpXd4_P5rEVIMTRIu_rPcDubUDi9SHld6yDC63b9ZxlKgpv6qtuVAjvNRRRamrzKyTvaphVJTFqX9lM01irieP9h6yhp0ihnDpXj5KBxWY3EeaQM04tozF9VLYs_nxF5_sTw-tOKbVjc0USoUmxDXeZ2TuZfqoDaFfIp4Eby1dtoGlMatdEtHlbyBbQRog9zCqw ) ; in this case, China certainly has an interest in the U.S. not building out more AI!
* Second, in a rather ironic twist, Meta does seem to have learned its lesson in terms of trying to control misinformation on its platforms, and doesn’t want to overtly censor again; of course the company gets no credit for this on the right (and is blamed for misinformation by the left), while also not being willing to defend its own interests on this specific issue.
* Third, X’s interest is to be anti-datacenter. Remember, X is now owned by SpaceX, and a big part of SpaceX’s upcoming public offering is the possibility of building data centers in space.

I haven’t, to be clear, seen any evidence of a thumb on the scale (or not) on any of these platforms; part of the problem, though, is that we would never know if there were.

The third thing tech could do — and this is where I think the answer lies — is simply start paying people off directly. Data center builders and operators already stress the economic benefits to local municipalities, of course, and Meta to its credit actually does quite a bit more than that ( https://about.fb.com/news/2026/03/meta-data-centers-support-energy-jobs-environment-local-communities/ ) , including workforce training. At the end of the day, however, I don’t think any of this is concrete enough to offset people’s anxiety about AI, which makes up the receptive soil in which the seed of misinformation lands and takes root. And, I would add, all of these benefits assume a level of trust in local governments, in terms of benefits trickling down to citizens, that is rapidly eroding.

Instead, the most obvious solution is the most crass: simply start giving people money. If data centers are a resource for our AI future, then start paying people for that resource. If that data center up the road weren’t sold to my neighbors based on amorphous tax benefits that my local government may or may not spend appropriately, but rather were to result in a check in the mailbox every year, I suspect you could get a lot more people on board!

Just to put some numbers on this, the data center up the road was expected to be 1.6 GW, which could generate around $3 billion in annual operator revenue. DeForest, the village it was to be built in, has around 11,500 people. You could pay every person in DeForest $10,000 a year for 3.8% of annual revenue grossed by the data center — I bet that proposal would have been approved, and I bet that the operator could very easily pass those costs on to the actual data center users (it also highlights how relatively pathetic QTS’s $50 million commitment ( https://q.com/news/qts-advances-plans-for-state-of-the-art-data-center-campus-and-announces-50-million-community-commitment-for-dane-county/ ) was).

I do get how ridiculous this sounds, but ridiculous is how we do things in America. We kind-of have universal health insurance — it’s called the Emergency Medical Treatment and Labor Act, which mandates care for anyone appearing in an emergency room. We have widespread welfare, it’s called Social Security Disability or Section 8 Housing or SNAP. And, once Big Tech starts paying people off for data centers, we’ll suddenly have AI-derived Universal Basic Income, via the most ridiculous mechanism possible, that both gets things done in an unfair kludgy way and lets us pretend it doesn’t exist — it’s just how we do things!

It’s also the reason this all works. Providing UBI via data center placement will, in the end, get data centers built, which will generate the economic growth that will make everyone better off. Yes, right now a lot of AI’s value is predicated on cutting below the line; what America does best, however, is focus relentlessly on the top line, doing whatever is necessary to make it go up and to the right.
