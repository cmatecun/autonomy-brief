UBER DOUBLES DOWN ON LUCID; $10B+ COMMITTED FOR AV, ASSET-LIGHT ERA ENDING
Let’s start with the story that ties the whole week together. 
Nuro and Lucid began public test rides this week
Nuro, using Lucid Gravity SUVs equipped with its autonomy stack, opened early rider access for select Uber employees in the San Francisco Bay Area. Safety drivers are still in the car. The fleet is small and the service runs through the Uber app. This is the first public-facing step of the Uber-Nuro-Lucid partnership announced in July 2025, when Uber committed $300M to Lucid and lined up a purchase of at least 20,000 Gravity SUVs.
Source: Nuro
That was not the only news out of the trio this week.
Lucid appointed a new CEO and raised $750M. 
On Tuesday, Lucid named Silvio Napoli as CEO. Napoli is the former head of Swiss elevator maker Schindler. He starts in the coming weeks pending US work visa approval. Marc Winterhoff, interim CEO since February 2025, returns to COO. “My job is to come in and make a sustainable business out of this incredible company,” Napoli told Bloomberg TV.
The same day, Lucid confirmed $550M in fresh capital from Saudi Arabia’s Public Investment Fund, its majority shareholder. Uber chipped in another $200M. Total: $750M. Shares jumped 11% pre-market on the news.
The Uber purchase commitment expanded from 20,000 to 35,000 vehicles. The new tranche includes Lucid’s upcoming midsize platform, which is expected to launch at the end of 2026 around the $50,000 price point.
Here is why this matters beyond the press release.
According to the Financial Times, Uber has now committed more than $10B to autonomous vehicle fleets and equity stakes across the robotaxi ecosystem. The FT’s math, based on analyst estimates and people familiar with Uber’s deals, splits that into roughly $2.5B in equity investments and more than $7.5B in vehicle purchase commitments. The latest Lucid expansion adds approximately $2B to the vehicle side.
Walter Piecyk at LightShed Partners told the FT this is “step one in a complete narrative change” for Uber. He thinks the company is “easing people into the concept” of Uber owning fleets. That reading is hard to argue with. The numbers speak for themselves.
CEO Dara Khosrowshahi was blunt with investors in February: “We are putting our capital up in order to guarantee supply going forward. Much of that supply is going to be on profitable economics. And we will continue making these kinds of commitments.”
That is a clean break from the asset-light model that made Uber a disruptor in the first place.
The market has punished the stock for it. 
Uber shares are down roughly 23% in the past six months. The damage is driven by milestones from Waymo, Tesla, and Zoox. 
Source: Financial Times
Investors are worried Uber's role as demand aggregator gets overtaken by vertically integrated AV players going direct to consumer.
That fear is rational. Uber is now competing against three of the most valuable companies on earth. Alphabet owns Waymo. Amazon owns Zoox. Tesla is Tesla. All three have thrived historically by cutting out intermediaries. Waymo already operates its own app in San Francisco and plans to do the same in other markets like London. Zoox told the FT it will run its own app wherever it deploys. Tesla has its own app and, according to Khosrowshahi, the talks with Uber "fizzled" because Tesla "wants to build it alone."
Dara’s counter-narrative is financialization
He has told investors repeatedly that Uber will not have to carry the full capital burden alone. “We do think that we will have a very, very healthy financing ecosystem both in terms of equity and debt. The whole system is going to financialise, just as you see data centres financialise as well.”
In other words: Uber underwrites the initial commitments, then offloads the assets to fleet management companies, institutional investors, and private credit over time. Whether that ecosystem actually shows up is the open question. Dara can afford it today. The question is whether he can keep affording it if any of these partners miss.
Staying asset-light was never going to survive first contact with Waymo scaling and Tesla going direct. If Khosrowshahi had kept doing buybacks while Waymo hit 1M weekly rides this year, the stock would look worse, not better. The 23% drop reflects a transition tax, not a strategy failure.
That said, not all AV partners will make it. When the consolidation comes, Uber will not follow on every investment. The open question is whether the end-state is Uber-owns-Nuro-and-Lucid as a rebranded vertical,  or Uber stays the aggregator with fewer but healthier partners. Both paths are live. Neither is cheap.
Uber's partner and biggest competitor had a busy week too.
Miami and Orlando are now open to everyone
Waymo lifted the early rider program in both Florida cities on April 15. Riders can hail through the Waymo app directly. In Miami, select riders also get access to limited freeway rides when they opt in. Orlando remains surface-street only. 
Harry Campbell  detailed the timeline in his newsletter, and this tells the story of acceleration. 
Miami: 
Miami expansion announcement, ahead of a future launch in December 2024. 
fully autonomous testing began November 2025
first public riders January 2026
full public launch April 2026. 
Orlando: 
first Waymo road trip announced May 2025
fully autonomous testing December 2025
first riders February 2026
full launch April 2026. 
The gap from first testing to full public launch has collapsed from multiple years in early Waymo markets like Phoenix and San Francisco to months in Orlando.
That compression is the real news. The technology generalizes faster. Each new city benefits from the training data, the operational playbook, and the regulatory templates built in the previous city. That compounding is exactly what we have been tracking for months.
Ontario Waymo’s next target?
According to the CBC, Waymo is applying to join Ontario’s Automated Vehicle Pilot program, per the Toronto mayor’s office. The move comes after Waymo registered as a lobbyist in the province. If approved, this would be Waymo’s first Canadian deployment and a test case for Ontario’s regulatory framework, which is more permissive than most Canadian provinces.
Toronto has roughly 3M people in the metro area. That is a larger initial TAM than many US secondary markets where Waymo has gone live via Uber.
How is it going in Waymo’s other international markets?
Waymo goes hands-off in London
London moved to the next testing phase. Waymo has shifted from manual, safety-driver-with-both-hands-on-the-wheel mode to hands-off autonomous testing in London. The final step is driver-out commercial operations, which is still gated on UK regulatory approval.
To be clear: London is not yet a commercial service. Waymo is running an expanding vehicle fleet in central London with safety drivers still in the front seat, but now not touching the wheel. The operating area is still limited to a 100-square-mile area of the city. Regulators have not yet cleared full driverless. 
One detail worth flagging on London. If Waymo thought Uber was a long-term partner, it would plug into the Uber app in London the same way it does in Austin and Atlanta. It is not. Waymo plans its own consumer app. That signals Waymo treats London as a primary market where it wants the direct consumer relationship. Uber’s $10B bet on alternative AV supply is partly a response to exactly this behavior.
Staying in London. 
Wayve raised $60M
The UK-based AV developer raised $60M this week from AMD, Arm, and Qualcomm. The investor list tells you where Wayve is going. AMD, Arm, and Qualcomm are ADAS chip suppliers first. Qualcomm is developing L4 compute, but the bulk of its autonomy roadmap today is L2+ platforms for passenger car OEMs. 
TechCrunch’s  framing of the deal is worth flagging. The article characterized the round as chipmakers “rushing to invest” in a company whose core product is an end-to-end AI model trained on driving video, and noted that Wayve’s commercial traction to date has been license deals for ADAS-grade systems rather than deployed L4 robotaxis. In other words, the chip money follows the ADAS revenue, not  autonomy.
Grayson on Road to Autonomy read this the same way: the chip investor mix signals Wayve is leaning harder into ADAS (L2+) than into robotaxi-grade L4. Both reads converge on the same point. Wayve is a bet on the L2+ embedded-AI stack for passenger cars, with optionality on L4.
CEO Alex Kendall framed it on X as “automotive chip suppliers are rushing to invest in this self-driving startup.” The skeptical read, which Walt pushed back with on the podcast and which TechCrunch’s reporting implicitly supports, is that chip suppliers invest because they want revenue, not because they believe in the end-state product. That is a fair point. When a chipmaker invests in an AV company, they are buying the right to supply silicon, not necessarily validating driverless capability.
For reference, Uber put $300M into Wayve in February as part of a broader partnership. Uber is not in this for L2. That tension between Uber’s L4 ambition and Wayve’s chip-investor ADAS pivot is worth watching.
Wayve's Nissan relationship is the other signal
Nissan itself made news. Nissan announced that 90% of its future models will ship with AI-based autonomous driving technology. That is not Nissan claiming 90% of its fleet will be L4. It is a commitment to L2+ and ADAS rollout at near-universal scope across the product line.
And Japan as a country is also raising its ambition. The Japanese government announced a target of owning 25% of the global autonomous vehicle market by the 2030s, pursued through public-private partnership investments. That is aggressive. For reference, Japan's current global auto market share is roughly 30%. 
TESLA EXPANDS ROBOTAXI TO DALLAS AND HOUSTON, ROLLS OUT FSD STREAKS
Tesla had two moves this week. One is expansion, the other is engagement.
Dallas and Houston are live. On April 18, Tesla announced via its @robotaxi account on X that Robotaxi service is now rolling out in Dallas and Houston. That is Tesla’s first expansion beyond Austin and the Bay Area.
Source: X/Tesla Robotaxi
The company posted two map images with the announcement and nothing else. No fleet size, no word on whether rides will be supervised or unsupervised, no pricing.
Source: X/Tesla Robotaxi
The geofences are small. Houston covers roughly 25 square miles based on early user analysis of the posted maps. Dallas centers on the Highland Park area with a similar footprint. For context, Tesla's Austin geofence has grown to roughly 245 square miles after months of gradual expansion. That took nearly a year to reach from an initial 20-square-mile footprint.
Tesla’s Austin operation still relies on only a handful of unsupervised vehicles. Somewhere between 4 and 12 Model Ys operate without a human safety monitor out of a total fleet of roughly 80 vehicles, though most are not operating at the same time. The remaining cars still carry safety monitors in the driver’s seat. 
Put differently: Tesla is adding cities faster than it is adding unsupervised cars.
The second move this week was FSD streaks. Tesla rolled out streaks and usage statistics to drivers via the April software update. The feature tracks how often a driver uses Full Self-Driving (Supervised) and gamifies engagement. On the surface, this is a consumer feature. Read more carefully, it is a data play. Tesla wants drivers to keep FSD engaged more often, because engagement generates training data and the robotaxi narrative needs a rising engagement curve.
It is not a regulatory milestone. It is not a driverless milestone. It is a dashboard for a narrative.
What this means
This week compressed the three biggest structural stories in AV into one news cycle. Uber is writing billion-dollar checks to not lose the race. Waymo is widening its lead city by city and going direct where the economics justify it. Tesla is adding city pins on a map faster than it is adding unsupervised cars under them. And Japan, via Nissan and its ministry targets, is quietly positioning to stay relevant in the software era. The common thread is that nobody believes the current positions are stable.

CHINA CORNER: SAFETY AUDIT ORDERED AFTER ROBOTAXI INCIDENT, BUT GLOBAL EXPANSION ACCELERATES
China’s Ministry of Industry and Information Technology (MIIT) ordered a nationwide safety review of smart vehicle road tests this week, following a robotaxi incident and the Baidu Apollo outage. According to The Economic Times India, the directive requires operators to reinspect autonomous test vehicles, tighten supervisor protocols, and report findings to regulators. Specific deadlines were not disclosed.
This is the second MIIT safety action. The regulator has been tightening quietly since the Xiaomi SU7 incident, and the pattern here is clear. Beijing will not allow a high-visibility robotaxi accident to derail a policy priority. It will slow the cycle to de-risk, then let companies resume.
That has also not stopped the international expansion.
The past weeks we have covered WeRide, Pony.ai, and Baidu staying committed to the Middle East despite the ongoing Iran conflict. This week, another Chinese player joined.
Didi announced a UAE pilot launch
Didi will launch robotaxi pilot operations in the UAE this year. The Dubai and Abu Dhabi positioning follows the same playbook we have seen from WeRide and Pony.ai. The UAE authorities, via the Roads and Transport Authority in Dubai, have become the de facto bridgehead for Chinese AV expansion outside China. Didi’s entry means another Chinese top-tier robotaxi operator is now in the Middle East.
Source: DiDi Autonomous Driving
WeRide launched its L2+ offering with GAC Aion
WeRide has deployed its end-to-end ADAS stack in the GAC Aion N60 SUV for consumer use.  According to the press statement, the system is designed to support assisted driving across a range of situations, including city roads, motorways, parking and safety-related functions, covering daily commuting scenarios. The launch also represents WeRide’s first mass-production application of its one-stage end-to-end technology on the Qualcomm Snapdragon platform.
Source: WeRide
This is a real dual-track strategy: L4 robotaxi operations globally, plus L2+ consumer ADAS licensing inside China. WeRide is one of the few companies running commercial driverless robotaxi operations in China with no safety driver, and now it is also shipping consumer ADAS to a major local OEM.
The skeptical framing is that ADAS licensing is not the same business as L4 robotaxis and structurally can distract from the L4 effort. The counter, which I find more persuasive, is that WeRide wants revenue diversification to offset China's capital markets pressure and margin compression on robotaxi unit economics. As a hedge against execution risk on L4 scaling, it makes sense. 
Goldman Sachs raised its China robotaxi forecast
Goldman Sachs now projecting the country’s fleet will nearly triple from 5,000 vehicles in 2025 to 14,000 by the end of 2026. The bank forecasts 3.1 million units by 2035, representing 36% of all ride-sharing vehicles.
“​​Commercialization is speeding up, with several players achieving city-level break-even,” Goldman analysts led by Allen Chang said in a note.
Goldman shares my opinion that Chinese robotaxi companies will increasingly rely on international markets for growth. The bank introduced overseas and robotruck fleet forecasts, projecting overseas fleets accounting for 29%, 9%, and 7% of the global fleets of WeRide, Pony AI, and Baidu respectively by 2026, rising to 43%, 37%, and 38% by 2035.
Goldman reiterated its Buy rating on its American Depositary Receipts (ADRs) with a 12-month price target of $30. “As the leading supplier in autonomous mobility, we forecast Pony AI revenues to grow at 100% CAGR in 2025-30E,” the analysts said.

DEFENSE: UKRAINE'S EMERGING AUTONOMY ECONOMY
I have been tracking defense autonomy in Quick Takes for months. This week the signal got too big for a one-liner.
President Volodymyr Zelenskyy announced this week that Ukrainian ground robotic systems combined with aerial drone systems have carried out more than 22,000 missions in the last three months of the conflict. He also confirmed that in April, Ukrainian forces took a Russian position using exclusively unmanned platforms. Ground robots plus drones. No Ukrainian soldiers put in harm's way. According to The Independent, this is the first confirmed battlefield capture by fully autonomous systems in recorded warfare.
Let that sit for a second. Ukraine has productized autonomous warfare to the point where it can take and hold territory without committing a human soldier to the assault. 
The market number
Ukraine’s domestic unmanned ground vehicle market grew 488% in 2025, reaching roughly $252M, according to data surfaced by the Road to Autonomy Signals team this week. In absolute terms, $252M is small. In growth rate, it is the fastest-growing defense technology vertical in the world right now. And critically, this is wartime domestic demand. The export potential has not yet shown up in the numbers.
Brave1
The Ukrainian government's defense tech cluster, Brave1, distributed more than $60M in grants to over 600 companies in 2025. That is a deliberately compressed venture ecosystem with a built-in procurement pipeline. The validation layer is a live  conflict rather than a simulation environment. As Grayson put it on the Road to Autonomy Signals episode, "there's no regulatory sandbox that is going to be able to replicate the data that is coming out of Ukraine."
Combat data sharing is the move that changes the industry
Ukraine launched a first-of-its-kind combat data sharing initiative designed to give allied governments, international defense companies, and tech startups access to its battlefield data. 
“The future of warfare belongs to autonomous systems. Our objective is to increase the level of autonomy in drones and other combat platforms so they can detect targets faster, analyze battlefield conditions, and support real-time decision making.”
That is what the Ukrainian Defense Minister Mykhailo Fedorov wrote in a release announcing the program.
For allied governments, it offers a faster path to fielding AI-enabled defense capabilities — without having to generate their own combat datasets from scratch.
Fedorov framed it as a “win-win”, partners get better training data, and Ukraine gets faster development of autonomous capabilities for its own front lines.
The datasets cover terminal guidance, lock-on sequences, GPS-denied navigation, and swarm coordination. 
Access is through Ukraine’s Ministry of Defense. At the center of the new program is a dedicated AI platform, which allows partners to train AI models on real battlefield data without gaining direct access to other sensitive military databases.
These are real-world datasets collected during active combat operations, which are continuously updated in almost real time as Russia’s full-scale invasion moves into its fifth year. No existing commercial deployment, no simulation, contains such  operationally rich data.
The commercial AV industry spends billions on simulation platforms trying to generate synthetic data. Ukraine is handing out the real thing.
Zelenskyy was in Germany this week for a press announcement. According to the same reporting, Germany will now use Ukrainian combat data to train its own military algorithms. 
Source: Euronews
To be clear: Germany is not deploying troops. Germany is acquiring the training data to build its own defense autonomy capabilities. This is the technology transfer flywheel turning in real time.
The human part of this story matters, and it points at what comes next.
None of what I am writing this week takes joy in a continuing war. The most important goal remains a just and sustainable peace for Ukraine. Holding both things in one thought is the only honest way to write this section. Ukraine did not choose to become the world’s leading live-fire testing ground for autonomous systems. The country was forced into it. Every capability described above exists because Ukrainian lives, infrastructure, and civilian freedom are still being attacked.
What is worth naming is that the capability Ukraine has built under those conditions is also a long-term economic and strategic asset. When the war ends, whenever it ends, the Brave1 ecosystem does not vanish. The combat-validated AI perception stacks, the swarm coordination IP, the GPS-denied navigation expertise built in three years are assets that survive. They redirect into reconstruction logistics, agricultural autonomy, port and rail automation, border surveillance, and dual-use export.
Ukraine has the opportunity to emerge from this period as one of the most capable autonomy and defense-tech economies in Europe. That would be a meaningful consolation, not a good trade for what has been lost, but a real path forward. The European Union already understands this. So does Germany. So do the international defense companies and the US primes who are now quietly talking to Brave1 portfolio companies.
The Ukraine autonomy stack is not a wartime artifact. It is likely to be one of the most durable industrial outcomes of the conflict, and the companies and allies that engage early will be the ones that help Ukraine capture the upside.
And Western defense is catching up. Two examples this week.
ARX Robotics booked its first British Army order and opened UK manufacturing. 
The German-founded UGV developer ARX Robotics secured its first British Army contract for its Gereon RCS unmanned ground vehicle platform. The award follows months of collaboration between ARX Robotics UK and British Army operators, during which the UGVs were refined through trials and operational experimentation, including through exercises in Kenya and the Baltics to demonstrate how autonomous platforms can keep soldiers out of immediate danger, strengthen situational awareness and accelerate decision making in complex operations.
The company is standing up UK-based manufacturing to supply. ARX Robotics has been expanding its UK presence as part of its wider European growth strategy, investing £45 million, creating at least 90 skilled jobs.
Minister for Defence Readiness and Industry, Luke Pollard MP said: “The British Army's adoption of autonomous ground systems is a clear signal that the UK is serious about modernising its land forces and staying ahead of our adversaries. ARX Robotics’ decision to begin manufacturing in the UK is exactly the kind of investment we want to see. It demonstrates that our defence industrial strategy is working - attracting innovative technology companies to build here, backing skilled jobs and strengthening our Armed Forces.”
The order size was not disclosed, but the signal matters: the UK Ministry of Defence is buying European UGVs rather than defaulting to US systems.
Einride appointed former NSA chief General Keith Alexander to its board
Einride brought on General (Ret.) Keith B. Alexander to accelerate its defense business. Alexander is a former Director of the NSA and former Commander of US Cyber Command. He is also on the boards of Amazon, IronNet, and others. This is not a ceremonial appointment. Einride is repositioning from commercial autonomous freight to dual-use logistics, with an explicit focus on US defense procurement and supply chain autonomy.
The defense pivot gives it a second revenue vector and a credible board member who can open doors at the Pentagon. Whether it closes contracts is the question for the next quarters.
A broader pattern: commercial autonomy companies are pivoting to defense
Quantum Systems, the German drone maker, acquired Munich-based autonomous trucking startup Fernride in December 2025. Fernride had been working on teleoperated and autonomous yard trucks. Quantum Systems is repurposing that IP for unmanned ground logistics in defense and dual-use applications. The pattern is clear: commercial VD companies are finding capital and contracts in defense.
Expect more of these pivots in 2026. Civilian autonomy companies with solid VD stacks but weak commercial traction will get shopped by defense primes. The IP is valuable. The application is where the money is.
