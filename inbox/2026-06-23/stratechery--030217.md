Memory Chips and China
----------------------

The Wall Street Journal ( https://www.wsj.com/tech/why-the-memory-crunch-is-almost-impossible-to-solve-4023cb40 ) has a good overview of the memory issue, which, as I discussed yesterday ( https://stratechery.com/2026/apple-price-increases-apple-intelligence-and-the-e-u/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDI2L2FwcGxlLXByaWNlLWluY3JlYXNlcy1hcHBsZS1pbnRlbGxpZ2VuY2UtYW5kLXRoZS1lLXUvIl19LCJleHAiOjE3ODQ4MDA5MzcsImlhdCI6MTc4MjIwODkzNywiaXNzIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9hcHAucGFzc3BvcnQub25saW5lL29hdXRoIiwic2NvcGUiOiJmZWVkOnJlYWQgYXJ0aWNsZTpyZWFkIGFzc2V0OnJlYWQgY2F0ZWdvcnk6cmVhZCBlbnRpdGxlbWVudHMiLCJzdWIiOiIwMTk1MTZmMS1hMzQ2LTc2MTQtOWU5Ni1iNjNiZDExZmU4NTYiLCJ1c2UiOiJhY2Nlc3MifQ.OErStM1OG81N99BEn9ZBugl6evwU_S-xtgRF-hjOANlQWuAV7KvTCpQw0wBWoq628-CoNaVHiQmjLQUZrtyp6IpQC4B6w0V-2UUpShsKAOTT6moMVbxQL4RMpoRcm9WrAtaT-6dvUAHuUKCfqK-4POCwZzFFXNF6LS-bHftrghzdPlvdGqj6R-m9r4F0EjBByYyPwXu_m4NoKkArL2se_K3qA9IMY_PskWmWl4w6snYFTTqkJ5ht9a0Af8bh6CtkmDE607ayPpxOgvGTLPeZ-vDaO5wE4zgibYdv-UNSsY-WupC-PiK7fmL_IT1Ur3JKJojKEOZof28BB0FBQ2g-8A ) , has even reached mighty Apple, once king of the supply chain; I thought it would be worth going through the article piece-by-piece to explain what is going on.

The last thing President Trump wants is another hit to consumers’ pocketbooks. But there is little policymakers can do to quickly address the memory-chip shortage behind Apple’s decision to raise prices.

AI in the long run should be deflationary, but as I noted in January after Nvidia’s CES keynote that wasn’t about consumer electronics at all ( https://stratechery.com/2026/nvidia-at-ces-vera-rubin-and-ai-native-storage-infrastructure-alpamayo/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDI2L252aWRpYS1hdC1jZXMtdmVyYS1ydWJpbi1hbmQtYWktbmF0aXZlLXN0b3JhZ2UtaW5mcmFzdHJ1Y3R1cmUtYWxwYW1heW8vIl19LCJleHAiOjE3ODQ4MDA5MzcsImlhdCI6MTc4MjIwODkzNywiaXNzIjoiaHR0cHM6Ly9hcHAucGFzc3BvcnQub25saW5lL29hdXRoIiwic2NvcGUiOiJmZWVkOnJlYWQgYXJ0aWNsZTpyZWFkIGFzc2V0OnJlYWQgY2F0ZWdvcnk6cmVhZCBlbnRpdGxlbWVudHMiLCJzdWIiOiIwMTk1MTZmMS1hMzQ2LTc2MTQtOWU5Ni1iNjNiZDExZmU4NTYiLCJ1c2UiOiJhY2Nlc3MifQ.Hd3v5nVJi9p6VhAzr8_S5Rm9FizuP2Pw7ozDaji51yJQO9Vn1pC-iIEfbJ4lr5SFJgi9w7-HDTblUwrx3D00WxZhu_7f-EjNYuC18Zm2NwhM5N0pfG5BaW3b3DYos_tQEOOGm2tj4QpAujIMBsQcUGOUD7cKodf-xHo8en0Y8cDqh7jRL2JtrInIxf7IBI9ujyw9BKW13CE2KfT6_nWRaiXrNZIc-SBJaPr_NXo8v0_j2_ure47_NfXVg-o2IirH1eRZS-jPvf83HeOHzedgYIKocF3q8dJy7FszdC7B4DCEUH7bUhvLLEpnzEq5rt-QfQ3hPlzGvBkdZ_tav83iFQ ) , the memory issue is going to raise prices on a category of goods that have traditionally always decreased in price:

A big story over the last few months has been the dramatically escalating cost of memory as the major memory manufacturers shift their focus to high-bandwidth memory for AI chips in particular. What that means is that everything else is going to get a lot more expensive: memory is one of the most expensive components in nearly everything tech-related, and given the competitive and commoditized nature of the industry those costs will almost certainly be passed on to the end users.

This AI crowd-out dynamic arguably started with the hyperscalers, who diverted ever increasing parts of their budget to GPUs in place of CPU purchases, but now it’s coming for everything from grid power to turbines and now to components, and it’s only going to increase and become more impactful to end users. In other words, Nvidia may not have talked about consumer electronics at the Consumer Electronics Show, but they are having the biggest impact on the industry by far.

Some aspects of these increased prices are simply going to result in fewer sales; still, consumer electronics — particularly smartphones — are the single most important devices in consumers’ lives so they will have no choice but to pay up.

Only a handful of companies make memory and storage chips, and it takes years to build new factories. Three industry titans—Samsung Electronics, SK Hynix and Micron Technology—dominate the business of memory chips, also called DRAM. They are using much of their capacity to serve the fast-growing artificial-intelligence industry, squeezing consumer-technology companies.

This paragraph is, in many respects, the root of the problem. One of the topics that Jon discusses in the aforelinked Asianometry video is the boom-and-bust memory cycle, where the industry responds too slowly to surges in demand, overbuilds, and then gets absolutely killed when prices plummet. The biggest losers are the least efficient producers who license their technology — which is basically what Jon’s video describes in Taiwan — but even for the companies that survive, it takes massive investment to stay on the cutting edge. The net result is that an industry that used to have tens of players now only has three, which makes it much easier to exercise discipline in terms of capacity expansion.

Burned by past boom-bust cycles, the big memory-chip producers are cautious about creating another glut, which is why their spending on new plants remains relatively conservative. That is also true for the makers of storage chips known as NAND, which include the three titans plus Kioxia and Sandisk. The caution comes despite gross profit margins that have soared to records: in Micron’s case, 80%.

I wrote earlier this year about TSMC’s delayed response to AI ( https://stratechery.com/2026/tsmc-risk/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDI2L3RzbWMtcmlzay8iXX0sImV4cCI6MTc4NDgwMDkzNywiaWF0IjoxNzgyMjA4OTM3LCJpc3MiOiJodHRwczovL2FwcC5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUvb2F1dGgiLCJzY29wZSI6ImZlZWQ6cmVhZCBhcnRpY2xlOnJlYWQgYXNzZXQ6cmVhZCBjYXRlZ29yeTpyZWFkIGVudGl0bGVtZW50cyIsInN1YiI6IjAxOTUxNmYxLWEzNDYtNzYxNC05ZTk2LWI2M2JkMTFmZTg1NiIsInVzZSI6ImFjY2VzcyJ9.ZdG5Dmuv-d_2944sUfgjoHm1MBER4DxIrFLdgKsFz2LUDC6OiO43CykkxtfXHhJsrknVPaei8bLAD_inkChkKyXCNW__u85RZqbtsenqK14_AFrrwkE8uivWdQIJpJ5UhRqb0HkUTierS1zxmK3PjF2-GN7JHxAhBAOUNdeT_7-UShoeo5fYeJPn3aCHzwgyLAkHuzYopSe-Q4ydmXC83aPIUCfoGX_Tn1RBKylo7Cr0eyyCoekE49tLPOlxTfwqdaQQZcZUUc_ymoijfKATdIPCsaeD3neuEYz0CXbfFkBaFu12gGjwSm9qZmju83tn4BYTJD-fjGU9lPAFek0LWg ) and why it was important for tech companies to get Intel and Samsung up to speed:

What the hyperscalers need to understand is that simply begging TSMC to make more isn’t going to fix this problem, because begging TSMC to make more is to basically ask TSMC to take back the risk TSMC is offloading to the hyperscalers — they already declined! Rather, the only thing that will truly motivate TSMC to take on more risk is competition. If TSMC were worried about not just forgoing its own extra revenue, but actually losing business to a competitor, then the company would invest more. Moreover, that extra investment would be stacked on top of the investment made by said competitor, which means the world would suddenly have dramatically more fab capacity.

TSMC’s de facto monopoly on the leading edge is very much intertwined with their slowness to react to the AI signal; there are three memory manufacturers, which makes the situation a little better there — at least in theory — but the fact of the matter is that if there were 7 or 10 we would be in much better shape right now (and in reality, the explosion in memory demand has been even greater than the explosion in chip demand, so the situation is worse right now).

Not long after, AI took off, stoking demand for a particular memory chip well-suited for use in computers that train and query popular artificial-intelligence models. AI data centers also need large quantities of more traditional memory chips.

What has compounded the memory issue is that the high bandwidth memory (HBM) chips used for AI are stacked DRAM chips, but include increasingly important base die and interface layers that are customized around the needs of accelerators, particularly Nvidia’s. The price Nvidia (and AMD) is willing to pay for HBM is far more than the DRAM is worth on its own; that means that the big three memory makers are completely focused on meeting Nvidia’s needs, and only then selling DRAM to everyone else.

Two Chinese chip makers—CXMT, the country’s top DRAM producer, and Yangtze Memory Technologies, which focuses on NAND storage—are growing fast and want to expand their global clientele. China is the closest thing to a quick fix for the chip shortage, but the solution is at best partial.

Yangtze Memory is building three new factories in China that would more than double its current capacity by the end of 2027, people familiar with the plans said. Meanwhile CXMT is seeking to raise $4 billion in an initial public offering in Shanghai, and it is building new factories. It said its revenue rose by more than 700% year-over-year in the first quarter of 2026, though it acknowledged that its products still trail those of the three industry leaders.

This is where the rubber is going to hit the road. There is one country that is ready and willing to put up the massive amount of capital that is necessary to increase the number of competitors in the space; it just so happens to be a country that the U.S. has national security concerns about. However, I don’t think that those concerns are going to carry the day.

Yet the memory-chip shortage has gotten bad enough that some big consumer-tech companies have asked administration officials to loosen the restrictions on working with China. The companies also want the U.S. to make it easier for Samsung and SK Hynix to boost production in China.

Apple Chief Executive Tim Cook said in a Wall Street Journal interview that “everything needs to be on the table” regarding policy choices. In 2022, Apple worked with Yangtze Memory to prepare the Chinese NAND maker to become one of its suppliers of flash storage but was forced to abandon that plan when lawmakers opposed it.

Some of the world’s biggest personal-computer makers, including HP, are in talks with supply-chain partners about using CXMT memory chips in products bound for Asia, people familiar with the discussions said.

That last paragraph hints at where this is likely going to go. Chinese companies cannot currently make competitive HBM, even if their chips were allowed onto AI accelerators, but again, the core component of HBM is DRAM, and basically all computing devices need DRAM. Moreover, DRAM, unlike logic, is fungible: it’s very viable for HP to use CXMT chips for computers that are sold in Asia, and chips from the big three for computers sold to the West. The same thing applies to smartphones or anything else.

Yes, it’s possible these are older types of DRAM, or less reliable, or whatever other deficiencies might entail going with a new supplier, but simply getting this volume will make the Chinese suppliers better. And, in the long run, Chinese suppliers will increasingly be in a position to compete with the leading providers; moreover, they very well may have the sort of subsidies that make them the low cost provider.

This, by extension, explains why the big three have probably already massively damaged themselves in the very long run: because they were comfortable enough with their oligopoly to not bring on more supply faster, they created the conditions for Chinese competition to arise, and I don’t think they’re going to like it very much.

Microsoft and Chinese Models
----------------------------

From Axios ( https://www.axios.com/2026/06/16/microsoft-copilot-cowork-tokenmaxxing-cowork ) :

Microsoft says companies using Copilot Cowork will pay based on how much compute they use. The company tells Axios it is exploring a fine-tuned version of DeepSeek V4, or another open-source model, as a lower-cost alternative to the Anthropic and OpenAI models now powering Copilot Cowork. Microsoft says it expects to make a lower-cost model available in the coming weeks and will confirm its choice then.

When I interviewed Satya Nadella earlier this month ( https://stratechery.com/2026/an-interview-with-microsoft-ceo-satya-nadella-about-finding-core-competencies/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDI2L2FuLWludGVydmlldy13aXRoLW1pY3Jvc29mdC1jZW8tc2F0eWEtbmFkZWxsYS1hYm91dC1maW5kaW5nLWNvcmUtY29tcGV0ZW5jaWVzLyJdfSwiZXhwIjoxNzg0ODAwOTM3LCJpYXQiOjE3ODIyMDg5MzcsImlzcyI6Imh0dHBzOi8vYXBwLnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZS9vYXV0aCIsInNjb3BlIjoiZmVlZDpyZWFkIGFydGljbGU6cmVhZCBhc3NldDpyZWFkIGNhdGVnb3J5OnJlYWQgZW50aXRsZW1lbnRzIiwic3ViIjoiMDE5NTE2ZjEtYTM0Ni03NjE0LTllOTYtYjYzYmQxMWZlODU2IiwidXNlIjoiYWNjZXNzIn0.r3lbMg-zoAob0_hwN0erYc1SiBLBoL3EO0TSiONX2nI6HWDc4mLr8l7W-PERVR-ijlawYL4nn0lLP2tRgLiKHvNx1fn2aSqkChMybBlypYdBEbh9pJX9DP3_hg0Cw0HiIj9duZz4xbiUVBnICFDvsatkheZibUNUooYRVbQk3VpETjZF6DN08Xwx2X0f2AGGz5_DKodnSpbn9j1sE1i-f399lh0JUEJvEJudbl4UZp5Y3yxa23fahgLiAZ8xEWlWD5yRb3QwNiutXAw4lyKVymyb-qld7s1-TZHs1k-8YwZvToSUiN_Rbd9IcyDEl5qUOQN6fZg0MuucslWjrF_XjQ ) , there was a point of confusion when he said that Cowork supports multiple models; that in fact hadn’t been announced yet, but based on this report it sounds like that announcement is coming soon.

Microsoft, like many software companies, is in a tough spot when it comes to token usage. On one hand, of course it makes sense that customers pay for usage for a product that has meaningful marginal costs. On the other hand, an important aspect of software-as-a-service is that the pricing is predictable: you know that an employee is going to cost you $50/month on an E5 plan, and while that’s a decent amount of money, it’s something you can budget and plan for.

And, particularly in the case of Microsoft, you do get a lot for your money. Rewind just under a decade when Slack was the hot new product with one big barrier to entry: it’s hard to get large enterprises to pay for new software. Microsoft made that decision a lot more difficult by launching Teams and adding it to the bundle. For many companies, it didn’t matter that Teams was “worse”, particularly in terms of the user interface; what mattered was that it integrated with their existing products and, critically, was effectively free, because it was a part of the bundle they were already paying for.

That’s the real challenge with a usage-based pricing model: if an enterprise is paying for marginal usage, then they’re going to look a lot closer at the marginal value they are or are not getting. That means that Cowork (or Copilot) doesn’t win by default; they actually have to be at least competitive with the offerings from Anthropic and OpenAI. After all, if you’re paying regardless, you might as well make sure you’re getting what you’re paying for.

This is where I think DeepSeek will come in: while I suspect Cowork will be best with Claude, and Copilot with GPT, Microsoft is surely concerned about how much usage they can offer in their new E7 plan (which includes both for $99/employee/month) without destroying their own margins by virtue of giving margin to Anthropic and OpenAI. What would be nice would be to be able to offer a significantly higher level of usage — enough for most employees — as part of the bundle, but that may only be viable with an open source model, and DeepSeek is one of the most compelling options because of the combination of performance and serving efficiency.

And so, yet again, the biggest proponents and beneficiaries of Chinese companies offering a cheaper alternative will be American companies. This is a pattern that isn’t going away, even if Microsoft ultimately does not go down this route (but I think they will).
