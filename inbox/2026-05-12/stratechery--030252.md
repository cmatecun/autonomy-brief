SpaceX and Anthropic
--------------------

From Bloomberg ( https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-06/anthropic-inks-computing-deal-with-spacex-to-meet-ai-demand ) :

Anthropic PBC has signed an agreement with Elon Musk’s SpaceX to bolster computing resources and meet surging demand for its Claude artificial intelligence software. Anthropic plans to access more than 300 megawatts of computing capacity from a large SpaceX data center in Memphis known as Colossus 1, the companies said on Wednesday. Anthropic said the partnership would “substantially” increase its computing resources and enable it to raise usage limits for its AI products. Terms of the deal were not disclosed. Other data center developers have recently been able lock in tenants at terms of anywhere from $1.5 million to $2 million per megawatt per year. At that rate, the Anthropic deal would easily represent hundreds of millions of dollars worth of business for xAI annually.

The arrangement brings together two competitors in the race to develop more advanced AI systems. Anthropic has seen demand surge in recent months as more customers flock to use its tools to streamline the process of coding and other tasks. In addition to SpaceX, Anthropic has also tapped Alphabet Inc.’s Google for chips and cloud services. At a conference after the SpaceX deal was announced, Anthropic Chief Executive Officer Dario Amodei said his company is “working as quickly as possible” to secure more computing resources after experiencing “80x growth” in annualized revenue and usage in the first quarter of this year.

This was a deal that was shocking but not surprising. First off, on the Anthropic side, I wrote last month in Mythos, Muse, and the Opportunity Cost of Compute ( https://stratechery.com/2026/mythos-muse-and-the-opportunity-cost-of-compute/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDI2L215dGhvcy1tdXNlLWFuZC10aGUtb3Bwb3J0dW5pdHktY29zdC1vZi1jb21wdXRlLyJdfSwiZXhwIjoxNzgxMTcyMTcyLCJpYXQiOjE3Nzg1ODAxNzIsImlzcyI6Imh0dHBzOi8vYXBwLnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZS9vYXV0aCIsInNjb3BlIjoiZmVlZDpyZWFkIGFydGljbGU6cmVhZCBhc3NldDpyZWFkIGNhdGVnb3J5OnJlYWQgZW50aXRsZW1lbnRzIiwic3ViIjoiMDE5NTE2ZjEtYTM0Ni03NjE0LTllOTYtYjYzYmQxMWZlODU2IiwidXNlIjoiYWNjZXNzIn0.ngacoDvJXsML3iN1s2b8MgJ3HTNELv1-IbjGRtC6r1uOrSwR6qKwQOc1CWq-P2hbqRB5h4CuxrOx6gLh1DUu5gkDFeM2rAD62UrcRfpehQsfWmhV40NHVX5CglcKNJ5aYZ2hfjmz9VRdcUdfemlPY8kZMmD1Ixtkp2gfHUraKJDbcrrZ-tSjwVni2xrCNBsyl1ewAO66cUgS5kxTugUWeMOz_pLThBjDGsh1vWJ7eBTdP6lO5q2uuCiBr5081NAQt5WnVNFWCMe5OsvjwkA5vR8RjWhYRFtLNHw-Me5HFsrDCSogvP2KgEASQ0OfYUeRpHv3kJG5o_LNZlV1n9Q77Q ) that I was not concerned about Anthropic losing their position due to a lack of compute:

OpenAI is betting that this compute constraint — and the deals they have made to overcome it — will matter more than Anthropic’s current momentum with end users…I’m less certain that this will be dispositive. When it comes to AI, distribution and transaction costs are still free — the two preconditions for Aggregators — which means that the winners should be those with the most compelling products. Those products will win the most users, providing the money necessary to source the compute to serve them; consider Anthropic’s deal to secure a meaningful portion of TPU supply, which, given the capacity constraints at TSMC, is ultimately an example of taking supply from Google. I suspect that Anthropic can take more, including already built hyperscaler and neocloud capacity. Yes, that compute will be more expensive, but if demand is high enough the necessary cash flow will be there.

This deal is a perfect example of what really is basic economics. First, if demand exceeds supply, then prices should increase. At the same time, prices are elastic: if they are lower there is more demand, and if they are higher there is less demand. In this case, while there is broad demand for computing, Anthropic has arguably the most demand; furthermore, Anthropic has the most willingness to pay, not just because they are making meaningful revenue, but also because they have the capacity to raise money in the pursuit of winning in AI.

It follows, then, that Anthropic was always going to have the willingness and capacity to pay the most for compute, which is exactly why I wasn’t worried about them getting compute and didn’t see OpenAI’s compute as a sustainable point of differentiation (although OpenAI is almost certainly paying less per equivalent unit of compute than Anthropic is). Yes, that means someone else will have less — in this case xAI ( https://simonwillison.net/2026/May/7/xai-anthropic/ ) — but that is why price matters.

xAI’s Two Companies
-------------------

xAI, meanwhile — and for now I’m going to refer to the original xAI part of the business, not the part that includes X, or is a part of SpaceX — has always been two companies in one: one is the model-making company, and one is the infrastructure company. These two companies have very different priorities, as all hardware and software companies do. The software company wants to serve as many people as possible and run in as many places as possible, the better to leverage its R&D costs; the hardware company wants to maximize utilization to leverage its fixed costs, at the highest price possible.

The way to achieve that high price for hardware differs according to the market: if the hardware is a commodity, with plenty of supply, then the hardware needs to be differentiated in some way, usually by software; if the commodity is in short supply, however, then the hardware earns a premium by maximizing demand, i.e. being available to the highest bidder. AI hardware obviously falls into the latter bucket: it is in short supply, which means the optimal strategy for the hardware company is to make itself available to the widest possible customer base in order to find the highest willingness to pay.

As long as xAI was focused on its own models, however, that integrated strategy was limiting the monetization potential of xAI’s hardware. Note that this limitation in potential would have existed even if xAI were actually using all of Colossus 1 for inference, instead of the 11% utilization that has been reported: simply being used is different than maximizing revenue; accomplishing the latter means opening up the hardware to the market. That, in the end, is what Musk did with Colossus 1 — and, frankly, it’s what he should do with Colossus 2.

Elon Musk and SpaceXAI’s Future
-------------------------------

TSLA is not at the level it was during the heights of the bull market, but Tesla is a real company, with real cars, and real profits; last quarter the electric car company made more money than Toyota (thanks in part to a special charge for Toyota; Toyota’s operating profit was still greater). SpaceX is a real company, with real rockets that land on real rafts, and while the company is not yet profitable, there is certainly a viable path to making money; the company’s impact on both humanity’s long-term potential and the U.S.’s national security is already profound…

I suspect the distinction is that both companies are rooted in the physical world: physics has a wonderful grounding effect on the most fantastical of narratives. Digital services like Twitter, though, built as they are on infinitely malleable software, are ultimately about people and how they interact with each other. The paradox is that this makes narratives that much more alluring, even — especially! — if they are wrong.

I added a few weeks later on a Sharp Tech episode ( https://sharptech.fm/member/article/another-weekend-with-musk-more-chaos-fewer-options-and-an-object-lesson-in-the-importance-of-principles ) :

There’s a big difference between hardware and software, and Tesla and SpaceX are hardware companies. There’s a certain inherent constraint that comes from the physical world — physics is a constraint on what you can do. One of the great things about software and one of the terrible things about software is there are no constraints, like why is it so hard to ship something? Because at some point if you’re a product manager or you’re a CEO you have to say, “We’re shipping on this date”, because you don’t wait until it’s done, it’s never done, software is never ever done, that’s why you get crunch: you get down to the last minute and like, “You’re going to ship it no matter what”, because that’s the only way to get stuff out the door, but that also means you can do stuff on a whim — there’s no constraint on your whims. And I feel like you have someone come in who probably had all sorts of crazy ideas and want to do all sorts of fanciful things, and then physics comes back and hits you in the face as that’s not possible, “Can’t be done, XYZ, we have to produce these things at scale”, and that’s an ongoing constraint that’s independent of any one person or someone in management speaking up to Elon Musk. At the end of the day, physics is sort of an unforgiving mistress — it’s going to stop you from doing things that can’t be done.

That 2022 observation seems more pertinent than ever in light of xAI. All of the praise for Musk’s prowess came in terms of the speed and creativity with which he built Colossus 1 and 2, basically inventing large scale behind-the-meter power ( https://stratechery.com/2026/an-interview-with-jeremie-eliahou-ontiveros-and-ajey-pandey-about-building-power-for-ai/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDI2L2FuLWludGVydmlldy13aXRoLWplcmVtaWUtZWxpYWhvdS1vbnRpdmVyb3MtYW5kLWFqZXktcGFuZGV5LWFib3V0LWJ1aWxkaW5nLXBvd2VyLWZvci1haS8jYmVoaW5kLXRoZS1tZXRlciJdfSwiZXhwIjoxNzgxMTcyMTcyLCJpYXQiOjE3Nzg1ODAxNzIsImlzcyI6Imh0dHBzOi8vYXBwLnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZS9vYXV0aCIsInNjb3BlIjoiZmVlZDpyZWFkIGFydGljbGU6cmVhZCBhc3NldDpyZWFkIGNhdGVnb3J5OnJlYWQgZW50aXRsZW1lbnRzIiwic3ViIjoiMDE5NTE2ZjEtYTM0Ni03NjE0LTllOTYtYjYzYmQxMWZlODU2IiwidXNlIjoiYWNjZXNzIn0.OjmAdPZo2UBNR4w6SuG4UPDZINgz1Alq0I8bxuPJeMTlu6_soMChcuaIcdCVpmzbm4bzGGjMaQIc_6ZMOiGvnNSefMPEtVWowuZwLB29z1oM02e5TDt2EZ8_RrjLEcxMQeguXQz81l0DlzGr0ZeIzYes-VAEdcfuBZE6vCHX0sh5UOuAdvDjncqycdY4i1IBywICwGzU7fSPJFQFZuzsrGon2MtaLJ3wckMCUCdBEoU7Gx3aziuNbrYpFH3e89Tm3gFmpxFRZclmhvhMluv2QPYqypsjNZoGHy7yLeQf88CHAB1Kxm0n_4cVqKfMfshvTmzvBWAv13AkH9zKHMN-tw#behind-the-meter ) along the way; the software has been another story, and the nadir of that story — Grok being used to undress females on Twitter — was about as good of an example you can get of what can go wrong when the limitless nature of software meets a desperate attempt to increase usage and leverage a bunch of idle GPUs.

I do think the biggest driver of this deal was what I wrote above: Anthropic offered a price that was simply too good to turn down, particularly as SpaceX gears up for an IPO. Suddenly Colossus 1 is a revenue generator, not a depreciating underutilized asset. Fast forward, however, and if Musk ever does realize his vision of space-based data centers, it is going to be even more critical that those assets be maximally utilized. Remember, the entire argument for them existing is the assumption that earth-based data centers will be power and/or regulatory constrained, which is to say that demand will exceed supply; in that world selling to the highest bidder is the optimal strategy — SpaceX might as well commit to it now!

Of course there is the matter of Cursor, a deal which I wrote made sense in theory ( https://stratechery.com/2026/john-ternus-and-apples-hardware-defined-future-spacexai-and-cursor/?access_token=eyJhbGciOiJSUzI1NiIsImtpZCI6InN0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LnBhc3Nwb3J0Lm9ubGluZSIsInR5cCI6IkpXVCJ9.eyJhdWQiOiJzdHJhdGVjaGVyeS5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUiLCJhenAiOiJIS0xjUzREd1Nod1AyWURLYmZQV00xIiwiZW50Ijp7InVyaSI6WyJodHRwczovL3N0cmF0ZWNoZXJ5LmNvbS8yMDI2L2pvaG4tdGVybnVzLWFuZC1hcHBsZXMtaGFyZHdhcmUtZGVmaW5lZC1mdXR1cmUtc3BhY2V4YWktYW5kLWN1cnNvci8iXX0sImV4cCI6MTc4MTE3MjE3MiwiaWF0IjoxNzc4NTgwMTcyLCJpc3MiOiJodHRwczovL2FwcC5wYXNzcG9ydC5vbmxpbmUvb2F1dGgiLCJzY29wZSI6ImZlZWQ6cmVhZCBhcnRpY2xlOnJlYWQgYXNzZXQ6cmVhZCBjYXRlZ29yeTpyZWFkIGVudGl0bGVtZW50cyIsInN1YiI6IjAxOTUxNmYxLWEzNDYtNzYxNC05ZTk2LWI2M2JkMTFmZTg1NiIsInVzZSI6ImFjY2VzcyJ9.EY-Tf2q-1Pe_CQkm9WMxasQjBowT0YsHpZvyGNB6DMf0ol7ZMcdaRIx8qM2eQDco7g3zwl4p48vOqug9-B2uNju4h63DKb5Z1ilqCkJO4VSevLXIcJ3JpuCnkCDkojsWy1mby5-aGBqJcV_pNwyWDhTzX5wn1DKrD-VsnuiakSlso_mOpvGGmVUfSYN4GTBRCpSebkl7zzVzUIdN-ndIx3gxOHrqQhZM0pdOhEQLIx3d6vpi_GW1CfA8toQvWEDL3up6LzEQt57wTyc2UxMSpc32q3_1QOaJQuYuk_si-FuHzOmrDNUL1RIa4K_BtkS77W-n8DZTpnimMQBfy_sCpA ). It’s not dead — xAI still has Colossus 2 to train Cursor’s new models — but I think the model fight is one that xAI is not well-suited towards, temperamentally and economically. Musk should stick to the physical world, while Cursor makes a lot more sense for a Microsoft or Amazon. Of course Musk isn’t one to give up easily, but he is, in the end, a businessman — which is exactly why this deal was a shock but not a surprise.
