## Key Developments

### Market Moves

**Waymo launches Ojai to public riders.** Waymo began offering rides in its Zeekr-built Ojai minivan across Los Angeles, San Francisco, and Phoenix, with San Diego, Las Vegas, and Denver launches coming this summer. The vehicle is Waymo's first purpose-built for autonomy rather than retrofitted. Rides are currently free while Waymo awaits California Public Utilities Commission approval to charge fares, expected by June 27. Waymo says it's scaling toward capacity for tens of thousands of units annually (*CNBC, AV Market Strategist*).

**Revel and Voltera merge charging networks.** The combined company will operate under the Voltera brand and serve more than 1,000 charging stalls across 11 major US markets. Revel CEO Frank Reig will lead the merged entity, which aims to serve autonomous and ride-hail fleets while exploring adjacent opportunities including battery storage and energy management (*Driverless Digest*).

**Texas AV database reveals fleet compositions.** A new Texas database shows Waymo operates 577 registered autonomous vehicles while Tesla's unsupervised fleet has shrunk to just 20 vehicles. The data comes as Texas Senate Bill 2807 requires authorization for commercial L4/L5 operations, which took effect May 28 (*AV Market Strategist, r/SelfDrivingCars*).

**BYD unveils 4nm smart driving chip.** The Chinese automaker announced a new 4nm chip for its autonomous driving systems and is offering smart driving with full liability coverage at one-fifth the cost of Tesla's FSD. BYD's "God's Eye B" package costs 12,000 yuan ($1,800) one-time versus Tesla's 64,000 yuan ($9,400) in China (*r/SelfDrivingCars*).

### Technology & Product

**Rocsys M1 solves robotaxi charging bottleneck.** Dutch company Rocsys launched the M1, the world's first multi-bay hands-free charging system for robotaxi fleets. A robotic arm moves between bays to plug in vehicles automatically, eliminating the manual charging step that currently breaks autonomous operations at scale. Rocsys CEO Crijn Bouman explains this is critical infrastructure as "a self-driving car cannot plug itself in" and manual charging undermines depot economics (*AV Market Strategist*).

**Mobileye's Meteor AI hunts systematic edge cases.** Mobileye unveiled Meteor, a multi-agent AI system that analyzes millions of hours of driving data to identify reproducible failures rather than chasing unrepeatable "black swan" events. The system uses vision-language model embeddings to automatically generate hypotheses, search for similar scenarios, and surface high-value training examples (*r/SelfDrivingCars*).

**Xpeng defends vision-only strategy.** The Chinese automaker stated that LiDAR is "no longer necessary for cars," defending its pure vision approach as sensor costs decline and AI improves (*r/SelfDrivingCars*).

### Regulatory & Policy

**Waymo begins Virginia mapping amid blue-state resistance.** Waymo started mapping Arlington and Alexandria with human drivers, calling it "an important preparatory step" though stressing no current commercial plans. The move comes as Virginia weighs AV legislation, but Maryland killed its AV bill last month and DC's measure remains stalled in city council—suggesting regional Democratic resistance to robotaxis is spreading (*AV Market Strategist*).

**Mercedes targets Germany urban rollout.** Mercedes announced plans for year-end rollout of urban assisted driving in Germany, racing to catch rivals in its home market (*r/SelfDrivingCars*).

**Bliq receives Europe's first driverless approval.** Estonian startup Bliq.ai received authorization to operate on public roads without a driver behind the wheel, marking the first such approval in an EU member state. The company operates what it believes is Europe's largest fully driverless fleet (*Driverless Digest*).

### Safety & Operations

**Reuters investigation questions Tesla safety claims.** A Reuters report found that Tesla's AI trainers don't trust the company's self-driving technology or its safety statistics, raising concerns about the methodology behind Tesla's reported safety numbers (*r/SelfDrivingCars*).

**87-year-old dies in Tesla Autopilot crash.** A Tesla on Autopilot mode crashed into a pond, killing the 87-year-old driver in the latest fatal incident involving Tesla's driver assistance systems (*r/SelfDrivingCars*).

**Blind riders praise Waymo independence.** The New York Times profiled blind Waymo users who describe the service as transformative, offering unprecedented independence and the "joy of riding alone" without depending on drivers or explaining their disability (*r/SelfDrivingCars*).

### Capital & Financials

**Pony.ai reports 395% robotaxi revenue growth.** In Q1 2026 earnings, Pony.ai showed massive year-over-year robotaxi revenue growth, though specific figures weren't disclosed. The company is scaling operations across China and has begun removing front-row safety drivers in select markets (*AV Market Strategist*).

**Starship surpasses 10 million deliveries.** The autonomous sidewalk delivery robot company crossed 10 million completed deliveries, demonstrating scaled operational viability for last-mile robotics (*Road to Autonomy*).

## Strategic Takeaways

The Ojai launch crystallizes a tension at the heart of Waymo's scaling strategy: Chinese manufacturing capacity is enabling rapid fleet growth, but political exposure is rising. Waymo's bet that stripping Chinese telematics and finishing vehicles in Arizona will satisfy both regulators and lawmakers looks increasingly fragile as even preparatory steps like Virginia mapping trigger resistance. The company is racing to establish facts on the ground—thousands of vehicles operating commercially—before political headwinds become regulatory barriers (*AV Market Strategist*).

Meanwhile, the infrastructure layer is emerging as the real constraint on robotaxi economics. Rocsys's argument is simple and compelling: autonomy doesn't end when the car pulls into the depot. Without hands-free charging, fleets need humans for every vehicle cycle, which destroys the unit economics that justify the technology. The M1 represents a recognition that AV companies can't scale service areas or vehicle counts without simultaneously scaling depot automation. This is the unglamorous infrastructure that determines whether robotaxis work at city scale (*AV Market Strategist*).

Eduardo Rojas's warning about the Waymo-Uber relationship deserves attention from anyone watching autonomous mobility's commercialization. If Waymo increasingly treats its first-party app as the primary distribution channel and views TNC partnerships as temporary or secondary, it risks repeating taxi industry mistakes—building a great product but struggling with customer acquisition and utilization. Uber's core value isn't just demand aggregation; it's the network effect that comes from riders checking one app for all mobility options. Waymo sidelining that partnership to avoid sharing margin could be strategically shortsighted if it means slower scaling and lower fleet utilization (*Driverless Digest*).
